May be methodological, metaphysical, textual, or
epistemological. The unified authorial subject is
a product of the Enlightenment, and our modern
legacy. But as the academy addresses itself toissues of gender, diversity, and postmodernity, it
becomes difficult to assume the value and meaning of unity, either as an aesthetic for writing or
an ethic for cooperatively living. Perhaps for rhetoricians, the achievement of unity has never been
taken for granted. Kenneth Burke explains that
words and their histories motivate people to act
in ways that both distinguish themselves from one
another and seek commonality. Bakhtin expresses
an analogous tension in his concepts of the centrifugal, or univocal, and centripetal, or polyvocal,
tendencies of texts. And the sophistic tradition has
always reconciled truth with circumstance or
kairos, suggesting a contingent unity.
Philosophically, the origins of unity can be
traced to Pythagoras (number one as constituent
of all things), Plato (one asinterpenetration of
beauty, truth, and good), Aristotle (correlation of
unity and being), Plotinus (God as One), Aquinas
("transcendental unity"), and Kant ("transcendental unity of apperception"). Non-Western traditions (such as Native American and Hindu)
appear to express similar notions of cosmological
unity. But in a modernist context, unity has served
utilitarian sociopolitical purposes, particularly as
it has been manifested in written communication.
To function as a unified, productive body ofconsumers, the eighteenth-century emergent middle
class needed to read and write. The shift from a
simple market to an industrialized economy increased the demand for information, and the
printing press accelerated its dissemination. Likewise, the masses required and demanded education. As a result, writing was routinized and standardized, which in turn made proper taste in
speech and writing an object of consumption.
Proper literature, for example, was Aristotelian,
exemplifying a unified plot, the tragedy being the
highest expression of this unity, replete with rising action, climax, and denouement or resolution.
The letter and, in turn, the essay, also produced
and modeled unity, Hugh Blair's (1718–1800)
Man of Letters the enviable, unified, learned authorial subject. A learned man (gender significant)
was a rational, observant man, a Spectator, to
apply the title of one of the popular coffeehouse
newspapers of the time, a single, unified voice.
Perhaps the strongest academic influences on
unity were two mid-nineteenth-century rhetoricians: Alexander Bain, who, deriving from faculty
psychology, prescribed a paragraph unified in
purpose and shape, and Henry Noble Day, for
whom discourse was a rational procedure that
should be singular in its aim and its subject in
order to best represent a natural combination of
the two. The two-plus-centuries-old five-paragraph theme and current-traditional rhetoric's
emphasis on method reflect further the expedience
and utility of unity.
Although the Romantics celebrated a personal genius transcending didacticism, they nevertheless subscribed to the ideal of unity. The text
lives and spontaneously arises whole from nature;
as Coleridge claimed (borrowing from Bacon), it
expresses a natural and perhaps divine quality
through the harmony of its parts and can be constructed through an equally divine, unified
method. Turning away from expressionistic aesthetics, New Criticism emphasized the structural,
linguistic coherence of the text, but still confronted
the problem of a unity that excludes, in this case
the reader.
In an increasingly complex world, the question remains as to how unity can function ethically without collapsing difference and aesthetically without misrepresenting the subject.
Andy Crockett
University of Arizona

320. Visual Rhetoric
321. Narrowly, the study of the design of text on
322. pages; more generally, the study of all visual
323. signs, including the semiotics of the graphic arts,
324. television, and other media. Traditionally, rhetorical study has defined within delivery, one of
325. rhetoric's five canons, the visual dynamics of
326. speech. The recognition that a speech is, in part,
327. a visual event and should be accompanied by
328. appropriate gestures has been recognized since
329. classical times. During the late eighteenth century, an emphasis on delivery led to elaborate
330. elocutionary manuals showing, among other
331. things, all possible hand movements, postures,
332. and facial expressions that might accompany and
333. embellish an apt delivery. The modern inheritors
334. of this tradition treat the visual rhetoric of face-
335. to-face verbal interaction under such headings as
336. kinesics (or body language) and proxemics (or
337. the study of how speaker/interlocutor distance,
338. posture, and touch affect interpersonal communication). The lesson of such study is that much
339. of the meaning in an interpersonal exchange is
340. tied up not in the words exchanged but in the
341. physical posturings, in observable behaviors that
342. are seen and can be interpreted as meaningful
343. aspects of the encounter.
344. The emerging field of visual rhetoric, however, is concerned more with written text than
345. spoken. A written text must be visually apprehended, and the spread of ink on paper carries
346. meaning. At the level of the whole text, the visual gestalt communicates to a potential reader
347. whether the text is inviting or forbidding. Written texts are visually examined for meanings:
348. for cues to genre, organization, emphasis, or
349. beginnings and endings. Writing forces writers
350. to indicate divisions and hierarchies, to show
351. what goes with what, to create chunks of text
352. that together constitute a whole. In contrast to
353. the flow of speech, the graphic quality of writing heightens boundaries and discontinuities.
354. The visual design of the text filters the information on the page. Because writing indicates hierarchy, the reader is cued as to what is
355. necessary to read, what is important to read,
356. and what can be skimmed or skipped. A visual
357. format, such as that which informs a research
358. article, allows a reader to find general information quickly in the abstract, introduction, or
359. conclusion sections. Readers use the visual design to find their place, locate specific information, or skip whole sections that they do not feel
360. the need to read. A newspaper is another good
361. example of a visually informative text, one that
362. provides multiple cues to readers about the status of various sorts of information. Headlines,
363. column rules, captions, font size variation, and
364. conventions of body versus advertising copy all
365. work together to provide a highly informative
366. visual design. Because of the design, the reader
367. can use highly efficient processing strategies,
368. scanning large blocks of text and using highly
369. evolved strategies to process large amounts of
370. information in very little time.
371. Printed text offers an impressive array of
372. visual features that have no exact counterparts
373. in speech: punctuation, symbols such as asterisks or bullets, numerals, indentation, margins,
374. white space. Type itself, with rich variation recently made available to writers through desktop publishing tools, becomes a powerful element in the rhetoric of the text, with meaningful
375. variation expressed in type fonts (bookish
376. Times Roman versus trendyAvant Garde or
377. anachronistic Courier), type sizes (the larger the
378. point size the more important the information),
379. and type styles (with italics, small caps, shadowed, double underlined, and other variations
380. all carrying their own meanings, whether playful or emphatic).
381. When text becomes visually informative by
382. exploiting its graphic potential, the rhetorical
383. meanings of that text are then shared between
384. the logical/semantic/interpersonal meanings
385. that are expressed verbally and those supporting or complementary meanings that are expressed visually. The visual features of the text
386. work together with verbal features to structure
387. meaning. Writers can indicate and readers can
388. infer what sort of text it is in a holistic way simply from the text's appearance. The visual features guide reading, suggesting what is most
389. important and what is less so; what should be
390. read first, last, or not at all;what sections are
391. subordinate to other sections; and so on.
392. The visual rhetoric of texts also subsumes
393. those elements collectively referred to as "the
394. visuals": pictures and illustrations, line drawings and photos, charts and graphs. Each of
395. these visual elements makes meaning by being
396. interwoven with the written text itself, with
397. words referring to the visuals and providing
398. reference and naming, elaboration, direction of
399. attention, or emphasis. The visuals themselves
400. have their own rhetoric of display, and well-
401. developed principles apply to designing visuals
402. that are efficient, informative, emphatic, honest, relevant, and sufficient. Studies of perception, for example, show that people are good at
403. comparing the height of columns across two bar
404. charts but not so good at comparing the relative sizes of two shaded areas across two pie
405. charts. Knowing such principles allows writers
406. to choose among line graphs, bar charts, or pie
407. charts for certain sorts of data display. The
408. rhetoric of visuals also provides principles that
409. lead to honest representation: using scalesappropriate to the data, being careful not to combine one- or two-dimensional data with three-
410. dimensional (as in faulty comparisons of height
411. to volume), using zero baselines or clearly indicating otherwise, and presenting data in context. These are only a few examples of rhetorical generalizations that derive from the study of
412. visuals.
413. Visual rhetoric is hugely influenced by
414. desktop publishing. A personal computer, a laser printer, and some software give a writer full
415. control of the appearance of the text. In addition to granting control over typography, desktop publishing makes text into manipulable
416. objects, moveable pieces of language that can be
417. combined with graphic objects. Text as object
418. can be stretched, fit to curves, flowed around
419. objects or within boundaries, overwritten,
420. screened (printed at less than 100 percent density), given color, or otherwise manipulated and
421. composed. A whole terminology has sprung up
422. around desktop publishing, with terms and
423. concepts borrowed from graphic design: leading, kerning, alignment, rule, body type, style,
424. descender, gutter, grid, and separation. All indicate control: control granted to the writer
425. (rather than the printer) of the shape of the text.
426. It is difficult to overestimate the importance
427. of desktop publishing to visual rhetoric. The
428. technology redefines the role of writer into
429. writer/designer. The world is now inundated
430. with publications that attempt to combine words
431. and images into strong and interesting designs.
432. The technology reinvigorates the word composition, making writing less a matter of words and
433. sentences and more a matter of effectively integrating verbal and visual elements into a pleasing and effective whole. Writers who wish to
434. become text designers must develop aesthetic
435. insight and an affinity for the art of an informed
436. practice with its basis in graphic design. In some
437. ways, desktop publishing suggests a return to the
438. earlier scribal tradition of illuminating manuscripts. The scribe is both writer and artist.
439. Another key influence on visual rhetoric is
440. the transition of media from print to screen.
441. Screens as a medium of composition differ dramatically from paper. They afford easy visual/
442. verbal integration and the luxury of open space.
443. They provide easy access to color and to special
444. textual effects: inverse lettering, animation, and
445. all the manipulation of text that is characteristic of desktop publishing, only in a more dynamic medium.
446. Principles of visual rhetoric for screen design are only beginning to emerge. We know
447. readers have little tolerance for extended reading on screen, that they frequently become lost
448. in large textbases, and that they need to develop
449. new strategies for navigation. Books—whether
450. computer manuals or popular magazines—cannot simply be put into electronic files and be
451. useful. Information must be designed for the
452. screen to be effective. Readers of on-screen text
453. need to know how to open the text and adjust
454. its display. They need to be constantly reminded
455. of where they are and where they have been.
456. They need to know how to make the text work,
457. how to find what they need and remember it.
458. They need to know how to customize the text
459. and what to do if they make unintentional
460. changes. New roles and responsibilities are
461. emerging for writers and readers.
462. In its more abstract manifestations, the
463. boundaries of visual rhetoric tend to blur with
464. those of media criticism, aesthetic theory, advertising, physiology of vision, and semiotics. Suffice it to say that like many areas of rhetoric, it
465. is extremely difficult to draw tight boundaries
466. on what constitutes visual rhetoric versus other
467. related areas of inquiry. Rhetoric is fundamentally cross-disciplinary, and visual rhetoric is a
468. prime case in point. To ask how language functions as a visual experience is to open wide the
469. doors to many other disciplines.
