McKeon, Richard P. (1900–1985)
Distinguished American philosopher who studied the theoretic presuppositions behind rhetorical doctrines, traced the changing meanings and
applications of rhetorical themes throughout
Western intellectual history, and speculated on
innovative extensions of rhetoric in philosophy,
natural science, practical affairs, and the arts,
forming a philosophy of culture in which rhetoric, expanded beyond a simple verbal skill to an
intellectual art of invention and inquiry, provides the framework for pluralism and a new
organization of learning.
Richard McKeon was born in Union Hill,
New Jersey, on April 26, 1900. He studied at
Columbia University in preprofessional programs of law and engineering before the First
World War interrupted his education. Afterward,
his interests shifted to humanistic studies, and he
read literature, philosophy, history, and the classics. His Master's thesis in philosophy was on
Tolstoi, Croce, and Santayana, and his Ph.D.
dissertation, still cited as an important study of
seventeenth-century philosophy, was on the philosophy of Spinoza. While at Columbia he studied with Frederick J.E. Woodbridge and John
Dewey. In 1922 he went to Paris to study with
Leon Brunschvicq, Leon Robin, andEtienne
Gilson, leading philosophers and scholars of the
day, and received a degree from the University of
Paris and l'Ecole des Hautes Etudes. Like Dewey,
whose trip to the Far East marked an intellectual
turning point, McKeon was stimulated by his
experiences in Europe to an interest in communication across cultures and ages that remained
a central theme throughout his life. From 1925
to 1935, McKeon taught at Columbia University,
where he established medieval philosophy as a
serious branch of philosophic studies. In 1935,
at the invitation of Robert Maynard Hutchins,
he went to the University of Chicago to become
dean of the humanities. He was one of the primary architects of the general education program
of the "Hutchins College" at Chicago andremained a powerful voice for interdisciplinary
studies and the liberal arts in higher education in
the United States and abroad.
In the period following the Second World
War, McKeon served as an American representative to UNESCO and participated in the
Committee on the Theoretical Bases of Human
Rights, which helped prepare for the adoption of
the Universal Declaration of Human Rights by
the United Nations. He was a founding member
of the so-called "Chicago School" of literary
criticism, reputed to be "neo-Aristotelian" in
methodology but, in fact, strongly committed to
critical pluralism and an expanded form of rhetorical inquiry that provoked angry responses
from the formalist New Critics. Among many
other national and international distinctions in
a long career in philosophy, he was awarded the
highest honor of the American Philosophical Association when he was invited to give the Paul
Carus Lectures in New York in 1965.
McKeon was commonly—and mistakenly—
described as an "Aristotelian" because of extensive commentaries on Aristotle, important editions of the works of Aristotle, and many essays
on ancient Greek philosophy. Although influenced
by reading Aristotle's works, McKeon accurately
characterized himself as a philosophical pluralist
and an American philosopher in the tradition of
the pragmatists. His work opened a new direction
in American pragmatic philosophy that subtly
resonates with C.S. Peirce and John Dewey yet
projects the new role of rhetoric in addressing
philosophic and practical problems. Some observers argue that McKeon explored the themes of
deconstructionism and other late-twentieth-century philosophies decades before their popular
emergence but that he avoided relativism and
solipsism through the principles of objective inquiry and the possibilities of productive intellectual debate. His active involvement in promoting
universal human rights and World Federalism
indicates an optimism that stands in sharp contrast to the pessimism over establishing shared
understanding that is found in some forms of literary and philosophic deconstructionism. He remained at Chicago until his retirement in 1974
and continued to lecture and publish until his
death in 1985.
McKeon was the first major philosopher to
recognize the central importance of rhetoric in
the philosophic and cultural revolutions that
began in the early decades of the twentieth century. He argued that debate in Western culture
shifts periodically through a sequence of master
topics or commonplaces that he identified as
"things," "thoughts," "words," and "actions."
The shifting debate progresses from questions
of being and metaphysics, to questions of epistemology and forms of thought, to questions of
language and the circumstances and consequences of action. In periods of metaphysics and
epistemology, the formal discipline of rhetoric
often plays a secondary role among the cultural
and intellectual arts and is frequently reduced to
a mere verbal art, while the devices and themes
of rhetoric move informally throughout the arts
and sciences. But in a period when debate in all
fields and disciplines finds its beginning point in
questions of expression, experience, and communication, rhetoric becomes a broadly organizing
"architectonic" discipline that provides the issues
and instrumentalities for innovation and revolution, leading to new fields of investigation, new
disciplines to guide inquiry, and new occupations
for men and women in all areas of life.
The rise of rhetoric in the twentieth century
began among scientists, artists, and philosophers
who revolted against earlier dogma "by seeking
meaningful questions in the concrete and real; by
cultivating experience and phenomena, existence
and nature; by appealing to science and history,
common sense and language." However, the presence of rhetorical themes and issues embedded in
the new discourse did not ensure the rapid emergence of a new art of rhetoric. Indeed, the progress
of twentieth-century revolutions has been frequently blunted and deflected into ideological
conflicts and semantic disputes among philosophies by the lack of a new art of rhetoric adequate
to the fundamental task undertaken in these revolutions: the formation of a world culture that is
both universal in extension and individual and
plural in expression. Such an art emerged in the
Roman Republic oriented toward practical affairs
and law. It emerged in the Renaissance oriented
toward beaux arts and belles-lettres. But in the
twentieth century, McKeon argued, the new architectonic art of rhetoric would find its orientation,
not in the practical or the poetic, but in science and
the theoretic. The new rhetoric would be an "art
of science" or a "science of art": an intellectual
technology that combines theory with practice for
new productive purposes.
McKeon's writings—more than 160 articles
and eleven books—are a record of his continuing
exploration of the dimensions of the new architectonic art of rhetoric directed toward the intellectual and practical problems of the twentieth
century. Some of these writings are explicit treatments of rhetoric that shifted the direction of
contemporary scholarly inquiry. For example,
the seminal "Rhetoric in the Middle Ages"
(1942) opened the way for the serious study of
medieval and Renaissance rhetoric that forcefully
emerged in the second half of the twentieth century. Rhetoric, he argued, is commonly treated
as an art with a fixed subject matter, usuallya
simple verbal discipline. Based on this conception, the resulting histories of rhetoric give only
fragmentary accounts and monotonous enumerations of doctrines that fail to explore the influence and development of rhetoric—the spread of
its devices and themes—in subject matters far
removed from those ordinarily ascribed to it. As
a consequence, the history of rhetoric in the
Middle Ages appeared brief, equivocal, and unimportant. In contrast, McKeon proposed a
history of rhetoric that reflects on the changing
conceptions of the subject matter, nature, and
purpose of the art, conceptions that rhetoricians
employ to distinguish and advance their doctrines in novel applications. When applied to the
medieval period, this approach revealed a startling pattern of innovation in the tradition of
rhetoricians themselves, in the tradition of philosophers and theologians, and in the tradition
of logic, demonstrating not the demise of rhetoric but its renaissance in new forms.
"Rhetoric in the Middle Ages" influenced the
course of rhetorical scholarship in the twentieth
century, but it also implied the need for a new
approach by which to understand the contemporary development of rhetoric beyond the confines
of a narrow verbal discipline or a fixed subject
matter. In many essays, McKeon explicitly sought
to expand the understanding of rhetoric as an
intellectual art, showing how the instrumentalities
of rhetoric—particularly the common and proper
places associated with invention—could be reconstituted to address contemporary problems of culture. Examples include "The Methods of Rhetoric and Philosophy: Invention and Judgment,"
"Creativity and the Commonplace," "Arts of Invention and Arts of Memory: Creation and Criticism," "Symbols, Myths, and Arguments," and
the powerful, provocative essays "Discourse,
Demonstration, Verification, and Justification,"
"Philosophy of Communications and the Arts,"
and "The Uses of Rhetoric in a Technological Age:
Architectonic Productive Arts." In many other
essays—the majority of his writings—McKeon
implicitly used rhetoric as an intellectual art with
which to explore contrasting perspectives on central themes of philosophy and culture, always with
a view toward issues in contemporary scholarship,
philosophy, science, practical affairs, or the arts.
The range of these essays and the scope of treatment of each theme never fail to reveal unexpected
connections and intellectual possibilities that
remain hidden in narrower or more traditional
studies. His goal was a classic expression of rhetoric: to discover new ideas and create new methods that would sustain an ongoing revolution in
thought and action to better realize the potential
of the human community.
Two rhetorical devices provided the foundation of McKeon's approach to communication: amplification and schematization. Amplification is the device for extending the scope
of rhetoric from words to actions, thoughts,
and things. He often spoke of the principle of
indifference, by which words convert to actions, express thoughts, and refer to things.
"Objectivity is the inclusive principle of indifference by which it is recognized that being is
grasped only in what we think, and say, and do
about it" (McKeon, "Circumstances and Functions" 111). Schematization was the primary
device for identifying commonplaces and generating inventions. Schemata are uninterpreted
but suggestive relations of terms that might
open up possibilities for subsequent interpretation and development in discourse.
Although McKeon traced the many uses of
schemata in other philosophies in order to identify topics or commonplaces of inquiry, his own
primary schematism was a "semantic matrix,"
a paradigm for the discovery of meanings in all
forms of discourse. He described and illustrated
this matrix in "Philosophic Semantics and Philosophic Inquiry" (242–56). The basis of the semantic matrix was a set of four terms which,
placed in schematic relationships, formed the
commonplaces of discourse: things, thoughts,
words, and actions. Variations of these terms
(for example, cognates of the verb to know:
knowledge, knower, known, and knowable)
proved to be an ongoing source of discovery for
McKeon. He sometimes identified the fourissues of the semantic matrix as selection, interpretation, method, and principle, indicating the
fundamental issues of all discourse and communication. In essence, the semantic matrix was a
highly innovative and unique development of
the four scientific questions of Aristotle and the
four master topics—the constitutions or questions—of Roman rhetoric.
Using amplification and schematization,
McKeon explored four subject matters in a new
organization of learning that cuts across traditional disciplinary divisions to reveal interdisciplinary connections. He examined Topics in all
areas of thought and action, seeking the sources
of creativity and innovation. He investigated
Hypotheses in the concrete semantic formulation
of inventions that emerge from Topics, seeking
the content of doctrines and philosophies. He
traced Themes, or the patterns of art that individuals employ to develop their inventions in
argument and narration, seeking the lines of
thought that open up new connections for investigation and action. He explored Theses, or the
fundamental propositions that anchor discourse
in what is and what is real, as determined by
philosophic systems, the consensus of communities, and the nature of things. Commenting on
the new study of discourse that he proposed,
McKeon wrote, "Speculation concerning discourse must avoid the fixities of categories, doctrines, methods, and assumptions which discourse assumes in any one form of philosophy or
inquiry, if it is to include all the forms which discourse takes in philosophy and in inquiry, action,
and production. This is possible because the variety of categories or elements is approached in
discourse by way of common topics or 'commonplaces'; the variety of facts or statements of
what is the case by way of common hypotheses;
the variety of arts or methods of treating problems by way of common theses; and the variety
of assumptions or principles by way of common
theses" (McKeon 1950: 45).
The scope and direction of McKeon's work
is further revealed in a reshaping of the rhetorical arts. He extended the classic division of the
arts of discourse—rhetoric, grammar, logic, and
dialectic—into a new rhetorical and philosophic
framework:
The new rhetoric may be broadened from
persuasion to include all elements of existence and to use commonplaces or topics for
discovery of the unknown. The new grammar may be broadened from the composition of statements to include all facts of
experience and to use hypotheses of semantics for recovery of the known. The new
logic may be broadened from inquiry and
proof to include all discursive sequences and
all sequential series stated in discourse and
to use the themes of the arts for presentation of connections. The new dialectic may
be broadened from systems of thought and
being to include all ordering principles, elements, and causes and to use the theses of
inquiry to unify and transform possible
world orders and human orders. The new
philosophy can not be a monolithic inclusive shared ideology, but it may oppose the
dogmatisms of partial and divisive universalisms to become a philosophy of communications and the arts. (McKeon, "Circumstances and Functions" 119–20).
Richard McKeon was one of the foremost
theoreticians of rhetoric in the twentieth century,
yet he deliberately resisted systematization of his
views on rhetoric or other matters in a body of
doctrines or dogma. Instead, he cultivated a new
organization of subject matters suited to a dynamic
philosophy of culture and a comprehensive set of
intellectual arts suited to the exploration of those
subject matters. This was so complete a departure
from the typical practice of philosophy in the early
and middle twentieth century that few contemporaries understood the full significance of his project.
For this reason, McKeon remained a problem and
an enigma to those individuals who expected a
conventional expression of philosophic doctrine
and found, in his persistent willingness to consider
diverse views on all matters, a tragic waste of a
brilliant mind. But many others continue to find in
McKeon's work a new path to the organization of
learning grounded in rhetoric and suited to the new
circumstances of thought and action. As the twentieth century progresses in an ongoing struggle
between relativism and anti-intellectual dogmatism—a struggle that McKeon believed to be the
unfortunate center of many philosophic and practical problems in this period—the outlines of his
philosophy of culture progressively emerge with
more striking character and relevance.
Richard Buchanan
Carnegie Mellon University

122. From Antiquity to the Middle Ages
123. During Greek and Roman antiquity, education
124. prepared citizens for public life, and speech was
125. considered the basis of all social order. Message
126. transmission remained essentially oral, although written documents occasionally served
127. as supplements. Therefore, Greek and Roman
128. schools prepared students to be equally adept
129. at both speaking and writing. In the fifth century B.C.E., the Platonic-Socratic ideal, not unlike the sophistic ideal of Isocrates, promoted
130. rhetoric as a way to unite philosophical wisdom
131. with verbal skill to attain the height of education: a public demonstration of oratory.
132. After the fall of the Roman Empire in the
133. fifth and sixth centuries, however, large-scale
134. educational decline resulted in a society in which
135. few were capable of reading or writing. Church
136. institutions and a feudal system of government
137. supplanted the classical function of statecraft and
138. legal procedure, rendering public speaking useless on social or political issues. The Areopagus
139. and the Forum were silent spaces. Oratory became the domain of church authorities trained in
140. logic and theology, and writing was left to the
141. few, usually clerics, who were skilled in discourse. Growing complexity in the worlds of
142. both church and state, however, demanded effective communication. Thus, the study of rhetoric
143. fragmented into three new forms: letter-writing
144. (ars dictaminis), preaching (ars praedicandi), and
145. poetics (ars poetriae). Before discussing these
146. forms, it is important to examine briefly the traditions from which they were derived.
147. Four Classical Traditions
148. According to James J. Murphy, four distinct classical traditions are represented by the rhetorical
149. texts of antiquity, which were widely copied and
150. circulated throughout the Middle Ages. Murphy
151. designates these as the Aristotelian, Ciceronian,
152. grammatical, and sophistic traditions.
153. The Aristotelian tradition, a more philosophical, logic-based approach to discourse than
154. the others, was represented by Aristotle's
155. Rhetorica (c. 350 B.C.E.), the oldest extant textbook on the subject, which reached the West in
156. the thirteenth century via the translations of
157. Arabic commentators. Given the "scholastic" environment and surge of interest in Aristotle during that century, one would assume that his theory of discourse would dominate. Yet Boethius
158. assumed that Cicero's rhetoric should be the
159. standard, and medieval encyclopedists ignored
160. Aristotle's rhetorical theory. His Poetics remained virtually unknown until the fifteenth
161. century, and his Topica and De Sophisticis Elen-
162. chis influenced the studies of ethics and political
163. science in the universities of the twelfth through
164. fifteenth centuries far more than they influenced
165. the development of rhetoric. Thus, although
166. Rhetorica survives in nearly one hundred medieval manuscripts, its influence on medieval rhetoric has not been definitively determined.
167. The second tradition, based on precepts
168. originated by Isocrates, was the more practical
169. approach that dominated rhetorical studies
170. during the Middle Ages. Designated by Murphy
171. as the Ciceronian tradition, it includes the seven
172. rhetorical works of Cicero himself (106–43
173. B.C.E.), the Rhetorica ad Herennium (nearly
174. contemporary with Cicero and wrongly attributed to him until 1491), and a mutilated text of
175. Quintilian's Institutio oratoria (92C E . ) , the
176. great summary of the classical Latin arts ofdiscourse. This corpus transmitted the Ciceronian
177. scheme of the five parts of rhetoric: inventio
178. (finding of material), dispositio (arranging of it),
179. elocutio (putting words to invented material),
180. pronuntiatio (oral delivery), and memoria (retention of ideas, words, and their order).
181. Without doubt, Cicero was the magister
182. eloquentiae of the Middle Ages, with the name
183. Tullius occurring in nearly every discussion of
184. discourse and among most of the medieval writers, from Thomas Aquinas to Boccaccio. Indeed,
185. his reputation continued through the eighteenth
186. century and beyond. All of the early encyclopedists allude to Cicero as their chief auctor. Cicero,
187. along with Seneca, is placed in the first circle (of
188. Limbo) by Dante in his Divine Comedy.

191. rhetorical works. Cicero had constructed his
192. pragmatic scheme of rhetorical elements in close
193. association with Roman law in De Inventione,
194. and Rhetorica ad Herennium closely parallels
195. it. Therefore, although Cicero composed seven
196. rhetorical treatises over a period of fifty years,

204. name, in the form of Donet, became a synonym
205. for primer, and it is to him that we owe the
206. transmission of such standard concepts as the
207. "eight parts of speech." Abbreviated versions of
208. Donatus's works introduced medieval scholars
209. to Latin, and Priscian's Institutionum gram-
210. maticae (510C.E.) became the standard advanced grammar textbook.
211. Also included in this tradition is Horace's Ars
212. poetica (written between 23 and 13 B.C.E.), representing an extension of the basic grammatical
213. process. As Quintilian explains in Institutio
214. oratoria, grammar is not only the science of speaking and writing correctly but also the art of interpreting the poets (enarratio poetarum). Thus,
215. Horace's Ars poetica, consisting of 476 verses of
216. preceptive advice about the composition of poetry,
217. is an important work of the grammatical tradition. Its inclusion as part of the medieval grammar
218. curriculum preserved this significant poetics treatise and transmitted it from antiquity to the Renaissance poets and critics and beyond.
219. Finally, a fourth tradition arose from the
220. Second Sophistic period (50–400 C.E.), in which
221. two schoolroom exercises flourished—decla-
222. matio (exercises in fictitious speechmaking) and
223. progymnasmata (short exercises in composition).
224. During the Middle Ages, however, the function of
225. the declamationes shifted from an emphasis on
226. speechmaking to writing, as the declamationes
227. were used as sources for stories or exempla (examples). The sophistic tradition, then, emphasized
228. the pedagogy of writing by generating collections
229. of examples and preceptive guides and by itsfocus on exemplary models, preserving an idea of
230. the connection between the arts of language and
231. ethical behavior. Two representative texts include
232. Priscian's De praeexercitamentis rhetoricis (551–
233. 560), a translation (or adaptation, some argue)
234. into Latin of part of Hermogenes' Progymnasmata and the Elder Seneca's Declamationes.
235. Despite their varying degrees of influence,
236. all four traditions assumed that the arts of discourse could be learned preceptively. Throughout the medieval period, the works of classical
237. tradition and the works of modern theorists existed side by side but never equally. Although
238. the works of antiquity had the advantage of
239. auctoritas in a bookish age, modern works had
240. the appeal of the new.
241. Augustine's De doctrina christiana
242. The first of the "new" emerged in the fifth century and represented a movement toward a modern medieval theory of rhetoric. This was a time
243. of great debate over how much, if any, of Greek
244. and Roman culture should be adopted by the
245. Christian community. In 392C.E., when the
246. emperor Theodosius formally abolished paganism by decree, many recent converts—among
247. them writers, poets, orators, and artists—reacted
248. violently against their former culture, renouncing paganism completely and never again referring to any pagan poets, rhetoricians, or orators
249. in their works.
250. Augustine of Hippo (354–430 C.E.), a rhetorician and convert himself, did not regard the
251. culture of the old order with the same revulsion
252. and fear that many of his contemporaries did.
253. Instead, he viewed it more practically as a tool
254. to aid Christian thinkers.
255. In De doctrina christiana (composed
256. between 396 and 426), Augustine used this figure to promote the conscientious use of pagan
257. philosophy:
258. Just as the Egyptians had not only idols
259. and grave burdens which the people ofIsrael detested and avoided, so also they had
260. vases and ornaments of gold and silver and
261. clothing which the Israelites took with
262. them secretly when they fled, as if to put
263. them to better use. (II.xl.60)
264. Augustine himself took the "Egyptian gold" of
265. Greek and Roman philosophy and logic and
266. skillfully put it to work for the new task of interpreting Christian Scripture, thereby synthesizing old and new.
267. Augustine was the first theorist of the Middle Ages to establish a clearly preceptive tradition in discourse for the Christian community.
268. De doctrina christiana begins with these words:
269. "There are two things necessary to the treatment of Scriptures: a way of discovering those
270. things which are to be understood, and a way
271. of teaching what we have learned" (I.i).
272. De doctrina is composed of four books.
273. The first three deal with how the words of
274. Scripture may be interpreted by examining language in terms of "signs." Book 3 deals specifically with the problem of ambiguity in both literal and figurative language. Book 4, however,
275. called by some the "first Christian manual of
276. rhetoric," suggests how to teach by pleading
277. for eloquentia and advocating the union of
278. pagan rhetorical form (chiefly Ciceronian) and
279. Christian content. De doctrina christiana was
280. important not only in establishing a preceptive
281. tradition but also in authorizing Ciceronian
282. rhetorical precepts and advocating imitatio as
283. a way to learn eloquence, thereby helping to
284. preserve and transmit the "gold" of classical
285. authors into the Middle Ages.
286. Transition and Transmission
287. Classical rhetorical form and pagan content
288. were preserved and transmitted into the Middle
289. Ages via two other means based on the preceptive principle: medieval encyclopedias and educational curricula.
290. Encyclopedias and Educational Curricula
291. In the fifth century, Martianus Capella gave this
292. famous description of Lady Rhetoric in his allegorical encyclopedia De nuptiis philologiae et
293. mercurii (410–427):
294. The garment under her arms was covered
295. by a robe wound about her shoulders in
296. the Latin fashion; this robe was adorned
297. with the light of all kinds of devices and
298. showed the figures of them all. . . indeed
299. it was thought that she could hurl thunderbolts like Jove. For like a queen with power
300. over everything, she could drive any host
301. of people where she wanted and draw
302. them back from where she wanted; she
303. could sway them to tears and whip them
304. to a frenzy, and change the countenance
305. and senses not only of cities, but of armies
306. in battle. (Stahl and Johnson 156)
307. In addition to this flamboyant portrayal of
308. rhetoric, which cleverly employs the very devices
309. it portrays, De nuptiis philologiae et mercurii has
310. been credited with introducing the seven liberal
311. arts—the whole curriculum of the pagan Roman
312. schools and thus rhetoric as well—into the Middle
313. Ages. It should be noted, however, that unlike De
314. doctrina christiana, Martianus presents rhetoric
315. and the other disciplines without taking an explicit
316. theological or philosophical stand in the intellectual controversies of his times.
317. The De nuptiis offers seven highly ornamented allegorical portraits of the subjects that
318. made up the Roman educational curriculum in
319. the following order: grammar, dialectic, rhetoric, geometry, arithmetic, astronomy, and music. (By the fifth century, medicine and architecture, which had been included in the curriculum
320. earlier, had been deleted.) Each allegorical portrait is followed by a compendium of the art.
321. In the next century,Flavius Cassiodorus
322. Senator (480–575), in his Institutiones divinar-
323. um et saecularium litterarum, and Bishop Isidore
324. of Seville (570–636), in his Etymologies, followed the order of the curriculum elements laid
325. out by Martianus in their encyclopedias, thus
326. affirming the elements of the trivium (the first
327. three subjects of the curriculum, which deal with
328. words) and the quadrivium (the last four subjects, which deal with mathematical concepts),
329. the core curriculum throughout the Middle Ages.
330. Cassiodorus's Institutiones, influenced by
331. both Augustine and Martianus, was designed
332. to instruct clerics in their religious and secular
333. studies. Divided into two parts, the first half
334. is devoted to divinity and the second half to the
335. liberal arts; thus its two parts were often copied separately. Rhetoric, in the liberal arts
336. section, is borrowed from second- and third-
337. century works such as the Artis rhetoricae libri
338. III of Fortunatianus and Julius Victor's Ars
339. rhetorica (the first rhetorical work to include
340. a section on letter-writing).
341. The last encyclopedist of the age of
342. the Church Fathers, Isidore preserved a large
343. amount of classical lore in his Etymologia, but,
344. unlike Cassiodorus or Martianus, he established
345. the seven liberal arts as preliminary to further
346. work in divine studies. Etymologia describes a
347. whole educational sysrem in which the first two
348. books, including grammar, rhetoric, and dialectic
349. (the trivium), lay out a general curriculum for
350. all clergy. The remaining seventeen books focus
351. on information particular to various fields
352. and professions.
353. Thus, during this period of transition, classical rhetorical ideas hardened, via their inclusion
354. in somewhat empty pedagogical formulas such
355. as the seven liberal arts. In theory, the trivium
356. was taught to all students in the grammar
357. schools, while the quadrivium was reserved for
358. only the best students, preparatory to studies for
359. careers in the learned professions of theology,
360. philosophy, law, or medicine. In practice, however, not every school was able to offer a full curriculum; thus grammar was the only universally
361. studied subject (hence, "grammar schools"). Students whose schools offered the entire trivium,
362. however, studied rhetoric, which included instruction in the composition of both prose and
363. poetry, with special attention, particularly after
364. the tenth century, given to letter-writing.
365. By the ninth century, it had become common practice among encyclopedists and
366. educators to assimilate the whole system of
367. classical rhetoric into Christian methodology.
368. However, this began to change when Rabanus
369. Maurus, a pupil of Alcuin's, broke with tradition in his rhetorical treatiseDe institutione
370. clericorum (819C.E.), by making pragmatic
371. choices and incorporating only those classical
372. concepts that served his purposes.
373. Instead of adopting only the Ciceronian
374. system as a whole, as Augustine and his followers had done, Rabanus chose his tenets from
375. various sources—sometimes from personal experience or dialectic, as well as from Cicero and
376. Augustine. Although Rabanus clearly borrows
377. most from Augustine, this too is a break with
378. tradition in that he replaces Cicero with a more
379. contemporary rhetoric master.
380. Authors after Rabanus adopted his method,
381. picking and choosing from classical and other
382. sources according to their needs. The evolution
383. of discourse in the Middle Ages, therefore, shows
384. fragmentation instead of unity: The authors of
385. treatises on letter-writing chose only the parts of
386. works applicable to their art, as did the authors
387. of manuals on preaching and poetics.
388. The Three Rhetorical Arts
389. Letter-writing
390. Because citizens in antiquity were well
391. educated, an orator like Cicero or an emperor like Augustus Caesar was completely
392. capable of composing and transcribing
393. his own thoughts. But because of the educational decline of the fifth and sixth centuries, relatively few were capable of reading or writing in the Middle Ages. The
394. growing complexity in the worlds of both
395. church and state, however, required communication.
396. The early solution to this problem was the
397. drafting of sets of formulas and exemplary
398. models to be duplicated and adapted to various situations. Medieval formularies, like so
399. many business forms today, usually provided
400. blank spaces for inserting information such as
401. names. Sometimes this was indicated by the
402. letter N for nomen ("name").
403. Basically notarial, most medieval formularies dealt with contracts of some type or other,
404. such as this one from Tours:
405. It is right that those who have promised
406. us unbroken faith should be rewarded by
407. our aid and protection. Now since our
408. faithful subject [name] with the will of
409. God has come to our palace with his arms
410. and has there sworn in our hands to keep
411. his trust and fidelity to us, therefore we
412. decree and command by this present writing henceforth the said (name) is to be
413. numbered among our antrustiones [dependents]. If anyone shall presume to slay
414. him, let him know that he shall have to
415. pay 600 solidi as a wergeld for him. (Murphy, Rhetoric 201)
416. But even several hundred such models could not cover all the intricacies of
417. message transmission and documentation.
418. A new solution was found with the birth
419. of the ars dictaminis, or the preceptive art of
420. letter-writing.
421. The first to link rhetoric with letter-writing
422. (after Julius Victor) was Alberic, a monk of
423. Monte Cassino, who around 1087 wrote formal
424. treatises on the new art: Dictaminum radii (or
425. Flores rhetorici), which deals with rhetorical
426. ornament, and Breviarium de dictamine, which
427. deals with letter-writing specifically. In both,
428. Alberic assumes that rhetorical principles may be
429. employed in writing as well as speaking, inaccordance with classical tradition. But he breaks
430. with that tradition when he encourages students
431. to apply Cicero's parts of a speech to the composition of letters.
432. The next development in ars dictaminis
433. shifted attention from Monte Cassino to Bologna, where several significant works on the
434. subject appeared in the early part of the
435. twelfth century. Important theorists included
436. Adalbertus Samaritanus, Hugh of Bologna,
437. and Bernard of Romagna. The first to set forth
438. the standard medieval five parts of a letter,
439. however, was the anonymous author of
440. Rationes dictandi (1135C.E.).
441. This treatise, written in a pragmatic
442. tone, assumes the reader's acceptance of
443. a basic doctrine of letter-writing by presenting an "approved format" for letters: "There
444. are, in fact, five parts of a letter: the Salutation, the Securing of Good-Will, the
445. Narration, the Petition, and the Conclusion"
446. (Murphy, Three Arts 7). The treatise is composed of thirteen sections, the greatest
447. emphasis being on the salutation and the
448. need of the writer to attend carefully to
449. the social level of the letter's recipient. The
450. author presents a range of proper addresses,
451. from greetings to the Pope to "salutations of
452. Delinquent Sons to their Parents." In addition
453. to being an excellent example of a popular
454. letterwriting manual, Rationes dictandi also
455. provides an interesting view of the strata of
456. medieval society.
457. By 1200 many popular manuals were being written, mostly in Italy. The doctrine underlying all of these manuals was the analogy
458. between letter-writing and the Ciceronian
459. parts of a speech. In addition to these letter-
460. writing manuals, several collections of model
461. letters circulated in medieval Europe. After the
462. early thirteenth century, however, few innovations appeared; instead, the masters were copied and used.
463. F reaching
464. A second branch of the medieval arts of discourse was the ars praedicandi, or the art
465. of preaching. Preaching sermons was an important feature of the Church, and preachers
466. probably followed Augustine's advice to study
467. good preachers instead of good books, or used
468. Cicero's rhetoric as a model. In any case sermons were apparently rather informal, direct,
469. and conversational in tone. Referred to as being in the "homily" style, these sermons did not
470. follow a systematic progression of ideas or a
471. prescribed format.
472. That situation changed when several Latin sermons patterned in a new way were
473. preached at the University of Paris in the academic year 1230–1231. The new form, called
474. the "university-style," or "thematic" sermon,
475. employed a scriptural quotation as "theme"
476. and then a complex system of division and
477. amplification based on the opening quotation.
478. Within a few years of the advent of
479. this development, several specialized preaching manuals emerged throughout northern Europe. Nearly three hundred authors of these
480. manuals, written into the sixteenth century,
481. have been identified, with English authors especially prominent.
482. Robert of Basevorn's Forma praedicandi,
483. written at Oxford in 1322, is typical of the art
484. that designates six parts of a sermon, analogous
485. to the Ciceronian parts of oratory. It is distinct,
486. however, from the format described for ars
487. dictaminis in that it emphasizes division and
488. amplification. The organizational plan is as follows:
489. 1. Theme: a Scriptural quotation
490. 2. Protheme: introduction for the theme
491. itself, usually followed by a prayer
492. 3. Introduction of a theme (antetheme):
493. explanation of the purpose of the sermon
494. 4. Division of the theme: usually into three
495. (or multiples of three), with auctoritates
496. to "prove" each division
497. 5. Subdivision of theme
498. 6. Amplification or dilation of each of the
499. divisions and subdivisions
500. Even though at first the "thematic" sermon
501. might seem to be rather academic and similar
502. to a spoken gloss, the whole emphasis of the
503. first parts was upon involving the audience, in
504. accordance with Cicero's theory of oration.
505. What distinguishes the ars praedicandi from the
506. ars dictaminis and other forms of discourse, in
507. fact, was the attention given to the fact of a living audience.
508. In addition to the preaching manuals that
509. provided the form, medieval preachers made
510. use of other types of aids to supply the matter
511. of their sermons: the Scriptures themselves,
512. collections of exempla, concordances and alphabetized lists designed to find material, and
513. collections of sermons.
