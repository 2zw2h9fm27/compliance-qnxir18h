Educator whose work focuses on rhetoric as a
dynamic discipline operating within institutional
communities. Lauer began with an inquiry into
rhetorical invention to correct the undervaluing
of invention in rhetorical practice in the composition pedagogy of the 1960s and 1970s. Her
interests have since extended to include persuasion, the interdisciplinary nature of rhetoric, and
the issues of program development and research
in rhetoric—particularly with empirical research
in collaboration with specialists in psychology,
linguistics, and educational measurement. Although this may suggest a diffused focus, in fact
it represents a coherent point of view controlled
by the following assumptions:
1. A knowledge of the history of rhetoric is
a resource for understanding contemporary theoretical problems in rhetoric
2. The value of rhetorical knowledge is in
its applications to pedagogical practice
based on the resources of empirical, descriptive, and conceptual research
3. Rhetoric is most usefully understood as
an inquiry-oriented process
4. Rhetoric runs the risk of being institutionally "ghettoized" if it does not work
with other correlated disciplines, especially in the social sciences
5. Assessment scales based on rhetorical/
interpretive aspects of writing are more
useful than those based on formal/linguistic scales in assessing writing
A substantial part of Lauer's published
work is collaborative and interdisciplinary:
Much of her published work is collaborative,
involving rhetoricians (Richard Enos, Andrea
Lunsford, and Janet Emig), linguists (Ulla Connor), psychologists (John Nicholls), and specialists in educational measurement (William
Asher). The collaborative process runs deeply
and richly through all her work in its situational
quality—from her reliance on Richard Young's
tagmemic methods in her writing to her dialectical controversy with Ann Berthoff.
Her work in the history and theory of rhetorical invention has been in the main descriptive and encyclopedic. This historical background, moreover, is frequently drawn on in
other works to help to shape the language and
models for practice in the development of pedagogical materials and research methods.
Her work in the area of discipline and
program cultivation has an axiological flavor:
She sees value in disciplines and interdisciplinary
teams insofar as they comprise productive communities that are extending the realm of knowledge and practice, and she works to draw on the
specialized knowledge and methods of other disciplines, particularly psychology and learning theory, that share issues or problems with rhetoric.
Her work in the area of empirical research
demonstrates how empirical, descriptive, historical, and theoretical studies can and should
complement and enhance each other. She wrestles with the problems of defining the training
requirements for students in a field so broadly
defined, and at the same time she models the
process of strategically integrating methods and
perspectives and collaborating with other disciplines.
Her most important achievement has been
to demonstrate the wide applicability of rhetorical knowledge and training. Her publications
are a gold mine of applications of rhetorical
concepts. The integration of theoretical, empirical, and pedagogical method thus gives her
work a decidedly pragmatic cast, which makes
the separation of theory and practice appropriately difficult.
Phil M.Keith
St. Cloud State University

14. Legal Rhetoric
15. Any oral or written discourse inscribing or supporting the generation, interpretation, application, amendment, or repeal of laws and contracts
16. in a liberal, representative democracy. Legal rhetoric since Aristotle has been conceived to have
17. two main divisions, forensic and deliberative. It
18. is distinguishable from political rhetoric, whose
19. aim is the praising or blaming of individuals or organizations (for their intelligence, moral character, and past actions) during electoral campaigns.
20. In its forensic mode, legal rhetoric encompasses the enormous range of discourse conditioned by the settlement of disputes through
21. court litigation and arbitration before neutral
22. third parties. The paradigm forensic situations
23. are considered to involve (1) lawyer-to-judge or
24. (2) judge-to-lawyer rhetoric. Thus there is the
25. lawyer's argument in behalf of a plaintiff or defendant directed to a judge or jury—that is, in
26. trial courts and courts of appeal. Adapting earlier rhetoricians such as Hermagoras, Cicero
27. maintained that all such arguments fall into one
28. of four kinds of stases: arguments of fact (whether or not an act was committed), arguments of
29. definition (how an act, if committed, was to
30. be categorized or described in view of existing
31. laws), arguments of jurisdiction (whether a case
32. was properly tried in a given court at a given
33. time), and arguments of quality (whether an action, even if committed and deemed illegal, might
34. still be deemed just or excusable in view of the
35. equities). At the conclusion of a trial or appellate
36. hearing, modern judge-to-lawyer rhetoric occurs
37. in written judicial opinions that announce and
38. present justification for the court's decision.
39. Closely connected to this forensic paradigm
40. are many exigencies requiring lawyer-to-lawyer
41. rhetoric, such as when one lawyer attempts to
42. persuade another (opposing lawyer) not to go to
43. trial because the opponent's case lacks foundation in law or fact or when among a team of lawyers there is a debate about the proper arguments
44. to make on a specific legal issue. Other exigencies involve lawyer-to-witness discourse, as when
45. a lawyer uses rhetorical strategies to elicit testimony from a witness before or during trial. Conversely, one also observes legal rhetoric in the
46. efforts of witnesses or clients to shape the perceptions, beliefs, and actions of lawyers concerning
47. the handling of a case. And finally, there is the
48. juror-to-juror rhetoric that occurs during formal
49. jury deliberations at trial.
50. In its deliberative mode, legal rhetoric encompasses the enormous range of discourse
51. conditioned by the making of laws and binding
52. agreements in assemblies or negotiations.
53. The paradigmatic deliberative situations are
54. thought to involve ( 1 ) legislator-to-legislator or
55. (2) legislator-to-citizen rhetoric. There is thus
56. the senator's effort to persuade colleagues to
57. vote for (or against) a particular bill, and the
58. similar effort to drum up or reduce support among the public by members of executive
59. branches of government such as governors,
60. presidents, and prime ministers. Conversely,
61. there are the efforts of citizen, activist, or lobbying groups to persuade legislators to generate
62. or adopt particular bills, or direct citizen-to-citizen rhetoric exercised during initiative and referendum campaigns that sidestep legislative
63. review and enactment to put new laws on popular ballots.
64. Perhaps the common element in bothforensic and deliberative modes of legal rhetoric
65. is the effort by a rhetor to persuade others as to
66. what particular laws or contracts have meant in
67. the past, what they will mean in the future, or
68. what they should mean at present, in view of a
69. particular case or social exigency.
70. History of Legal Rhetoric
71. In the indispensable work by Rieke, the historical demarcation of legal rhetoric from rhetoric
72. generally conceived began with the rise of
73. professional schools for lawyers in the late Middle Ages and early Renaissance. These schools
74. tried to shed the legacy of the empty reasoning
75. practiced under the name of rhetoric in the
76. Latin trivium, while at the same time they
77. appeared to lose sight of the philosophical
78. and political necessity for legal rhetorical theory grandly defended in the writings of Cicero and Quintilian. Even the more directly utilitarian, technical methods for discovering legal
79. arguments painstakingly articulated by Cicero in De inventione, perhaps the greatest single work on forensic rhetoric and argumentation ever written, seem not to have been
80. assimilated when lawyering and law schools became professionalized during this period. Ever
81. since, rhetorical theory has been forced to maintain an apologetic stance toward law and lawyers, needing to demonstrate its relevance and
82. value in the face of great skepticism.
83. In Rieke and other scholars, several explanations are offered as to why there has been little
84. later sustained development of classical rhetorical theory for lawyers. One of these explanations,
85. according to Rieke, is that Western democratic
86. legal systems, with their increasingly elaborate
87. rules for evidence and proof, actually absorbed
88. and reflected classical rhetorical theory in an implicit way. That is, to the extent that Aristotle and
89. Cicero addressed themselves in their rhetorical
90. treatises to norms for proper reasoning, Rieke
91. suggests that their work did in fact have influence—but not as rhetorical theory or as legal rhetorical theory per se. A second explanation is that
92. the training of lawyers through apprenticeship
93. and imitation, which began in the late Middle
94. Ages, obviated the need for any comprehensive
95. theorizing; one learned legal rhetoric "by doing
96. it" under the watchful eyes of a mentor. In that
97. sense it might be said that the pre-Aristotelian
98. schools of sophistic rhetoric became the models
99. for lawyers and for legal education in rhetoric.
100. A third explanation is the Enlightenment
101. view that both making and proving a correct
102. legal decision are matters of scientific logic and
103. deductive demonstration, a view that reached its
104. zenith in Langdell's case method for preparing
105. lawyers at Harvard Law School. That view, perhaps more than any other factor, is what to this
106. day has marginalized the study of legal reading
107. and writing tasks, as well as study of rhetorical
108. theory within professional law schools. As Rieke
109. further details, there were some important experiments in adapting classical rhetorical theory
110. to the preparation of lawyers in American law
111. schools early in their history, particularly at Yale;
112. but those experiments have had little lasting influence, and rhetorical theory is rarely a required
113. course of study in modern American law schools.
114. Relation of Rhetoric, Legal Argumentation,
115. and Legal Reasoning
116. Underlying the issue as to whether lawyers draw
117. upon or need rhetorical theory is the deeper question concerning the nature of legal reasoning and
118. legal argumentation. Is legal reasoning different
119. from other forms of reasoning, especially scientific induction and deduction? And if it is different, how should it be described? Much modern
120. inquiry by philosophers, jurisprudential scholars,
121. and rhetorical theorists has addressed itself to
122. this issue. Despite widespread recognition that
123. deductive logic can be used to analyze only certain aspects of legal reasoning and argument,
124. as variously shown in the important works of
125. Frank, Gottlieb, Stone, Levi, Wasserstrom, Perel-
126. man, Llewellyn, Moore, and Parker, a coherent,
127. widely accepted model of the reasoning manifest
128. in legal argumentation has not been realized. As
129. discussed in more recent inquiries, such as those
130. of Stratman, several problems contribute to the
131. difficulty. One is that many theorists confound
132. the theoretically distinct tasks of description with
133. prescription; commonly, how a theorist believes
134. legal arguments and decisions are constructed
135. largely reflects how, from an ethical or epistemo-
136. logical perspective, they believe they should be
137. constructed. This confusion has particularly
138. haunted theorists who, like Wasserstrom, find
139. deductive logic to be the best framework in
140. which to understand legal argumentation.
141. A second problem is that most theory has
142. uncritically assumed that the rhetorical reasoning of lawyers as advocates and the rhetorical
143. reasoning of judges as decision-makers is fundamentally the same—that is, that there is some single root reasoning process for their activity. For
144. example, until theorists like Perelman, there has
145. been relatively little concern for how audience
146. perception and social cognition generally fit into
147. legal reasoning and argumentation processes.
148. A third problem, and arguably the most
149. serious, is that theorists have confounded the
150. complex processes of discovering legal arguments and making legal decisions with the texts
151. that are the results—or outputs—of those processes (for example, court briefs or opinions).
152. Thus, for example, Jensen remarks at the opening of his book, "My concern as a student of
153. logic is with the process leading to judicial decisions in so far as they revolve around a question
154. of law, and not with the legal soundness of
155. the decisions themselves. Moreover, only one aspect of the judicial process [will be] considered,
156. namely, the reasoning a judge gives in support of
157. his decisions, and which is also believed to be the
158. reasoning by means of which he arrived at them"
159. (1 ). Though the problems with this equation are
160. discussed in the literature, most of the major
161. work before 1970 attempts to build theories of
162. legal rhetoric by taking only formal written arguments as relevant data, so that the "processes"
163. of legal rhetoric are reduced to processes that
164. seem inherent in or inferable from these texts.
165. Research On Legal Rhetoric: Sources
166. Empirical research on legal rhetoric increasingly
167. examines contexts, social and psychological
168. processes, and genres that extend far beyond
169. lawyers' briefs and judges' court opinions. The
170. effort to widen the scope of inquiry in legal
171. rhetoric can be seen clearly in the educational,
172. sociolinguistic, and anthropological, as well as
173. psychological, communication, and cognitive
174. science research streams. For educational researchers, Wren and Wren provide a careful historical account of how instruction in legal
175. research and written legal genres came to be
176. marginalized in professional law school curricula. For sociolinguists, anthropologists, and
177. students of the history of legal language, the
178. works of Melinkoff, O'Barr, and Danet reflect
179. the ongoing effort to understand the effects of
180. legal rhetoric within larger social and cultural
181. contexts. The effort to understand the relation
182. between cognitive processes involved in the production of legal rhetoric and the cognitive processes involved in its interpretation during attempts at persuasion in appellate courts can best
183. be seen in the work of Stratman and Skinner.
184. Finally, for what is perhaps the most active and
185. intensive empirical research stream—jury instruction, decision and influence processes—the
186. work of Sales, and Hastie, Penrod, and Pennington provide comprehensive overviews.
