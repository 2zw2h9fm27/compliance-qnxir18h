## Definition & Etymology

<div className="flex items-center mb-8">

<div className="prose prose-lg max-w-none leading-relaxed bg-blue-50 border-blue-200 border-l-4 pl-6 py-2 pr-4 rounded-r-lg">

Most generally, language in use. An instance of
discourse employs a language, as studied by linguistics, and organizes this language into coherent sentences, as studied by semantics. Studying
the sentences as discourse, however, focuses on
the text's functions in the human world. An
instance of discourse may be written or oral.
Discourse has many social functions; that is
to say, people use language for a wide variety of
purposes. These diverse social functions can be
said to give rise to groups of instances of discourse that all share certain conventions of language use, of greater or lesser specificity and
binding force, recognized by all users. Thus the
term discourse may be used to refer to one of
these groups, as well as to one instance of language in use.
Theories of discourse typically classify
groups of instances of discourse according to
broadly defined social functions. For example,
Alexander Bain's important premodern theory of
discourse classifies discourse types according to
what they convey to recipients about their subject matter: "description" sets forth a static
scene; "narration" tells a story; "exposition" offers rational explanation; "persuasion" seeks to
move to some action concerning the content; and
"poetry" arouses pleasurable emotions about the
content. James Kinneavy's important modern
theory of discourse classifies instances of discourse according to what need of the producer
(or "encoder") is primarily addressed: to express
feelings ("expressive"); to influence the recipients
(or "decoders"; "persuasive"); to convey information about reality ("referential"); or to call
attention to the language of the discourse itself
("literary").
The concept of discourse may also be used
to refer to more specifically defined groups of
instances of discourse, with conventions of language use more narrowly defined within a particular human group. For example, "academic
discourse" refers to typical language use within
the academic community, shaped by shared
conventions of language use among all members of the academic community. Instances of
academic discourse might be a student paper,
classroom lecture, a scholarly book, or a conversation between two researchers.
Patricia Bizzell
College of the Holy Cross

    **Discourse** constitutes a fundamental concept in rhetorical theory, referring to the structured organization and deployment of persuasive means. Its meaning has evolved from classical codifications to encompass both technical procedures and philosophical principles.
  </Tabs.Item>
  <Tabs.Item title="Historical Development">
    The Roman rhetoricians devoted extensive discussion to **Discourse**, systematizing it into rules and catalogues designed to guide the practitioner. From *ad Herennium* and *De Inventione* through the medieval ars dictaminis, the concept retained its central role in rhetorical education.
  </Tabs.Item>

11. The study of texts and readers' interactions
12. with them. Some scholars make a clear distinction between text linguistics and discourse
13. analysis. Given this distinction, those scholars
14. who work in the field of text linguistics are
15. primarily concerned with units of language
16. that extend beyond sentence boundaries, even
17. though a text may consist of a single sentence
18. or even a single word—as in the case of many
19. traffic signs. Those scholars working within
20. the realm of discourse analysis, on the other
21. hand, are interested in how readers/listeners
22. form coherent discourse as they interact with
23. texts. That is, discourse analysts study how
24. readers process texts. While many scholars
25. do make this distinction, others use only the
26. term discourse analysis to denote both texts
27. and readers' constructions of meaning from
28. those texts.
29. In the elementary stages of discourse
30. analysis, scholars looked, for the most part,
31. at the occurrence of syntactic features. In the
32. 1930s, John Firth was one of the first to urge
33. linguists to study language in context, but it
34. was not until 1952 that Zellig Harris, trained
35. in the tradition of structuralist linguistics,
36. published an article titled "Discourse Analysis," in which he addressed the need to describe the formal features of texts. Throughout the 1960s linguists began in earnest to
37. examine texts linguistically, but, because they
38. were primarily trained to examine units at or
39. below the level of the sentence, they tended to
40. look at texts, more or less, as simple strings
41. of sentences. At the same time that interest in
42. larger linguistic units was growing, seman-

44. emerging as extensions of Noam Chomsky's
45. transformational syntax.
46. In the early 1970s, scholars began
47. offering a chorus of criticisms of sentence
48. grammars. Alternative views of language
49. came from anthropologists, sociologists, and
50. sociolinguists, who emphasized social interactions within groups of language users.
51. Concurrently, computer scientists were
52. becoming interested in simulating natural language production and comprehension. Psychologists began conducting human memory
53. studies that evolved into an interest in
54. memory for information in texts. Contributions from and interest demonstrated by
55. scholars in all of these fields helped to shape
56. text linguistics and discourse analysis into
57. an integrated field with a broad base of
58. support.
59. Much of the work in the field throughout the 1970s was concerned with discourse
60. coherence. Some scholars, whose work grew
61. out of the Prague School of linguistics,
62. considered textual coherence as the result
63. of efficient connections between old or given
64. information ("theme") and new information ("rheme"). Other scholars examined
65. discourse coherence within the framework
66. of generative semantics, with coherence
67. as the product of local and global connections within texts. Still others treated coherence as the product of arrangement or organization of texts. The greatest refinement
68. in the study of discourse coherence was
69. that offered in 1976 by M.A.K. Halliday
70. and Ruqaiya Hasan in Cohesion in English.
71. Especially in the late 1970s, cognitive psychologists and reading theorists were examining the extent to which readers construct
72. textual coherence in a bottom-up manner
73. (that is, using words as building blocks
74. for larger segments of text) and the extent
75. to which readers construct textual coherence
76. in a top-down manner (using the title of a text
77. as an organizing device for processing
78. the words, phrases, clauses, and paragraphs in
79. that text).
80. Another major movement in the 1970s
81. was pragmatics, the study of language
82. use and users in context. Perhaps the greatest influence on pragmatics was speech
83. act theory, which examines relations among
84. language forms, intentions, functions, and
85. meanings in context. Related to pragmatics is
86. the ethnography of speaking, whose goal is to
87. describe the linguistic options that are available and situationally appropriate to members of a speech community.
