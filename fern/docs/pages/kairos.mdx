## Definition & Etymology

<div className="flex items-center mb-8">

Right timing and proper measure—directly related to the rhetorical importance of time,
place, speaker, and audience, the proper and
knowledgeable analysis of these factors, and
the faculty of using the proper means in a particular context to arrive atbelief. For the
Pythagoreans, kairos is one of the laws of the
universe referring to the balance between thesis and antithesis.
In order to understand kairos better,
one must try to understand Greek ways of
knowing and thinking. Key elements in Greek
thought are thesis and antithesis. Many Greek
thinkers explore polarities that lead to a oneness in duality. Only by identifying the polarities
and apparent contradictions in the universe
can one hope to find a balance that will lead
to knowledge. The struggle between polarities such as doubt versus belief, appearance of
truth versus reality, and deception versus persuasion is central to classical thought and forms
one basis for important debates among rhetoricians. Kairos is central to this struggle and
the ensuing balance.
The Pythagoreans subscribe to a construct
based on opposing concepts. They see the function of kairos as allowing these forces to bring
harmony to conflicting opposites. For the Pythagoreans, kairos represents an "overall sense
of Tightness—a critical point in time and
space . . . the conflict and resolution of form
and matter that initiated the creation of the
universe and all that is therein" (Carter 102). It
is from such a philosophy of balance and harmony embodied in kairos that Gorgias and
other sophists derive their concept of adapting
persuasive discourse to the particular circumstances of each discourse act.
The sophistic search for truth and ethical
communication in a relativistic world relies on
the choices and balances between competing,
conflicting logoi. Poulakos suggests two roles
for kairos in guiding these choices in Gorgianic
rhetoric: first, the need for "temporality of the
situation" in which the rhetorical act occurs
and, second, the "impetus for discourse, the
tension in the situation" (Poulakos 39–41).
These roles expand on a generative kairos that
breaks up opposing elements in a rhetorical
situation and allows rhetors to persuade themselves and others of the difference between right
and wrong (Kinneavy 1979:14). Thus kairos
exhibits rich ethical implications in addition to
its epistemological and rhetorical facets.
Modern uses of kairos are linked closely
with situational context and have direct applications to a variety of fields. James L. Kinneavy
traces the development of classical Greek kairos
and offers some suggestions for its use in contemporary contexts and in contemporary college curriculum planning. He argues for the
inclusion of kairos in literary, biblical, legal, and
philosophical hermeneutics and insists that
kairos is implicitly evidenced in speech communication, tagmemics, poststructuralist literary
criticism, and in the work of Freud, Kenneth
Burke, and E.D. Hirsch (Kinneavy 1986:104).
Kinneavy bases his own design for a college
composition program around a multifaceted
application of kairos. He stresses the need to
explore the ethical, epistemological, aesthetic,
and rhetorical aspects of a complete definition
of kairos, a concept far richer and more complex than "saying the right thing at the right
time."
Sheri L. Helsley
Old Dominion University

450. Kristeva, Julia (b. 1941)
451. Linguist and semiotician born in Bulgaria in
452. 1941 but living in Paris since 1966, contributing
453. important work on relationships between language and gender. Kristeva served as one of the
454. only women on the editorial board of Tel Quel
455. and is currently a professor at the Sorbonne and
456. a practicing psychoanalyst. She has worked with
457. Lucian Goldmann, Claude Lévi-Strauss, Jacques
458. Lacan, and Roland Barthes, one of her strongest
459. influences. Her initial and primary theoretical
460. influences are Marxism, Russian Formalism, and
461. Hegel. A combination of socialist and psychoanalytic theories, she believes, is better able to accommodate fundamental differences among all
462. people within an egalitarian structure than can
463. Marxism alone.
464. Kristeva explains in essays such as "About
465. Chinese Women" that the symbolic realm is the
466. realm of men, of paternity, of linear time, of
467. knowledge and power, of traditional academic
468. discourse; it is a realm from which women are
469. excluded. Women, in contrast, are associated
470. with the unconscious, withjouissance, with
471. maternity, with nonlanguage, nonspeech, with
472. the body, with the presymbolic, with the un-
473. nameable.
474. Emphasizing the importance of the disruptive nature of women's marginal discourse,
475. Kristeva sees women's order as outside time,
476. outside traditional notions of truth and falsehood. All discourse bears traces of women's
477. discourse, according to Kristeva; they can be
478. recovered by listening for the unspoken, by
479. emphasizing whatever remains unsatisfied, repressed, new, eccentric, incomprehensible. As
480. she explains in her stylistically innovative essay
481. "Stabat Mater," maternal love is an exit from
482. representation, a shield against death (177).
483. In "Women's Time" Kristeva discusses the
484. limitations of contemporary feminist movements to solve the problem of the political imbalance between the sexes. The first generation
485. of feminists in struggling for equality aspired
486. to gain a place in linear time as the time of
487. project and history. A difficulty with thisapproach, however, is that the increase of women
488. in positions of power has not radically
489. changed the nature of that power because
490. women in high positions become guardians of
491. the status quo. A second generation of feminists, according to Kristeva, focus on the specificity of female psychology and its symbolic
492. realizations and seek to give a language to the
493. intrasubjective realizations left mute by culture
494. in the past. The creation of a countersociety by
495. radical feminists, though, results in a kind of
496. inverted sexism. Kristeva calls for a third generation of feminism that includes the two
497. previous generations. This third generation
498. questions the dichotomy man/woman as an
499. opposition between two rival entities and attempts to replace scapegoating with the analysis of the potentialities of victim/executioner
500. that characterize each identity, each subject,
501. each sex.
502. In "Psychoanalysis and the Polis," Kristeva
503. sees that the interpretation of texts can involve
504. the domination of the text by the interpreter,
505. clearly an undesirable approach. A more desirable approach is to allow the text to speak to
506. the interpreter, thus permitting the constitution
507. of a new theory. Kristeva, however, suggests a
508. third alternative, a posthermeneutic one. This
509. involves a setting off of semantic, logical, phan-
510. tasmatic, and indeterminable sequences. Such
511. an approach, the method of psychoanalysis,
512. amounts to a return to the archaic mother who
513. is resistant to meaning.
514. Kristeva's work prepares the way for the
515. development of a poststructuralist or postmodern feminist rhetoric.

552. Kuhn, Thomas S. (b. 1923)
553. Historian of science who formulated the concept of paradigms. Thomas S. Kuhn is professor of philosophy and history of science at MIT.
554. His writings enormously influenced a variety of
555. disciplines such as sociology, political science,
556. economics, business management, and rhetoric.
557. Kuhn's most widely known book is TheStructure of Scientific Revolutions, first published in
558. 1962, which examines the making and changing of knowledge in scientific communities. He
559. is usually credited with popularizing the concept of paradigm. His work remains of broad
560. interest because of its discussion of how authorized knowledge changes and because of its
561. focus on the communities and the context in
562. which knowledge is created.
563. As a historian of science, Kuhn examines in detail the cultural setting of a scientist's
564. work. The Copernican Revolution (1957)
565. and the Black Body Theory (1978) focus on
566. the historical context in which Copernicus and
567. Planck developed their ideas. Kuhn argues in
568. these books that knowledge in science is an
569. integral part of the cultural constraints and
570. assumptions of an era. This claim stands against
571. the commonly held assumption that scientific
572. knowledge is based on an objective reality
573. which exists unaffected by opinions of a particular community of scholars.
574. The Structure of Scientific Revolutions describes the process of change in science. In
575. a "pre-paradigm" period of an immature science, competing theories coexist. As a science
576. matures, an approach will gain wide acceptance
577. because of its ability to solve problems, a "paradigm" will develop, and normal science will
578. proceed in an orderly fashion. However, when
579. too many anomalies, which cannot beaccounted for within a paradigm, are encountered, normal research will be interrupted. A
580. crisis will develop, and a scientific revolution
581. will occur. Kuhn argues that this change is not
582. evolutionary but revolutionary. "The transition
583. from a paradigm in crisis to a new one is areconstruction of the field from new fundamentals, a reconstruction that changes some of the
584. field's most elementary theoretical generalizations as well as many of its paradigm methods
585. and applications" (84–85). Further, the new
586. paradigm will be accepted only through persuasive argument that "converts" the professional
587. community (158).
588. Kuhn defined paradigm in a number of
589. different ways, and in the 1970 edition of The
590. Structure of Scientific Revolutions he added an
591. extensive postscript to clarify the term. "Paradigms are something shared by the members of
592. [groups that are] . . .the producers and
593. validators of scientific knowledge" (178). When
594. members of a community share a paradigm,
595. they share commitments, beliefs, values, and a
596. way of looking at the world which allows them
597. to then conduct "puzzle-solving research."
598. Because Kuhn explores the way knowledge
599. is created and interpreted, his work has been of
600. particular interest to rhetoricians. First, the
601. publication of Kuhn's work coincided with a
602. shift within the field of composition from afocus on the written product (often referred to as
603. the current-traditional approach) to an interest
604. in writing as a process. Rhetoricians found
605. Kuhn's description of changes in what counts as
606. knowledge in science applicable to what isoften called the "paradigm shift" in composition,
607. from product to process.
608. Second, Kuhn's description of how knowledge is generated and gains authority within
609. communities has been adopted and adapted by
610. many disciplines. For scholars interested in
611. rhetoric as epistemic, as a knowledge-making
612. activity, Kuhn's theories provide support because he argues that scientific knowledge is
613. based on the contingent judgment of specific
614. communities rather than on "objective reality."
615. His emphasis on community acceptance as key
616. to validation of scientific knowledge also supports rhetoricians interested in the social construction of knowledge.
617. While The Structure of Scientific Revolutions was initially received with skepticism and
618. even hostility, citation indexes in science, social
619. science, and arts and humanities demonstrate
620. the continuing importance of Kuhn's work.

643. Lamy, Bernard (1640–1715)
644. Orator, teacher of philosophy and theology, author of L'Art de parler. Fervent disciple of René
645. Descartes and friend of Nicolas Malebranche and
646. Port Royalists Antoine Arnauld and Pierre Nicole, Lamy devoted his life to teaching and writing texts for student use. Lamy taught at the seminary of Grenoble and at Rouen and was exiled
647. twice during his career because of his Cartesian
648. sympathies. Among other works, he published
649. Entretiens sur lessciences (1683), Les Eléments
650. de géométrie (1685), and Traité de perspective
651. (1701), but it is L'Art déparier (1675) for which
652. he is best known. Named to mirror the Port Royalists' L'Art de penser, Lamy's book went through
653. five editions and nine printings, becoming enlarged as Lamy refined his rhetorical theory during the last twenty years of his life. The Art of
654. Speaking, also known as the Port Royal rhetoric,
655. was first published in translation in England in
656. 1676 and reprinted in 1696 and 1708.
657. A marked departure from neoclassical rhetorics of the late seventeenth century, L'Art de parler
658. was intended as a "scientific" (that is, rationalist)
659. account of how discourse works upon the mind.
660. Part I dealt with phonetics and grammar. Following Descartes's injunction to begin with the simplest elements of one's subject and progress by degrees to the more complex, this section described
661. the formation of words, the structure of sentences,
662. and standards for correct language usage.
663. Part II, on tropes and figures, emphasized
664. the idea that the orator's form of expression
665. should be proportioned to the idea conveyed. For
666. Lamy the tropes and figures were especially significant because they acted as discursive "triggers," exciting desired emotional responses in the
667. listener. Like Descartes, Lamy believed that a
668. listener's passions could be aroused directly by
669. sensible impressions and vivid description. In this
670. section Lamy provided definitions, explanations,
671. and examples for each trope and figure.
672. Part III discussed the acoustical properties
673. and periodic structure of speech; Part IV
674. considered the relation of style to the situation
675. and genre of expression. In considering historical, philosophical, and poetic styles along with
676. oratory, Lamy anticipated belletrism's concern
677. for taste and good composition across genres.
678. The fifth and last part ofL'Art de parler treated
679. persuasion explicitly. Lamy disparaged the commonplaces as useless and insisted on demonstrative, syllogistic proofs as the only adequate
680. logical form. He also encouraged use of craft,
681. or l'adresse, to gain acceptance for one's views
682. by emphasizing common ground.
683. Many attributes of Lamy's L'Art de parler,
684. taken together, make it unique. It systematically considered the acoustical, phonetic, and
685. periodic properties of speech. It relied extensively on Descartes's psychology in developing
686. an account of how rhetorical appeals acted on
687. the mind. And it expanded consideration of
688. style to the arts of writing in history, philosophy, and literature. In these aspects and others,
689. L'Art de parlerturned its back on neoclassi-
690. cism and presaged the major Enlightenment
691. rhetorics.
