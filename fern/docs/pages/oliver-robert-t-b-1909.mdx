## Definition & Etymology

<div className="flex items-center mb-8">

<div className="prose prose-lg max-w-none leading-relaxed bg-amber-50 border-amber-200 border-l-4 pl-6 py-2 pr-4 rounded-r-lg">

Innovative scholar-adventurer whose academic
interests span four broad themes: basic communication skills, the history of public speaking in
Britain and America, Asian rhetoric, and rhetoric and culture. His investigations have ranged
from the power of symbolism to the psychology
of persuasion, from conversation to the role
communication plays in a healthy personality.
He was the first American speech scholar to
publish essays on the speech of international
relations and on Asian rhetoric.
In the past half-century, Oliver has produced over fifty books and literally hundreds of
articles. For over twenty years he headed the
department of speech communication at Penn
State. For eighteen years he served as counselor,
publicist, and speech writer for the Republic of
South Korea. He was elected president of both
his regional and national professional associations. He is a perceptive rhetorical critic and an
experienced journal editor. In short, Robert
Oliver is a person of boundless energy, insatiable curiosity, and remarkable perseverance.
Oliver writes in a fresh, lively style. His
forte is synthesis and structure: He possesses the
ability to absorb large bodies of material and to
select from that data the vivid detail that graphically tells a story. His expertise lies in breadth
of conception rather than originality, macroscopic overviews rather than microscopic insights. For decades he has been recognized as a
scholar willing to tackle challenging assignments no one else would dare undertake.
One key to understanding Oliver is to understand his practical involvement in the speech
of international relations. His is not the view of
the armchair theorist, isolated from the scene of
diplomatic action. Rather, he is one who has
himself struggled for years to establish crosscultural understanding and has closely observed
diplomatic negotiation at the UN and elsewhere.
Nations do not always tell the truth to one
another, Oliver notes; sometimes they rationalize, presenting socially acceptable reasons to
mask the real reasons for their behavior. As
early as 1942, in the first college textbook ever
published on the psychology of persuasive
speech, Oliver argued that there are three avenues of motivation available to the advocate:
rational appeal, emotional appeal, and "a process of pseudo-reason . . .termed rationalization." While many of his fellow academics
scoffed at the inclusion of rationalization in this
trilogy, Oliver himself had no doubt about its
efficacy; he remembred all too well Neville
Chamberlain's "peace in our time."
A quite different phase of Oliver's rhetorical travels involves his investigation of Asian
rhetoric. As one reviewer wrote of his Communication and Culture in Ancient India and
China, what the book clearly demonstrates is
"that rhetoric in one culture is different than
rhetoric in other cultures." Oliver's study of
Asian rhetorics, the reviewer continued, should
"help Western rhetoric shake off its provinciality." No mean achievement that.
A stimulating speaker as well as a prolific
publisher, Robert Oliver reminds one of a
dauntless traveler ever interested in exploring
new ideas and new cultures.
Goodwin Berquist
Dartmouth College

113. Orality
114. In the "Great Leap" or "Great Divide" models
115. of literacy articulated by Eric Havelock, Jack
116. Goody, Walter Ong, and David Olson, a theoretical construct in binary opposition withliteracy; as used by Ong, orality is often associated
117. with Havelock's preliteracy and Olson'sutterance. Great Leap theories of literacy hold that
118. the alphabetic literacy of Western languages is
119. a causal factor in the development of abstract,
120. logical thinking. Cognition, language use, consciousness, and culture in oral societies are typified as different from, usually opposite, those in
121. literate societies. While the Havelock-Ong depiction of literacy and orality has been exploited
122. by many historians of rhetoric, it has proved
123. more controversial in composition.
124. Though a concern with literacy and orality
125. runs though the body of Ong's work in rhetoric
126. and literature, the definitive account of orality
127. occurs in his 1982 Orality and Literacy. Here,
128. defining primary oral cultures as "cultures with
129. no knowledge at all of writing" (1), Ong says
130. that the memory of primary orality requires that
131. valuable information be set in a narrative form
132. with either formulaic or poetic surface features.
133. Like primary orality, secondary orality—the
134. orality of the electronic media—is communal,
135. participatory, and formulaic, but unlike primary
136. orality, it is orality based on the written word
137. (136). The informality and spontaneity of secondary orality are self-consciously planned, as
138. are the texts on which it is based (136-37).Residual orality is Ong's term to describe pockets
139. of orality that exist "in dominantly high literacy
140. societies, such as urban black subcultures or
141. Chicano subcultures in the United States" (160).
142. In their strong versions, Great Leap or Great
143. Divide theories assert that simply reading and
144. writing with a Greek-derived alphabet actually
145. causes fundamental advances—great developmental leaps—in human cognition, and that
146. these cognitive leaps then bring about alterations
147. in not only the consciousness of individuals but
148. also in cultures. These models typically present
149. orality and literacy as either a dichotomy or a
150. single continuum contrasting the modes of
151. speech, composition, behavior, and thought of
152. orality—that is, of both oral cultures and oral
153. persons—with those of literacy—that is, of both
154. literate cultures and literate persons. The prototype of an oral culture is held to be Havelock's
155. description of orality in ancient Greece, though
156. in the scholarship, orality is often equated with
157. informal conversation (Elbow). The prototype of
158. literacy is advanced academic literacy in modern
159. industrialized Western countries—that is, the
160. ability to read critically advanced, extended texts
161. and to produce theoretical expository prose,
162. abilities putatively leading to "higher" states of
163. consciousness.
164. Characteristics of Orality
165. For Ong, because orality perceives holistically,
166. both its thought and language are said to be
167. additive, aggregative, redundant, conservative,
168. empathetic, participatory, and situational (37-
169. 50). Because literacy sees parts and relationships, both its thought and language are described as subordinate, analytic, exploratory,
170. objective, distanced, and abstract (37-50). Oral
171. language therefore relies upon juxtaposition
172. and coordination—that is, parataxis—while
173. literacy employs subordination—that is,
174. hypotaxis (37-38).
175. According to Ong, oral cultures and oral
176. persons differ from literate cultures and persons
177. in their use of surface language, in attitude and
178. behavior, in elements of the narrative, in views
179. of history, in concepts of self, and in epistemol-
180. ogy. In addition to its use of paratactic structures, oral language is distinguished from literate language by surface features that generally
181. serve mnemonic functions—rhythm, alliteration and assonance, repetition, and balanced
182. patterns like parallelism and antithesis (34).
183. Oral cultures also rely on forms that have
184. clearly marked surface structures—proverbs,
185. riddles, and epithets. Oral narrative is also
186. highly redundant (24, 38).
187. According to Ong, oral cultures see language as "a mode of action," as "power," as
188. "magical" (32). For him, oral cultures are "ago-
189. nistically programmed" (44), engaged in
190. "triumphalism" (49). This mindset shows up,
191. Ong says, in verbal contests in contemporary
192. cultures that have a high degree of "oral residue"—for example, in the "dozens" in the
193. black communities in the U.S. (44). In Ong's
194. depiction, oral cultures celebrate physical behavior, and their narratives, he says, are marked
195. by the "portrayal of gross physical violence"
196. (44). The characters of oral narratives are
197. "heavy" and stereotyped—Ong uses Nestor
198. and Brer Rabbit to exemplify—not subtle and
199. psychologically complex like those of modern,
200. literate fiction (150).
201. Oral cultures begin their narratives "in
202. medias res" because, according to Ong, without writing they have "absolutely no way to
203. organize them in strict chronological order"
204. (143). Goody and Watt argue that because narratives in oral cultures serve to justify present
205. social structures, oral societies employ structural amnesia in order to change the story to
206. support present conditions. Ong believes that
207. because oral cultures lack "a sense of difference
208. between past and future" (30), in orality there
209. is no real history.
210. According to Great Leap models, people in
211. oral cultures cannot separate themselves from
212. the group and therefore do not regard themselves as individuals, but rather define themselves according to the roles and expectations
213. of the society of which they are a part. The oral
214. personality is "more communal and externalized, and less introspective" than the literate
215. personality, Ong writes (69). In oral cultures,
216. according to Ong, "knowing means achieving
217. empathetic, close, communal identification
218. o
219. with the known, 'getting with it'" (45-46); participation, not analysis, is the basis of knowledge in oral cultures, Ong says. Oral cultures,
220. he argues, have "a highly traditionalist or conservative set of mind that with good reason inhibits intellectual experimentation" (41).
221. Some compositionists argue that the oral
222. side of the Great Leap dichotomy or continuum
223. is useful in describing the thought patterns and
224. the language, particularly the written language,
225. of freshman English students and minorities in
226. American colleges and universities (Farrell;
227. Lazerre). According to Ong, "Our deeper understanding of the psychodynamics of orality in
228. relation to the psychodynamics of writing is
229. improving the teaching of writing skills, particularly in cultures today moving rapidly from

233. others in composition argue that such claims
234. and the proposals built on them are pedagogi-
235. cally and logically untenable and can be, like
236. Farrell's "IQ and Standard English" article,
237. both psychologically and educationally damaging (Greenberg et al.; Rose; Walters).
238. The Theory
239. According to Havelock, in preliterate Greece the
240. knowledge and values necessary for the survival
241. of the culture were transmitted through poetry,
242. particularly Homer's. The content was tradition,
243. the language formulaic. Using oral formulas, the
244. poet, or bard, constructed the poem itself in a
245. public oral performance in which both bard and
246. audience entered into an almost trancelike, or
247. "mimetic," state (Havelock 176). This mimetic
248. relationship prevented the bard from introducing new ideas and the audience from questioning or analyzing. When about the time of Plato
249. literacy became internalized, mental "energy"
250. previously needed for the memorization of the
251. content of the poetry was released for other sorts
252. of cognitive activity. With this new energy, literate persons began to compare texts and note
253. inconsistencies, synthesize conflicting opinions,
254. and question the authority of the oral tradition.
255. As Havelock explains it, Plato's dialectic, a product of these literate modes of thought, was a way
256. of breaking the mimetic spell in which learning
257. had hitherto occurred and thus a method of forcing the audience to question and analyze received
258. information (208-9). Goody and Watt assert
259. that Aristotle and the next generation of Greek
260. thinkers used these new analytical, logical modes
261. of thought and the concomitant inquiring attitude to develop systematized abstract thinking;
262. they cite as evidence the syllogism, the categories,
263. and the taxonomies in various fields of study.
264. After the fall of Rome, these literate patterns of thinking were maintained by those
265. possessing what Ong calls "Learned Latin"
266. (112-15). Used almost exclusively by men for
267. academic debate, Learned Latin augmented
268. objective, abstract thought and moved knowledge further into the realm of the theoretical
269. and away from daily life. Next, the invention of
270. the printing press spread both these skills and
271. the consequences of literacy. The technology of
272. print made literacy accessible to more people,
273. releasing more mental energy for abstract thinking in many new fields. Western culture underwent a recapitulation and an extension of the
274. Great Leap. The climax, according to Olson,
275. was the seventeenth-century British essay,
276. which was used by thinkers like John Locke to
277. explore abstract problems and to create new
278. theoretical knowledge (262-69).
279. Criticisms
280. The Havelock-Ong Great Leap theory of literacy, along with its orality construct, has been
281. disputed by researchers in a number of fields.
282. Halverson argues, for example, that Havelock's
283. reading of Homer, the basis of the Great Leap
284. depiction of primary oral cultures, is essentially
285. inaccurate. Street refutes Goody's argument by
286. pointing to flaws in both research and logic.
287. Heath's ethnographic work in the Piedmont
288. Carolinas shows that oral language in an African-American community that could be classified as "residually oral" certainly can have far
289. more complex syntax than literate language
290. with the same message, speaker, and audience.
291. Indeed, anthropologists and linguists working
292. in a number of cultural contexts present empirical evidence that calls into question several key
293. assertions of the Great Leap theory, especially
294. those pertaining to orality. Some of this research
295. is briefly summarized in Daniell's PRE/TEXT
296. article; papers by Akinnaso, Chafe, Scribner
297. and Cole, Heath, Lakoff, and Tannen, among
298. others, can be found in edited volumes by
299. Frawley; Tannen; and Kintgen, Kroll, and Rose.
300. Most scholars of language use—whether they
301. study other cultures, ethnic subcultures in the
302. U.S., or middle-class, literate Americans—believe that the written and spoken discourse of
303. actual persons is too complicated, too complex,
304. too rich in meaning and in rhetoric for an oral-
305. literate continuum or dichotomy to have much
306. valid explanatory power.
307. Other critics charge the Great Leap theory
308. with ethnocentrism. Walters argues that
309. the Great Leap's claim that alphabetic literacy
310. causes its users to think more logically than
311. people in cultures without alphabetic literacy or
312. without any writing system is "an idea that
313. many Westerners find appealing, no doubt because it 'explains' what they perceive to be the
314. superiority of Western culture" (175). Giving
315. substance to this charge is the fact that Ong
316. does not count writing systems such as "cuneiform, Chinese characters, Mayan script, and the
317. Japanese syllabary, and so forth" (3) as real literacy; others assert that such systems are unable
318. to bestow the cognitive benefits of reading and
319. writing (see Farrell's reply in Greenberg et al.
320. 474-76). Ong believes that his term oral is "less
321. invidious and more positive" than terms formerly applied to traditional societies, such as
322. savage, primitive, and inferior (174), despite
323. deconstructionists' assertions that because in a
324. binary opposition one side is always valorized
325. and the other degraded, literacy becomes the
326. privileged term and orality comes to be associated with the pejoratives.
327. Other critiques argue that claims of"residual oral culture" are an inadequate explanation for the school failure of inner-city students,
328. since this account omits social, political, and
329. economic conditions (Ogbu; Greenberg et al.).
330. Both compositionists and linguists point out
331. that assertions about the paratactic, oral language of African-Americans ignores linguistic
332. facts (Walters, esp. note 6,184-85; Hartwell in
333. Greenberg et al.). Marxist critics see changes in
334. education in general and in writing instruction
335. in particular in the nineteenth century not as
336. part of the shift from orality to literacy, as Ong
337. claims, but as a part of the shift from an agricultural economy to industrial capitalism (Berlin; Douglas). Many feminists point to what
338. they see as the essentialism of Ong's association
339. of orality with women: his identification of
340. orality with "mother tongues" (113-15) and
341. breast feeding (Interfaces 22-27) and his depiction of orality as focusing on emotion, immediacy, and practicality (but cf. Swearingen).
342. Some cultural critics point out that many
343. characteristics in Ong's portrayal of orality have
344. been typically listed in academic research as
345. aspects of the language, thought, and behavior
346. of relatively powerless groups—women, blacks,
347. other ethnic minorities, and the poor (Ehren-
348. reich; Tronto). Others argue that like other developmental theories, the Great Leap model
349. puts its researchers' own values, experiences,
350. and abilities at the top of the hierarchy. Still
351. others criticize the Great Leap theory for its
352. assumption of an isomorphic relationship between thought and language and for its application of historical, cultural, and literary theories to actual individuals and groups.
