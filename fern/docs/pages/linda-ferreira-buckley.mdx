34. Nomos
35. A term having two predominant meanings
36. in ancient Greek rhetorical and philosophical
37. texts: first, "custom" (the way things are always
38. done in specific contexts); second, "law" (the
39. way things ought to be done in specific contexts). Though distinguished here for purposes
40. of definition, most classical Greek thinkers recognized the intimate relationship between these
41. two senses of nomos. Laws, they argued, cannot be successful unless supported by customs,
42. and customs fade unless reinforced by laws.
43. In its earliest (pre-Socratic/sophistic) and
44. most common usage, nomos is usually translated as "custom," "norm," or "convention."
45. In this sense, nomos describes activities done
46. according to tradition or through habitual practice. The sophists viewed nomos as one of the
47. keys to rhetorical success. The anonymous author of the sophistic text Dissoi Logoi argues,
48. for example, that any practitioner of the art of
49. discourse (logôn technê) must know all customs
50. and conventions (nomoi), in addition to having
51. skill in the technical aspects of the art (technê).
52. The success of any discourse, in other words, is
53. relative to the degree to which that discourse
54. considers the cultural values, customs, and conventions of its audience.
55. In its second sense (especially in Plato and
56. Aristotle), nomos is often translated as "law,"
57. "ordinance," or "statute." Nomos (and its plural form nomoi), in this more prescriptive sense,
58. refers to laws derived through consensus within
59. a cultural setting, as in the laws of Sparta or
60. Athens. Later Greek texts occasionally precede
61. nomos with an article, thus attributing laws to
62. a single origin, as in "the laws of nature" or
63. "the law of Moses."
64. A common debate in fifth and fourth
65. century B.C.E. Greek rhetoric and philosophy
66. considered whether culture (nomos) or nature
67. (phusis) most profoundly influenced orators'
68. skills in the pragmatic uses of language. The
69. earliest manifestations of this debate, common
70. in pre-Socratic and sophistic fragments, consid-

73. communal consensus (via nomos) or are inscribed in nature (phusis) and therein discovered. In later Greek rhetorical texts, the notnosl

75. is acquired through formal instruction in the

101. privileging of fiction over nonfiction. The first
102. involves Plato's and Aristotle's respective takes
103. on rhetorical invention. Plato posited that the
104. search for ideas (for him synonymous with
105. truth) was internal and, to an extent, in-
106. tersubjective, while Aristotle opted for external
107. aids to invention, exploring probability instead
108. of certainty. Thus, as Winterowd suggests, Plato
109. and Aristotle became the progenitors of "creative" and factual literature respectively, Plato

111. Aristotle on observation and discovery (1993:

114. them Hugh Blair, Ralph Waldo Emerson, and
115. Samuel Taylor Coleridge. More than Campbell

117. associated with Blair), Blair stressed the written
118. dimension of rhetoric over the spoken. But his
119. focus on "belles-lettres" (fine writing) as well as
120. the aesthetic effects of such writing was used to
121. justify the exaltation of imaginative literature

223. Ogden, Charles Kay (1889-1957)
224. Born at Fleetwood, England, on June 1, 1889,
225. entered Cambridge on a Classical scholarship in
226. 1908, making the influence of language upon
227. thought in ancient Greece his field of specialization. Ogden developed and applied this theme
228. in his writing throughout his career.
229. In 1912 he launched the CambridgeMagazine, which originally emphasized philosophy,
230. psychology, aesthetics, religion, and later language. Ogden's best-known book (with LA.
231. Richards), The Meaning of Meaning, appeared
232. first as a series of articles in the Cambridge Magazine between 1921 and 1923. Ogden also founded Psyche, a journal of general and linguistic
233. psychology, which he edited from 1923 to 1952.
234. The Meaning of Meaning remains in print
235. today. Its objectives are to create awareness of the
236. misleading power of words, to formalize the study
237. of words as signs of thought, and to provide strategies for avoiding verbal confusion. At the core of
238. the book are a model of meaning as the relationship between symbol (that is, a sign), thought, and
239. what thought is of (referent), as well as six ordered
240. rules, called the canons of symbolism, which
241. reconfigure classical logic. The canons lead to a
242. method for giving definitions of words by a limited set of relations (definition routes) in which a
243. sought referent stands to a known referent.
244. The writings of Jeremy Bentham (1748-
245. 1832) inspired Ogden to develop the notion
246. of definition routes into a method for determining a small number of words that
247. can easily express many others. At the same
248. time, he limited the use of the vast verb system of English to just eighteen functioning
249. as operators (the words operating the others
250. in a sentence) to produce the 850-word system
251. he called Basic English.
252. Bentham's work not only molded Ogden's
253. views but became the subject of his scholarship:
254. He revised Richard Hildreth's 1864 translation
255. of Bentham's Traité de législation civile et pénale,
256. wrote the definitive version of Bentham's notion
257. of linguistic fictions (concrete qualities attributed
258. to a word's referent where none exists), and published many articles dealing with Bentham's ideas
259. about education, extinct languages, grammatical
260. symmetry, translation, metaphor, and so on.
261. Ogden translated fifteen books from French
262. and German, covering fields as diverse as
263. paroptics and politics, parapsychology and philosophy. These included the first English translation of Wittgenstein's Tractatus Logico-
264. Fhilosophicus. He also edited hundreds of books
265. in the International Library of Philosophy, Psychology, and Scientific Method. He died of cancer at the London Clinic, March 21, 1957.
