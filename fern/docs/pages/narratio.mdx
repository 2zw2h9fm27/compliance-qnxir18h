## Definition & Etymology

<div className="flex items-center mb-8">
  <Lightbulb className="w-6 h-6 text-blue-600 mr-3" />
</div>

<div className="prose prose-lg max-w-none leading-relaxed bg-blue-50 border-blue-200 border-l-4 pl-6 py-2 pr-4 rounded-r-lg">

The second component of classical argument, which conveys statements of fact furnishing either background information or context for
the case being argued. Narratio's translation,
definition, and emphasis have changed markedly
since the classical age. Originally placed following the exordium and preceding the propositio, narratio chiefly conveys facts. These facts
buttress the orator's credibility (a concern of
exordium) and provide an argumentativescaffold based upon history, precedent, or tradition.
This scaffold becomes the basis for a statement
of position or thesis conveyed in the propositio.
Many theorists, classical and contemporary, thus
define narratio's function as providing "background information . . . and the circumstances
important to the argument" (Woodson 38).
Greek rhetoricians treat narratio somewhat
passingly; it comes to prominence under the aegis
of Cicero and Quintilian. H.M. Hubbell, translator of De inventione, translates the term as "narration," thus perhaps leading to some of the confusion over narratio's definition and discourse
function. Cicero discusses many of the types of
narration/narratio in De inventione, beginning
with the following definition: "an exposition of
events that have occurred or are supposed to have
occurred" (1.19.27). Cicero also lists three types
of narrative. The first type focuses on "the case
second type contains "a digression . . . for the purpose of attacking somebody,. . . making a comparison, . . . amusing the audience,... or for amplification" (1.19.27). The last type of narrative
serves a different end—"amusement and training"—and it can concern either events or persons
(1.19.27). Cicero identifies three subtypes of narration concerning events: Fabula, which uses fictional events; Historia, based upon historical precedents; and Argumentum, narrative based upon
fictional events that could happen (1.19.27).
In addition to cataloguing types of narrative
and their respective characteristics, Cicero and,
later, Quintilian identify and discuss three crucial
traits of effective narrative: brevity, clarity, and
plausibility. These characteristics suggest a sense
of audience awareness, for they are all cautions
to the rhetor against using examples that are too
extensive, obscure, or incredible lest the audience
become alienated. These characteristics facilitate
narratio's appearances in deliberative and ceremonial discourse, as well as forensic discourse.
Today, narratio seems secondary to the concepts of narrative or narration, and many theorists have debated the virtues and characteristics
of this element of discourse. The concept of narration is particularly significant in the work of
Kenneth Burke, whose theories concerning dramatism contribute much to revisions of rhetorical thought. Echoing Cicero in De oratore, narration now functions as "the fountainhead from
which the whole remainder of speech flows"
(2.81.330) and becomes a crucial part of discourse because of its capacity for "applying principles to situations" (O'Banion 66).

Northwestern State University

25. Narrative Theory
26. Began with Aristotle, who in his Poetics discussed formal elements not just of tragedy, but
27. also of epic (plot, character, thought, and diction;
28. mimesis and diegesis) that remain concerns of
29. narrative theory today. From the fourth century
30. B.C.E. until the twentieth century, more attention
31. was given to drama and poetry (with poetryoften referring to literature) than to narrative as a
32. distinct mode, but after the emergence of the
33. English novel in the eighteenth century, writers
34. such as Henry Fielding, Walter Scott, Nathaniel
35. Hawthorne, Edgar Allan Poe, George Eliot, and
36. others discussed the nature and significance of
37. this new form, its relations to epic, to romance,
38. and to the moral functions of literature.
39. In the first half of the twentieth century, Henry James and E.M. Forster turned the discussion
40. to matters of novelistic form, withJames focusing
41. especially on issues of narration and Forster on
42. the distinction between plot and story and the
43. uneasy tension between character and plot. In the
44. 1960s, narrative theory moved into a new, international phase as the work of the Russian formalists of the 1920s and the contemporary French
45. structuralists was translated and disseminated
46. among Anglo-American theorists, while the work
47. of the Anglo-Americans was translated and disseminated in Europe. Since the 1960s, narrative
48. has received the kind of sustained, sophisticated
49. attention that had previously been reserved for
50. poetry, and narrative theory is now a rich, diverse,
51. and flourishing field.
52. If we define narrative as a mode of discourse
53. in which at least one narrator recounts to at least
54. one narratee at least two temporally related
55. events, then we can understand the project of
56. narrative theory as the effort to understand the
57. various elements of the mode—narrator, narration, narratee, event, setting, character, plot, and
58. language—the relation of these elements to each
59. other, and narrative's overall nature and significance. Sometimes the term narratology is used
60. synonymously with narrative theory, but here we
61. shall use it to refer more specifically to the formalist-structuralist account of narrative, and we
62. shall use narrative theory to refer to the intersections of narrative studies with critical theory
63. more generally—with, that is, not only formalism and structuralism, but also such movements
64. as Marxism, psychoanalysis, feminism, decon-
65. struction, reader-response criticism, and the various historicisms and cultural studies.
66. Narratology then, studies "the nature, form,
67. and functioning of narrative" and "examines
68. what all and only narratives have in common . . .
69. as well as what enables them to be different from
70. one another" (Prince). More particularly, the formalist-structuralist model distinguishes among
71. story (what happens, in its "natural chronological order"; what the Russian formalists called the
72. fabula), plot (what happens as it is presented to
73. the audience; what the formalists called sjuzhet),
74. and discourse (how the event is conveyed; the
75. narration). Narratological work on narrative
76. structure ranges from Vladimir Propp's Morphology of the Folktale, which identified an invariable
77. sequence of twenty-nine events occurring in all
78. Russian folktales, to Roland Barthes's "Introduction to the Structural Analysis of Narrative,"
79. which distinguished between kernel events (the
80. essential events of a sequence) and satellite events
81. (those that embellish a kernel).
82. In the Anglo-American tradition, the key
83. early texts followed James's lead and emphasized matters of technique or "treatment," gradually codifying a preference for what was considered an "objective" narration rooted in the
84. center of one consciousness—as practiced by
85. James and theorized by Percy Lubbock. In challenging this dominant view, Wayne C. Booth's
86. Rhetoric of Fiction also proposes a general view
87. of narrative as rhetoric and explores the variety of techniques authors may use to persuade
88. their audiences to adopt certain views of characters and events. In addition, Booth's book
89. introduces the widely adopted concepts ofimplied author, the self—distinct from the narrator—whom a writer conveys in constructing a
90. text; reliable narrator, who speaks in accord
91. with the norms of the implied author; and un-
92. reliable narrator, who diverges from the implied
93. author's norms.
94. Gérard Genette advances narratology's investigation into point of view by separating two
95. related but different questions, the question of
96. who sees (a question of vision), and that of who
97. speaks (a question of voice). Genette further unpacks the complexity of narrative discourse by
98. developing a set of categories that addresses its
99. manipulation of time and its relations between a
100. narrator and what is narrated. Order refers to the
101. relation between events in the story and events in
102. the text; under this heading Genette includes flashbacks, flashforwards, and other deviations from
103. straight chronology. Duration refers to the relation between story time and discourse time; two
104. years of story time may be narrated in a single
105. phrase while five minutes of story time may be
106. narrated for hours upon hours. Frequency refers
107. to the relation between repetition in story time and
108. repetition in discourse time; a repeated action may
109. be mentioned only once while a unique event may
110. be returned to again and again in the narration of
111. it. Genette points out that distinguishing between
112. first-person and third-person narration is misleading because all narrators are first-person speakers.
113. He proposes, instead, that we distinguish between
114. heterodiegetic and homodiegetic narration, between, that is, narration in which the narrator and
115. the characters occupy different narrative levels or
116. spaces (as in, for example, Tom Jones or Beloved),
117. where the narrators are not characters in the
118. events they portray, and narration in which the
119. narrator and characters are on the same level or
120. in the same narrative world (as, for example, in
121. Jane Eyre and The Invisible Man).
122. In Story and Discourse, Seymour Chatman
123. synthesizes the work of Genette, Barthes, Booth,
124. and others from the French and Anglo-American
125. traditions, dividing his study into the two parts
126. identified in the title and discussing, under "Story"
127. event, character, and setting and, under
128. "Discourse," varieties of points of view. More
129. generally, Chatman presents what has come to be
130. known as the communication model of narrative
131. analysis. In this linear model, an author creates an
132. implied author who then employs a narrator to
133. address a narratee. As the chain continues, the
134. narratee's understanding is reconfigured by the
135. implied reader (the implied author's hypothetical
136. addressee), and the implied reader's understanding is similarly reconfigured by the real reader.
137. While researchers in the 1980s and 1990s
138. generally acknowledge the usefulness of the
139. narratological communication model, they have
140. also become increasingly aware of its limitations. Deconstructionist critics such as Paul de
141. Man and J. Hillis Miller would emphasize both
142. the necessity to employ its categories and the inability of the communication to be effected as
143. the model predicts. Arguing that the formalist-
144. structuralist model renders narrative spatial and
145. static rather than temporal and dynamic, Peter
146. Brooks proposes in Reading for the Plot that
147. narrative theory attend to the psychic energy of
148. narrative dynamics, the way beginnings generate desires, middles involve a detour on the
149. route to fulfillment, a fulfillment finally delivered (or at least offered) in endings, which can
150. be seen as having determined the whole sequence. But Susan Winnett and others note that
151. Brooks assumes that male sexual desire is the
152. norm for narrative dynamics and, thus, once
153. again implicitly equates male with universal.
154. More generally, the narratological model
155. does not have much to say explicitly about ideology, difference, or culture, though it does not preclude its application to these matters. Peter J.
156. Rabinowitz's Before Reading extends the model
157. by uncovering the conventions of reading that
158. influence narrative communication in any one
159. work and by showing how the operation of these
160. conventions intersects with the politics of interpretation to influence such larger cultural matters as
161. canon formation. James Phelan proposes a way
162. of thinking about character as a combination of
163. mimetic, thematic, and synthetic components
164. (that is, as person, idea, and plot function), suggests that the relation of these components is determined by progression, and investigates the relation between understanding progressions and
165. resisting them. The translation of M.M. Bakhtin's
166. work in the early 1980s gave theorists a way to
167. link language and ideology and in so doing added
168. yet another layer to the understanding of narrative discourse. Any national language, says Bakh-
169. tin, is better thought of as a conglomeration of
170. mini-languages (for example, the language of the
171. marketplace, of the academy, of religion, of politics), each carrying its own ideological values, and
172. the novel is the genre that most dramatically
173. shows these languages and values colliding, contrasting, and challenging each other.
174. The move of narrative theory toward ideology has been given further emphasis by the work
175. of Marxists and feminists of the last two decades.
176. Fredric Jameson's Political Unconscious popularizes the dictum "Always historicize!" and argues
177. that critics need to search for the link between a
178. narrative's form and its often unexpressed or un-
179. conscious political agenda. Feminists consistently
180. search for the connections among gender, ideology, and power in narrative and culture. Elaine
181. Showalter, Sandra Gilbert, Susan Gubar, Nancy
182. Miller, Rachel Blau DuPlessis, and others have
183. studied the development of the female narrative
184. tradition as it has developed within a dominant
185. patriarchal culture. Some feminists, such as
186. Judith Fetterley and Patrocinio Schweikart, have
187. focused on the situation of the female reader. Still
188. others have employed the insights of other theorists as part of their investigations of gender and
189. power. To mention just one from a long list,
190. Nancy Armstrong adapts Foucault's ideas of discursive formations to rewrite the origins of the
191. English novel.
192. Feminist criticism has also made significant
193. contributions to film theory, especially in combination with structuralism and psychoanalysis.
194. Laura Mulvey's landmark 1975 essay "Visual
195. Pleasure and Narrative Cinema" transformed film
196. theory by calling attention to the way Hollywood
197. cinema reinforces patriarchal ideology in the way
198. that it assumes a male spectator and directs his gaze
199. toward an objectified woman. Since Mulvey's essay, film theory has continued to explore the dynamics of the gaze; the phenomenon called suture,
200. the process by which the spectator identifies with
201. the film image; the effects of various techniques
202. such as the shot/reverse shot; and in general the
203. relations among form, gender, and ideology.
204. The two most prominent recent developments in narrative theory are its inflections by the
205. work in new historicism and in multiculturalism.
206. Since the new historicism regards literature as part
207. of a continuous network of cultural texts, it shifts
208. narrative theory's attention to the cultural work
209. performed by narrative. To take just one example,
210. D.A. Miller looks at Victorian fiction through the
211. lenses provided by the new historicism and Foucault's Discipline and Punish and argues that the
212. Victorian novel performed a very significant policing function on its readers: In reading Bleak
213. House, the Victorian reader internalized culturally
214. sanctioned ways of thinking and behaving.
215. Work in multiculturalism is now influencing narrative theory in much the same way that
216. it had earlier been influenced by feminism. Henry
217. Louis Gates, Jr., demonstrates how the trope of
218. signifying, so common to African-American oral
219. culture, provides a way to understand the development of the African-American cultural tradition. Gloria Anzaldúa's work on what it means
220. to live and write on the borders of different cultures (figuratively and literally) shows how real
221. readers and writers (as opposed to the abstract,
222. ideal constructs of narratology) have multiple
223. and shifting relations to narrative texts. Theorists
224. of other ethnic literatures and of gay and lesbian
225. literatures also emphasize the difference that difference makes in all elements of narrative, at all
226. points of the communication model—and in the
227. way narrative theory is conducted.
228. The recent interactions of multicultural and
229. narrative studies have, in a sense, reinforced many
230. of the contributions folklore studies have made to
231. narrative theory. Work ranging from Propp's analysis of Russian folktales (discussed above) to
232. Dundes's structuralist and anthropological
233. approach to folklore has been instrumental in
234. guiding the study of folk narrative as a cross-cultural phenomenon. Barbara Babcock's work on
235. metanarration in folk narrative debunks the myth
236. of "primitive narrative," the idea that oral or folk
237. narrative is "simple, natural, and direct, . . .
238. [u]ncomplicated by . . . self-commentary," a debunking central to the validation of various narratives beyond those of the traditional canon (63).
239. Studies of folk narrative also intersect with literary criticism in the realm of new historicism and
240. ideological criticism. Richard Bauman's work on
241. the performance aspect of verbal art, especially the
242. nature and codes of verbal performance in storytelling, helps us understand not only the representation of performance in written narrative but also
243. the performative dimension of all narrative. In collaborating with Charles Briggs, Bauman also argues that theorists and critics must interpret the
244. performance context but then conduct a reflexive
245. ethnography, which considers the political and
246. ideological influences on both the narrative and
247. the folklorist.
248. As we hope this narrative shows, narrative
249. theory is indebted to a variety of disciplines and
250. movements, which are, in turn, indebted to it.
251. Perhaps this interaction shows, too, the ubiquity
252. and diversity of narrative, that it exists in numerous, intersecting forms, in everything from the
253. shortest jokes and ballads to the longest films
254. and novels. Because narrative is as common in
255. everyday conversation as it is in formal and informal writing, because theorists are increasingly
256. aware of the importance of the mode, and because it is such a vital component of our lives, we
257. expect narrative theory to continue to flourish as
258. long as people continue to tell and listen to stories—which is to say as long as there are people,
259. language, and social interaction.
