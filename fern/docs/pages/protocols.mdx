A conceptual framework and vocabulary for
talking about composing and establishing an
agenda for further research that has examined
the ways in which acts of cognition mediate
different rhetorical contexts for writing. Studies in composition have shifted during the last
twenty years, from analyzing written texts to
understanding how texts are constructed. Renewed interest in rhetorical invention, planning,
and revision has brought with it an important
research method, protocol analysis. Researchers have used think-aloud protocols to capture
a glimpse of writers' moment-to-moment thinking as they compose a text through analyzing
writers' tape-recorded reports of their writing
process. Think-aloud protocols, first used in
studies of problem-solving strategies in cognitive psychology, have enabled writing researchers to explore what is involved in the act of
writing, specifically how writers orchestrate the
underlying cognitive processes of composing.
Perhaps most notably, the work of Flower
and Hayes in the late 1970s and 1980s, based
on protocol analysis, drew upon rhetoric
and cognitive psychology to ask how we might
model or describe the basic, underlying, goaldirected processes that go on in composing.
While think-aloud protocols have been quite
useful in helping researchers understand more
about the what of writing, researchers have
begun to pose a different set of questions, not
so much about what writers do, but why. These
why questions arise from our increasing
concern with rhetorical situations and our
growing sensitivity to the diverse assumptions
and goals that influence writers as they move in
and across these different contexts. One of the
more striking examples of this shift is in the
study of novice writers. In the 1980s, researchers such as Flower and Hayes and Bereiter and
Scardamalia were using protocols to describe
whether and how novice performance differed
from that of experts. Expert/novice comparisons also laid the groundwork for a newapproach to teaching by showing that effective
writers had a distinctive repertoire of problemsolving strategies. Education could indeed intervene where it mattered by teaching the process
of writing, not just showing how to analyze a
written product. At the same time, process instruction has raised important questions about
whether it is possible to teach a natural process
of activities and ignore goals and strategies and
how we can scaffold the transition to new, often more difficult strategies.
Researchers in the 1990s are building on
this work by asking why such differences exist,
why writers recognize and attend to some strategies but not others, and whether the idiosyncratic behaviors we observe in different groups
of writers possess a "logic" of their own. Researchers who ask such questions argue that
differences in performance and strategies are
not always best described in terms of deficits
and failures. Using think-aloud protocols,
we have learned that other factors—for
example, writers' past experiences with writing,
the evaluative climate of the classroom, or their
assumptions about the task—can influence their
writing decisions and performance. The purpose of this growing body of research is to
clarify and understand some of these situational
and cognitive influences.
Think-aloud protocols, used alone or in
conjunction with other methods, such as text
analysis and retrospective interviews, have enabled researchers to build a rich understanding
of the relationship among texts, situational factors, and writers' constructive processes. Most
important, think-aloud protocols have provided
a window into writers' thinking, opening up
more grounded and sensitive ways to understand the strategies writers bring to the act of
composing, the logic they invoke, and the ways
teaching could foster the process of learning.
Stuart Greene
University of Wisconsin, Madison

20. A rhetorical perspective on Christian preaching.
21. Rhetoricians characteristically are drawn to the
22. intersections of theory and practice, and pulpit
23. oratory is an art that exemplifies the creative
24. dynamics between the two. Contemporary historians of rhetoric, though, tend to focus on
25. certain events in the development of preaching
26. theory, especially events that are seen as central
27. to the unfolding of the classical tradition. Most
28. accounts thus emphasize Augustine's endorsement of Greek and Roman rhetoric for Christian purposes; the importance of preaching
29. (along with letter-writing and verse-writing) as
30. one of the three rhetorical genres of medieval
31. Europe; the proliferation of manuals on the
32. thematic sermon in the thirteenth century; and
33. the influence of Blair, Campbell, and Whately,
34. the British triumvirate of minister-rhetoricians
35. who reinterpreted classical traditions for modern times. Attention to broader theoretical and
36. practical developments, however, offers a more
37. encompassing perspective.
38. The practice and theory of preaching, so
39. often associated with Euro-American Christianity, are rooted in the rhetoric of ancient Palestinian Judaism, especially as embodied in
40. the first-century synagogue. Both Jesus of
41. Nazareth and the Apostle Paul, whose discourse
42. practices shaped the communities of faith that
43. produced the New Testament, were immersed
44. in this richly developed Hebrew interpretive and
45. oratorical tradition. At the same time, from the
46. earliest days of the church, Christian preachers
47. have been informed by the legacy of classical Greek and Roman rhetors—especially
48. Isocrates, Aristotle, and Cicero. Currently, the
49. twentieth-century renaissance in rhetoric has
50. encouraged an unprecedented illumination of
51. the power claims encoded in biblical texts,
52. hermeneutical precepts, and ecclesiastical practices. Such investigation is proving to be particularly generative for the work of feminist
53. preachers.
54. The proclamation of the Christian message
55. (kerygma) emerged as a few of the disciples
56. began to announce their conviction that Jesus,
57. the crucified messiah, had been raised from the
58. dead. This is the core of the church's gospel
59. (euangelion), and according to the New Testament, the believers proclaimed it first to other
60. followers of Jesus, often evoking disbelief, and
61. later to Jews and Gentiles beyond their nascent
62. community of faith.
63. Since nearly all of the earliest disciples were
64. Jewish, they interpreted the crucifixion and resurrection events by drawing not only on their
65. memories of the words and actions of Jesus but
66. also on the elaborate store of imagery, narrative,
67. ritual, and conviction provided by their thousand-year-old religious heritage. As the new
68. faith spread, oral traditions were superseded by
69. written forms (letters, collections of sayings,
70. accounts of the passion and resurrection, and
71. finally, gospels). Eventually, Christian worship
72. came to include the pattern of preaching estab-
73. lished in the synagogue: a reading from the sacred texts was followed by an explication of its
74. meaning, which sometimes included an application or exhortation to members of the congregation.
75. Christian oratory also received a formative
76. imprint from the traditions of classical rhetoric.
77. To begin with, as James Kinneavy has shown,
78. the concept of faith inscribed in the New Testament reflects the influence of Greek theories
79. of persuasion. Kinneavy argues that the New
80. Testament writers and their audiences understood pistis (usually translated "faith") to refer
81. not only to human trust in God (the concept of
82. faith emphasized in the Hebrew scriptures) but
83. also to the human process of giving free assent,
84. despite some uncertainty, to a new perspective
85. (the notion of persuasion exemplified in the
86. writings of Isocrates and Aristotle). The early
87. church assumed, as Kinneavy's analysis reveals,
88. that persuasion to the faith was not a once-and-
89. for-all event, essential only for potential converts, but an ongoing process, a day-by-day
90. assent that would likely increase in depth and
91. power as doubt was explored and commitment
92. was tested.
93. During the early chapters in the church's
94. life, Christian orators designed a variety ofapproaches to this task of ongoing persuasion.
95. At first, worship took place in the homes of believers, and the preacher offered what Origen
96. would later call a homily (homilia, "conversation"), colloquial in style, which typically followed the scriptural text verse by verse. After
97. Nicea (325), when imperial persecution had
98. ended and worshipers gathered in large
99. churches, some preachers began to employ a
100. more artful expression. In the Eastern church,
101. preachers tended to draw upon the classical
102. rhetor's devices more freely than did their
103. Western counterparts. At the extreme was John
104. Chrysostom, bishop of Constantinople, who
105. endeavored (without success) to keep his congregation from interrupting his eloquence with
106. applause.
107. In the West, by contrast, church leaders
108. were more reluctant to use secular strategies for
109. Christian persuasion. Partly as a response to
110. this dilemma, Augustine, bishop of Hippo, developed a Christian theory of rhetoric. Far more
111. than simply giving permission for pulpit orators
112. to cultivate Greek and Roman modes of expression, Augustine reinvented classical theory
113. by articulating a Christian perspective on
114. the nature of signs, the art of interpretation,
115. and the relations between speaker and hearer.
116. Augustine's On Christian Doctrine(De doc-
117. trina christiana, 427) is the treatise most explicitly oriented to the preacher's work. Books 1-
118. 3 advise the Christian rhetor on how to discern
119. the truth in scriptural texts, while Book 4focuses on how to convey that truth so as to persuade one's hearers. Though most of Book 4 is
120. devoted to an application of Ciceronian theory
121. to the preacher's style, Augustine insists that
122. wisdom is more important than eloquence, and
123. his bias toward the preacher as teacher is clear.
124. Augustine's more innovative rhetorical
125. proposals, however, are expressed in Concerning the Teacher (De magistro, 389) and On
126. Catechizing the Uninstructed (De catechizandis
127. rudibus, 3909) as well as in Books 1–3 of
128. On Christian Doctrine, Of particular interest
129. here is Augustine's exploration of words as
130. "prompts"; his emphasis on the creative participation of the reader or hearer in making meaning and effecting persuasion; his awareness of
131. the mutual transformation involved in every
132. rhetorical interaction; and his insistence on the
133. "rule of love" as a guide for interpretation,
134. teaching, and preaching. These ventures prefigure several tenets fundamental to the "new
135. rhetorics" of our own day.
136. During the medieval era, preachers continued to adapt their methods according to
137. the exigencies of audience, context, and purpose. Even Robert of Basevorn, whose Form of
138. Preaching (1322) offers advice on how to compose and deliver the intricately structured "thematic" or "university style" sermon, acknowledges that there have always been nearly as
139. many ways of preaching as there are skillful
140. preachers. Robert's manual typifies the
141. hundreds of treatises on the art of preaching
142. (ars praedicandi) that circulated in this period,
143. promoting the use of the thematic sermon form.
144. The early preaching of Luther, for instance,
145. reflects the influence of thematic structures; as
146. the Reformation took root, however, and as his
147. passion for educating the whole community of
148. faith increased, Luther's sermon form became
149. less complex and more direct. This commitment
150. to instruction, along with the reformers'renewed emphasis on the preaching of the Word,
151. has left a lasting impression on the ministry and
152. mission of the church.
153. A balance between tradition and innovation has characterized pulpit oratory in the
154. United States. Teresa Toulouse has analyzed
155. major homiletical influences and shifts through
156. the sermons of Cotton, Colman, Channing,
157. Emerson, and Coleridge. Ernest Bormann
158. has traced the development of a "rhetoric ofromantic pragmatism" beginning with Jonathan
159. Edwards and culminating in the speeches
160. of Abraham Lincoln. Henry Mitchell has chronicled the emergence and influence of a distinctive African-American homiletic. David But-
161. trick has offered a contemporary manual, including an extensive bibliography, which
162. brings the resources of rhetorical theory to the
163. preaching task.
164. A significant issue often neglected in the
165. scholarship on preaching has been the exclusion
166. of women from interpreting and proclaiming
167. the Word. Women of faith have long responded
168. to this prohibition with rhetorical insight and
169. ingenuity. Even when the ordination of female
170. preachers was still unthinkable among church
171. officials, a few women managed to transgress
172. the boundary set down between them and the
173. pulpit. One of these was Hildegard of Bingen,
174. the twelfth-century abbess whose portrayal of
175. her visionary prophetic call so impressed the
176. bishops and the Pope that she succeeded in
177. making several preaching tours among the cities of the Rhineland, calling for spiritualrenewal and church reform.
178. Margaret Fell's prison epistle, Women's
179. Speaking Justified, Proved, and Allowed by the
180. Scriptures (1666), offers a deft and elaborate
181. argument in support of women preachers, anticipating some of the hermeneutical moves
182. practiced by feminist interpreters today. To
183. refute those who appeal to Pauline proscriptions of women's speaking, Fell demonstrates
184. how to read the Bible not as dogma but as
185. drama—as a sacred story centered in Christ,
186. who not only welcomed the ministry of women
187. but also commissioned them, according to
188. Matthew and John, to proclaim the Easter gospel. Her treatise and leadership encouraged an
189. abiding commitment to sexual equality in the
190. Society of Friends.
191. This century has witnessed the ordination
192. of women preachers in nearly every major Protestant denomination. Yet Christian feminists
193. continue to struggle with the Bible, acknowledging its primal role in their faith communities
194. and in their own spiritual formation, while also
195. recognizing the dangers of its pervasive
196. androcentric bias. Over the past twenty years,
197. feminist scholars have conducted a many-faceted examination of the rhetorical aims and
198. power claims inscribed in scriptural texts,
199. hermeneutical methods, and patterns of church
200. life. One key revelation has been the construction of the canon as a reassertion of patriarchal
201. hegemony against what Elisabeth Schüssler
202. Fiorenza has called "the discipleship of equals,"
203. which originally gathered around Jesus and to
204. which the New Testament provides only fragmentary witness. In her 1987 presidential address to the Society of Biblical Literature, Schussler Fiorenza called for a reorientation of
205. biblical studies in rhetorical terms. Her locus of
206. authority is not the texts of the Bible but the
207. community of believers, specifically those who
208. struggle for liberation from the interlocking
209. oppressions of patriarchy.
210. Feminist investigation thus undermines the
211. mystique of canonical status by insisting that
212. the divine Word (whether present in Christ, in
213. the Bible, or in a sermon), is always mediated
214. by communities of believing, wondering, arguing, transforming people like ourselves. Such
215. a conviction reconstitutes the traditional authority relations among community, text, and
216. preacher, as Christine Smith has shown. The
217. implications of a feminist hearing and preaching of the Word are becoming evident in Christian liturgy, theology, and ethics—indeed, in the
218. very religious imagination of the faithful.
219. Several important aspects of preaching remain unexplored here. Concentrating on developments in Europe and the United States, we
220. have not attended to Christian proclamation in
221. South Africa, Central America, or Asia, for instance. And given our focus on Christianity, we
222. have not considered the traditions of Jewish or
223. Islamic preachers. As religion so often turns out
224. to be a catalyst for violent conflict, continuing
225. study and conversation are essential, and it is all
226. the more urgent to investigate the interplay
227. between rhetoric and faith.
