120. The centrality of discourse to his work accounts for interest in Foucault among rhetoricians. His most complete theoretical statements

123. neither a thing, merely representative of thoughts
124. whose source was an author, a work, or a transcendental spirit, nor a process such as reading,

129. might cause some to treat discourse as static,
130. thereby allowing the creative power of discursive
131. action to occur unnoticed. Foucault argued,

133. fundamental to understanding the function of
134. discourse for creating knowledge, power, and
135. ethical systems. Treated as an event, discourse
136. provided sites for investigating the production of
137. knowledge and the formation of human relationships. Moreover, Foucault argued that because of
138. the awesome power of discursive events societies tend to manage discourse by creating taboos,
139. exclusions, categories, and other systems that put
140. parameters around who might talk about what
141. in which circumstances with what effects. Hence,
142. investigation of discursive practices provides a

324. Contemporary German philosopher and
325. hermeneutical theorist. Throughout his writings, Hans-Georg Gadamer elaborates and critically extends the tradition of philosophical

329. theory, with its key concept of historicallyeffected consciousness, involves a careful dialogue
330. with the Western philosophical tradition, starting with Plato and Aristotle and focusing on the
331. perennial question of the conditions of truth
332. and knowledge, by tracing expressions of human understanding from Greek philosophy to
333. Renaissance art (aesthetics) to contemporary
334. critical theory. Gadamer's wide-ranging and
335. uniquely interdisciplinary scholarship is important to scholars in biblical studies, critical theory, legal studies, literature, philosophy, and
336. rhetoric.
337. In 1960 Gadamer published his major
338. philosophical work, Wahrheit und Methode
339. (Truth and Method, 1975). Gadamer's study
340. engages the concept of interpretation as it generally relates to continental philosophy and
341. specifically to the Kantian question, What are
342. the conditions of our knowledge? Gadamer's
343. work posits the concept of conditioned knowledge as primary and inescapable. Contrary to
344. the formulations found in the phenomenology
345. of Edmund Husserl (1859–1938), bracketing
346. anything that consciousness does not intend
347. and following Heidegger's break with Husserl's
348. eidetics in the "temporal analytics of human
349. existence" (Gadamer 1975:xviii), the concept of
350. Dasein, Gadamer argues that the meaning of
351. language resides in history and tradition prior
352. to residing in the subject. This argument leads
353. Gadamer into a long debate with German philosopher Jiirgen Habermas over the status of
354. interpretation, truth, and the roles of language
355. and history in constructing human understanding. Truth and Method examines the idea of
356. truth and knowledge as unintended interpretive
357. acts within a mode of being. Initially, Gadamer's
358. book was misunderstood as prescribing an art
359. or technique for understanding that would unseat the established investigative methods in the
360. human sciences (Geisteswissenschaften) that
361. begin with German Romanticism in the early
362. nineteenth century and the modern natural sciences (Naturwissenschaften). Gadamer argues
363. in the foreword to the second edition that the
364. focus of his study is not on conflicting methods
365. or devising a new method but on the objectives
366. of knowledge. It is the proposition found in the
367. human sciences and the natural sciences claiming the possibility of an unmediated apprehension of truth, grasping the object as it really
368. exists, with which Gadamer finds himself in
369. conflict. Gadamer's concern is in extending the
370. epistemological question raised by Immanuel
371. Kant (1724–1804), How is understanding possible? Truth and Method explores this question
372. through an inquiry into the aesthetic consciousness brought on by an experience of an art object, the status of truth and understanding in the
373. human sciences, and a hermeneutics guided by
374. language.
375. Gadamer describes his own work as not
376. being a dogmatic solution to the question concerning human understanding but a philosophic inquiry into what is common to all
377. modes of understanding. He argues that human understanding is never purely subjective
378. behavior toward a given object but its own
379. history of influence reflected back ontoitself.
380. For Gadamer, viewers of art and readers of texts come to an understanding, a
381. hermeneutical consciousness, only under specific historical conditions. Gadamerian her-
382. meneutics offers the questions How does one
383. interpret? and Is objective understanding possible? For Gadamer, one interprets an object
384. through a historical horizon. The interpretation is not a pure grasping so much as it is a
385. dialectical relationship. The historical horizon
386. of the object meets with the historical horizon
387. of the viewer or reader, resulting in an active
388. or productive interpretation. The object under
389. consideration is never completely revealed in
390. the meeting of historical horizons. Gadamer
391. argues for a post-Romantic conception of interpretation that posits the object as in excess
392. of the author's or artist's mind (mens auctoris).
393. The meeting of historical horizons is not arecovery of the pure object, or the truth, but a
394. living and dynamic dialogue between past,
395. present, and future, continuing the Western
396. philosophical tradition (Uberlieferung) and
397. yielding productive interpretations giving new
398. meanings to human creative acts.
