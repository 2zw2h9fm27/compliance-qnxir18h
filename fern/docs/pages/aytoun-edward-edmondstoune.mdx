(1813–1865)
First Professor of English Literature at the
University of Edinburgh from 1845–1865. The
tradition of belles-lettres was already well established when Aytoun assumed the chair of rhetoric and belles-lettres at Edinburgh in 1845. In
his lectures he maintained "the utter worthlessness of the art" of rhetoric and criticized the
"artificiality of the system" that was encumbered with rules in such number "that were we
to observe them all, our speech would degenerate into a tissue of woven sophistry." In his introductory lecture, he stated: "I would much
rather sit in this chair as Professor of English
Language and Literature than as Professor of
Rhetoric and Belles Lettres." In the manuscript
"I would much rather" has been erased and "I
am delighted to sit in this chair" is substituted,
an alteration obviously made after his title was
changed. His lectures were hugely successful at
the University of Edinburgh, where the townspeople flocked to hear him speak on the subject
of English literature. During his tenure, attendance rose from twenty-seven to 150 students,
and, largely because of his influence and popularity, the Royal Commission of 1861 recommended that the study of English literature be
added to the curriculum in all of the Scottish
universities.
Aytoun was a strong supporter of the
Scottish system of education and took "to task
people who think it beneath the dignity of a
university to deal with rudimentary elements."
He opposed the compulsory entrance exams
that excluded the poorer Scots. He felt that the
title of the chair was prejudicial to attendance,
and, inasmuch as professors were customarily
paid fees by their students, this was not an
insignificant consideration. He believed that
"many persons entertain the idea that 'Rhetoric' is a trifling art, unsatisfactory as a department of mental training, and that the term
'Belles Lettres' signifies nothing more than general, and sometimes aimless criticism." He suggested that the course would be better called
"English Literature and Composition"; bowing
to part of his request, the Royal Commissioners of 1858–1862 doubled his salary and renamed the chair the Regious Professorship of
Rhetoric and English Literature. Thus the Scottish universities adopted English literature as an
academic subject some forty or fifty years ahead
of either Oxford or Cambridge.
The National Library of Scotland has a
large collection of Aytoun manuscripts in their
special collections. His lectures on rhetoric are
a rather pedestrian review of traditional rhetoric, interspersed with his constant criticism of
the ancient system. The most interesting work
in the collection is his introductory lecture, in
which he outlines his ideas on education, rhetoric, and the study of English literature. Aytoun,
like his American contemporary, Francis Child,
who was also an early professor of English literature (at Harvard), is best known in Scotland
today for his collection of ballads.
Winifred Bryan Horner
Texas Christian University

17. Bacon, Francis (1561–1626)
18. Lawyer, educational reformer, rhetorician, scientist, lord chancellor of England. Author of the
19. Advancement of Learning, Novum organum,
20. New Atlantis, and other scientific and literary
21. works, Francis Bacon sought to reform the
22. whole of human learning in his time by proposing new methods for the development and
23. communication of knowledge. Bacon sought to
24. replace Aristotelian and scholastic methods of
25. knowing with a new scientific method, developed a rhetorical theory as a means of communicating knowledge thus acquired, and presented both his scientific method and his
26. rhetoric in a paradoxical language that has inspired diverse positivist, institutional, and
27. democratic forms of scientific rhetoric.
28. Bacon's scientific method and his rhetoric are
29. both grounded in faculty psychology, a view of
30. human nature that posits distinct capacities corresponding to each stage in the development and
31. communication of knowledge. These faculties include understanding, reason, imagination, memory, appetite, and will. Understanding and reason
32. work together, understanding serving to assimilate
33. and organize past experience, reason serving to
34. communicate and express that experience. Imagination depicts in the mind past experiences,
35. either individually or in novel combinations.
36. Memory stores experience as singular instances,
37. derived from external stimuli, the raw materials
38. for all mental processes. Appetite and will convert
39. knowledge acquired through the other faculties
40. into fruitful action: appetite by its attraction to apparent good, will by translating that attraction
41. into action. Sense is not among these faculties, but
42. it enters into the knowing process when imagination depicts in the mind experiences derived from
43. sense and stored in memory.
44. Drawing upon this faculty psychology,
45. Bacon seeks to replace traditional methods of
46. knowing with his new scientific method. In the
47. Advancement of Learning, he specifically rejects
48. methods of knowing that he associates with
49. Aristotle and the medieval scholastics and that he
50. scornfully characterizes as "cobwebs of learning,
51. admirable for the fineness of thread and work, but
52. of no substance or profit" (6:122). Their method
53. of induction is "utterly vicious and incompetent"
54. because it enumerates particular instances on one
55. side without regard for whether or not contrary
56. instances appear on the other side (6:265). Their
57. method of deduction, the syllogism, is equally
58. faulty because it is based upon sequences of
59. propositions, which in turn are based upon words,
60. which themselves are merely popular notions of
61. things, "grossly and variably collected out of particulars" and therefore subject to error at the very
62. foundation (6:266).
63. In place of these faulty methods Bacon sets
64. his new scientific method, often identified with the
65. method of induction. As set forth in Novum
66. organum, however, this method actually has three
67. parts or stages, which serve as aids to sense,
68. memory, and understanding. These stages are the
69. compilation of natural and experimental histories,
70. the tabulation or arrangement of the content of
71. those histories, and the gradual development of
72. true forms via induction. The natural and experimental histories are uninterpreted records of observations of natural phenomena, derived from
73. sense and stored in memory. These histories, the
74. basis of all natural philosophy, are complete
75. records of all instances of a natural phenomenon
76. (for example, heat), from which may be selected
77. especially striking instances that point the way to
78. further inquiry. The tables and arrangements of instances drawn from the histories are lists of sig-
79. nificant instances that show their relationships (for
80. example, instances of the presence or absence of
81. heat or of increases or decreases of heat) and that
82. serve as an aid to memory. The method of induction is a guide to the understanding in its search
83. for the true form of the phenomenon under investigation, a means of identifying, through a process
84. of exclusion, "such a nature as is always present
85. or absent with the given nature, and always increases and decreases with it" (as heat is always
86. associated with motion) (8:204).
87. Drawing again upon the faculty psychology,
88. Bacon develops his rhetorical theory as a means
89. of communicating the knowledge acquired by
90. way of his scientific method. Bacon's rhetoric encompasses a variety of methods and styles of communication, including at least two different styles
91. suitable for use at different stages in his scientific
92. method. In the Advancement of Learning and its
93. Latin translation De augmentis scientiarum, Bacon describes several pairs of methods of communication, including the magistral and initiative
94. methods, the exoteric and acroamatic methods,
95. and aphorisms and "methods" ("the arrangement
96. and connexion and joining of the parts") (6:291).
97. The magistral and exoteric methods and "methods" address popular audiences for the purpose
98. of putting knowledge to use. The initiative and
99. acroamatic methods and aphorisms, in contrast,
100. address scientific audiences for the purpose of advancing knowledge.
101. In addition to these methods of communication, in the Advancement of Learning and
102. Parasceve Bacon also describes two styles suitable
103. for use in his scientific method: the imaginative
104. style is addressed to the understanding and is suitable for use in the method of induction; the plain
105. style is addressed to sense and memory and is
106. suitable for use in the natural and experimental
107. histories and perhaps also in the tables and arrangements of instances. The imaginative style has
108. justification in Bacon's claim that the duty and
109. office of rhetoric is "to apply Reason toImagination for the better moving of the will" (6:297). In
110. its figured form, this style employs the "force of
111. eloquence and persuasion" to move the will to
112. action (6:299). In its unfigured form, such as the
113. aphorism, this style does not employ figures but
114. demands that the imagination create its own figures. The imaginative style, in its unfigured form,
115. is most suitable for use in the method of induction.
116. The plain style has justification in Bacon's rejection of a highly figured style as the "affectionate
117. study of eloquence andcopie [copiousness] of
118. speech," in favor of a style that omits all "ornaments of speech, similitudes, treasury of eloquence, and such like emptinesses" and sets things
119. down "briefly and concisely, so that they may be
120. nothing less than words" (6:119; 8:359). The
121. plain style is most suitable for use in the natural
122. and experimental histories and perhaps the tables
123. and arrangements of instances.
124. Bacon presents both his scientific method
125. and his rhetoric in a paradoxical language that
126. has inspired positivist, institutional, and democratic forms of scientific rhetoric. Bacon's language is paradoxical because it combines tradition and innovation, engaging old mythologies
127. and traditions to present new ideas. In New
128. Atlantis, for example, Bacon engages the myth
129. of the lost civilization of Atlantis and the biblical portrait of Solomon's temple to create an
130. image of a scientific community dedicated to the
131. discovery of new knowledge and its practical
132. application for the benefit and use of humanity.
133. Such images have inspired diverse interpretations of Bacon's scientific method and his
134. rhetoric. Positivist science, for example, has
135. found in Bacon a justification for a stylistic ideal
136. of plain prose exhibiting clearness, brevity, and
137. appropriateness. Institutional science has found
138. in him an ideal for the communal organization
139. of science and a figured style suitable for use in
140. scientific communities. Most recently, proponents of democratic science have reinterpreted
141. Bacon's ideal of plain prose as a vehicle for broad
142. participation in science for the purpose not of
143. dominating nature but of addressing human
144. wants and needs. For their literary qualities,
145. therefore, no less than their substantive contributions to scientific method and scientific rhetoric, Bacon's writings have an enduring appeal.
