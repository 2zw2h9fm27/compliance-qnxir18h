A type of spoken or written discourse, recognized as conventional by members of an
intellectual community, that draws together
certain substantive and stylistic features in
response to a recurrent rhetorical situation.
Genre in its most general sense is an abstraction, a name given to the human propensity to
sort linguistic and artistic artifacts into categories or classifications; thus a genre or genres
are instantiations of this classificatory urge.
Because the notion of genre deals with types
of discourses, it is related to the traditional
concept of arrangement in rhetorical theory;

46. One of the more influential rhetoricians of the

48. Between 1885 and 1915, John Franklin
49. Genung's rhetoric treatises were the most
50. widely used rhetoric texts in North American
51. colleges and universities. Genung was a professor of rhetoric, oratory, and literature at
52. Amherst College from 1882 to 1918. During
53. that time, Genung produced four successful
54. textbooks: Practical Elements of Rhetoric
55. (1886), Handbook of Rhetorical Analysis
56. (1888), Outlines of Rhetoric (1893), and The
57. Working Principles of Rhetoric (1900).
58. Genung's rhetoric texts consistently advanced
59. the definition of rhetoric as "the art of adapting discourse, in harmony with its subject and
60. occasion, to the requirements of a reader or
61. hearer." This definition reveals Genung's debt
62. to the classical tradition as well as to the theoretical orientation of the New Rhetoricians,
63. George Campbell and Hugh Blair, who insisted
64. that the art of rhetoric consists of the ability to
65. apply fundamental principles to various subjects, contexts, and audiences.
66. Regarding rhetoric as an indispensable art
67. for everyday life, Genung believed that the student of rhetoric should learn to compose for a
68. variety of purposes. His textbooks constructed
69. a course in rhetoric that prepared students to
70. produce the "prevailing literary forms." Conceiving of "prevailing forms" quite broadly,
71. Genung stressed that rhetorical study should
72. take in all possible forms of discourse including
73. the political essay, scientific and descriptive treatises, short stories, editorials, and reporters'
74. columns, as well as the traditional forms of the
75. sermon and the public lecture. Genung's primary pedagogical interest was in offering rhetorical training that would allow students to
76. develop their innate voices as well as prepare
77. them to deal skillfully with all rhetorical occasions.
78. Genung's theory of rhetoric highlighted the
79. canons of invention and style. Like other influential nineteenth-century rhetoricians, such as
80. Henry N. Day and A.S. Hill, Genung subsumed
81. arrangement under invention as part of the
82. overall process of shaping a coherent composition. Defining invention as the "organization of
83. thought, according to its nature and object, into
84. a coherent and inter-related form of discourse,"
85. Genung treated invention as a process that develops an original conception into a completed
86. form that will have a desired effect (Working
87. Principles 388). Under invention, Genung discusses the development of a major theme with
88. supporting main ideas and methods of amplification. Genung defined description, narration,
89. exposition, and argumentation as inventional
90. processes that embody different intentions and
91. effects; therefore, the writer must consider the
92. effects of these modes and choose among them
93. appropriately. Genung's discussion of style also
94. focused on strategies that will allow the rhetorician to reconcile subject matter and intention
95. with the intellectual and emotional responses of
96. readers. Defining style as the "manner of choosing and arranging words so as to produce determinate and intended effects in language,"
97. Genung focused on the qualities of clearness,
98. force, and beauty and stylistic techniques such
99. as denotation, connotation, sentence structure,
100. and prosaic rhythm. In Genung's view the
101. audience's anticipated responses govern all stylistic choices. Whether treating style, invention,
102. or the importance of rhetoric as a liberal art,
103. Genung promoted the traditional rhetorical
104. principle that the rhetorician's greatest challenge is to communicate effectively.
