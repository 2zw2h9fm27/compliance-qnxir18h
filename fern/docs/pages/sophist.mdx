## Definition & Etymology

A professional teacher of political excellence in
ancient Greece noted for giving traveling lectures on Rhetoric for a fee. The ancient Greek
term sophist was used in both a general and a
specific way, both of which carried positive and
negative connotations that its user might highlight. In its general use, sophist can be found in
references dating from the sixth centuryB.C.E.
that draw upon its literal translation as "wisdom-bearer." Such sophists were respected
teachers or professors who sought to impart
wisdom to others; the "educational" role of
poets and philosophers in Greek society marked
them as "sophists" in this sense. A suspicion of
sophists who were "too clever for their own
good" (or for the good of society) added negative connotations to this term, much as the disparaging use of "intellectual" does today.
By early in the fifth century B.C.E., the term
became associated with the rising professional
class of traveling teachers who professed
to teach aretê, or political excellence, for a fee,
especially through lectures and courses in rhetoric. These sophists were educational innovators
responsive to social and political changes
that made the ability to speak effectively a valuable commodity. In Athens particularly, the
democratic reforms begun by Solon, continued
by Cleisthenes, and completed by Pericles and
Ephialtes by the middle of the fifth centuryB.C.E.
ensured broad participation in public life: in the
Assembly, where all citizens might speak, in the
courts where litigants were heard by huge lay
juries who decided civil and criminal cases on
matters of law as well as matters of fact, and on
ceremonial occasions such as public festivals.
The useful training offered by the sophists
gained them attention and respect, though it also
s
brought jealousy, anger, suspicion, and fear from
those who saw the most promising young
men drawn away from friends and families
who would have been their mentors in political life, and drawn toward these foreigners who
charged money on the promise that they could
impart practical wisdom to their students. In
Against the Sophists, Isocrates (436–338 B.C.E.)
complains that some of these competitors with his
school ofRhetoric promised more than they could
possibly deliver. In Gorgias, Plato (427–347 B.C.E.)
suggests that they make the worse case seem the
better by teaching this "form of flattery" that
persuades without educating. Plato's concern over
the sophists' teachings did not merely reflect his
bias against rhetoric, but rather a deeper philosophical difference summed up in the statement
of Protagoras of Abdera (490?–20? B.C.E.), one
of the earliest and best known sophists, that "man
is the measure and the measurer of all things."
Unlike Plato, the sophists doubted whether absolute truth existed or could be known by humans.
They believed that laws and customs were not
handed down from the gods or rooted in a natural order but were the products of human creation. And although they differed on the allegiance they believed was owed to human-made
laws and customs, they did believe that citizens
could take an active role in theRhetorical processes
that created them, confident that persuasive arguments could be made on either side of a given
controversy. Such philosophical relativism could
not but offend Plato and threaten defenders of the
status quo.
Among the prominent early sophists were
Protagoras of Abdera, probably the first to
charge fees for his teaching; Gorgias of Leontini
(480–380? B.C.E.), known for the jingling poetic
style evident in his Encomium on Helen; Prodicus of Ceos (470–370? B.C.E.), who insisted
on the precise use of language; Hippias ofElis
(465–395? B.C.E.), a teacher with broad knowledge who taught a wide range of courses and
developed a mnemonic system for speakers; and
Thrasymachus of Chalcedon (465?–10? B.C.E.),
who made original contributions to a theory of
Rhetorical style, emotional appeals, and delivery.
History has been less kind to the sophists,
perhaps, than their contributions warrant. Little
of the sophists' writings have survived, while
Plato's criticisms of them are readily available.
Yet, as practical educators, the sophists wrote
for their own age more than for posterity.
J. Clarke Rountree, III
University of Alabama, Huntsville

7. Sophistic Rhetoric
8. A modern phrase used to refer to a collection of
9. Rhetorical practices and doctrines attributed
10. to the "older sophists" of fifth-century B.C.E.
11. Greece. The term sophist (sophistês) means a
12. person of sophia (skill or wisdom). In ancient
13. as well as modern times, the label sophist is
14. assigned rather inconsistently according to
15. the perspective of the writer. In ancient times,
16. people as diverse as Prometheus, Homer, Hesi-
17. od, Damon, Solon, Thaïes, Pythagoras, Anaxa-
18. goras, Empedocles, Zeno, Plato, Socrates,
19. and Isocrates were called sophists. Aeschines,
20. Eudoxus, and Protagoras are included in
21. Philostratus's Lives of the Sophists as well as
22. in Diogenes Laertius' Lives of EminentPhilosophers.
23. In modern times, the group most often
24. meant by the phrase "the sophists" includes
25. Gorgias, Protagoras, Hippias, Prodicus, Thrasymachus, Critias, and Antiphon. However,
26. some scholars add some or all of the following
27. to the list: Polus, Euthydemus, Dionysodorus,
28. Callicles, Socrates, Antisthenes, Alcidamas,
29. Lycophron, as well as the authors of Dissoi
30. Logoi, the Anonymus Iamblichi, and the Hip-
31. pocratic Corpus.
32. The idea of a distinct "sophistic Rhetoric"
33. is a relatively recent notion. In classical Greece
34. "sophistic" and "rhetoric" were considered
35. distinct, though sometimes overlapping, arts or
36. technai (cf. Plato's Gorgias). In no surviving
37. Greek text—not even Plato's or Aristotle's—will
38. one find the exact phrase "sophistic rhetoric."
39. While in contemporary scholarship a distinct
40. "sophistic Rhetoric" is sometimes contrasted to
41. "philosophical rhetoric," classical authors did
42. not draw such a distinction. In Lives of the
43. Sophists, for example,Philostratus even claims
44. that "we must regard the ancient sophistic art
45. as philosophic rhetoric" (1.480).
46. One of the earliest efforts to describe a distinct "sophistical" rhetoric was made in 1855
47. by Edward M. Cope. Since that time, many
48. scholars have taken the category "sophistic
49. Rhetoric" for granted. Nonetheless, it is impor-
50. tant to recognize that the categories with which
51. ancient writers classified doctrines and practices
52. are not always consistent with contemporary
53. categories. Because of the diversity of the
54. projects that have been labeled sophistic, it is
55. more useful and accurate to speak of multiple
56. "sophistic rhetorics" than to posit a singular
57. and monolithic sophistic Rhetoric.
58. When contemporary scholars refer tosophistic Rhetorics, typically they refer to one (or
59. more) of three concepts: specific rhetorical practices, implicit or explicit theories of rhetoric,
60. and particular pedagogical practices attributed
61. to the sophists. The two sophists most often
62. cited by contemporary scholars of Rhetoric are
63. Protagoras and Gorgias, so it is from them that
64. most of the examples below are drawn.
65. Of the sophists' various Rhetorical practices, the most often discussed are their general
66. composition styles, their use of specific figures
67. and tropes, and their cultivation of two-sided
68. argumentation (dissoi logoi). The sophists
69. wrote during a time when written prose was still
70. a novelty. Archaic Greek culture relied almost
71. exclusively on oral modes of composition and
72. communication. The earliest "literature" of
73. Greek culture was epic poetry that rhapsodes
74. memorized and passed on from generation to
75. generation. Some of the first written compositions (other than lists or basic property markings) were records of these orally composed
76. mythical poems. Composition in oral Greek
77. society originally evolved to serve the needs of
78. memory, as it was through repetition and
79. memorization that one generation passed on to
80. the next what it had learned. For example, the
81. vocabulary of an oral dialect is usually limited
82. to a few thousand words, while modern English
83. has a recorded vocabulary of one and a half
84. million. The need to remember also affects syntax and composition, making verse, song, and
85. story the best vehicles in which to "store" the
86. records of Greek oral culture.
87. Some of the earliest theoretical or "philosophical" writings were created during a time
88. when the habits of an oral culture dominated
89. composition. Parmenides, often considered the
90. parent of Western philosophy, wrote in the sixth
91. century B.C.E. and used the same hexameter
92. verse as did Homer. Parmenides tells his philosophy in the form of a mythic journey of discovery in which he encounters a goddess.
93. Heraclitus wrote in easily memorized aphorisms. By the middle of the fifth centuryB.C.E.,
94. the time of the older sophists, writers were experimenting with different forms of composition that were both indebted to, and broke
95. from, previous poetic forms of composition. In
96. Plato's dialogue Protagoras, the title character
97. gives a speech that begins with a mythicalaccount (mythos) of the origins of human political arts, but then moves to a well-reasoned account (or logos). Gorgias' speeches known as
98. Helen and Palamedes follow the traditional
99. habit of treating time-honored mythical themes,
100. but do so in the process of developing sophisticated (for the time) forms of reasoning. It
101. is difficult to make generalizations about the
102. prose compositions of the sophists because they
103. are not as poetic or mythic as earlier writings,
104. yet they also are not as spare and analytical as
105. later writings. As contemporary theorists explore different modes of composition and discuss "secondary orality" as encouraged by electronic media, the "mixed" composition style of
106. the older sophists has provided an interesting
107. set of texts to consider.
108. A good example of a "sophistic Rhetoric"
109. described by contemporary scholars is "Gorgianic Rhetoric," which makes use of various
110. figures of speech. Gorgias' texts were carefully
111. crafted compositions that drew heavily from
112. earlier poetic modes of writing. The following
113. is from Gorgias' Epitapbios:
114. What did these men lack that men should
115. have?
116. And what did they have that men should
117. lack?
118. May what I say be what I sought to say,
119. and what I sought to say what I ought to
120. say—
121. free from the wrath of gods,
122. far from the envy of humanity
123. In Gorgias' surviving prose compositions,
124. one finds the use of alliteration, asyndeton,
125. antithesis, isocolon, parison, homoioteleuton,
126. assorted types of assonance, epanalepsis,
127. paronomasia, rhetorical questions, and the use
128. of compound words, metaphors, and "poetic"
129. terms. Often when contemporary writers use
130. "sophistic Rhetoric" to describe a particular
131. style of composition that is highly experimental or figurative, it is Gorgianic discourse that
132. they have in mind. At the same time, it is worth
133. noting that Gorgias' prose in many respects is
134. clearly rational and logical. For example, he
135. argues using the apagogic method by which an
136. advocate enumerates a series of possibilities and
137. s
138. addresses each in turn. Also, Gorgias uses certain Greek particles as logical connectives in his
139. texts in a way that distinguishes his style and
140. reasoning from that of traditional poetry.
141. We do not have complete speeches
142. by Protagoras that are confidently regarded
143. as authentic. Instead we have two speech
144. re-creations found in Plato's Protagoras and
145. Theaetetus and a series of short aphorisms. One
146. such aphorism declares that there are two contradictory accounts (logoi) possible about everything. A text titled Dissoi Logoi or Dialexeis
147. from around 400 B.C.E. demonstrates the idea
148. by presenting arguments on both sides of a series of topics. Thus, another important practice
149. often referenced as "sophistic Rhetoric" is the
150. idea of acknowledging and enacting a two-sided
151. form of reasoning sometimes called "antilogy"
152. or antilogia. Some contemporary theorists suggest that the practice of dissoi logoi is based
153. on an explicit sophistic or Protagorean theory
154. about discourse and the world. Such a theory
155. is influenced by Heraclitus' declaration that
156. reality itself is made up of conflicting opposites.
157. Just as what is real at any given time is theresult of a battle of opposites, what is understood
158. as true at any given time by humans is theresult of a battle of contradictory discourses.
159. There are a number of theoretical doctrines
160. attributed to the sophists that are sometimes
161. lumped together under the rubric of "sophistic
162. rhetorical theory." However, the Greek word
163. for Rhetoric, rhêtorikê, was not coined until the
164. early fourth century B.C.E. (possibly by Plato)
165. and is not found in any extant text or fragment
166. of the fifth-century sophists. Strictly speaking,
167. it is more historically accurate to call thesesophistic theories of discourse or persuasion than
168. of Rhetoric. Another option is to consider such
169. doctrines implicit or incipient theories of rhetoric, in order to distinguish them from later doctrines that are more direct and explicit efforts
170. to describe and theorize "Rhetoric" as a specific
171. subject.
172. Rhetorical theorists draw upon the sophists' texts for a variety of insights that are relevant to contemporary theories of discourse
173. and composition. For example, many writers
174. have shown great interest in the epistemologi-
175. cal doctrines of the sophists. Protagoras, for
176. example, declared that "humanity is the measure of all things" rather than the gods, and thus
177. he has been characterized as an early humanist.
178. While certain Greek thinkers viewed contradiction and disagreement as a sign of human error,
179. Protagoras saw them as evidence of a contradictory reality. If to one person the wind feels cold
180. and to another it feels not-cold, then is the wind
181. "really" cold or not-cold? Protagoras would say
182. both, while many Greek thinkers, including
183. Plato and Aristotle, would say that such contradiction is impossible—the people disagreeing do
184. not really know what they are talking about.
185. Protagoras has been characterized as a relativist because he would say that the truth of statements about the wind are relative to the person
186. experiencing the wind.
187. Contemporary theorists who stress that
188. "truth" is not an absolute concept and argue
189. that different discourse communities understand the world differently find precedent for
190. their theories in the doctrines attributed
191. to Protagoras. It is worth noting, however,
192. that throughout twentieth-century philosophy,
193. Protagoras has been appropriated by a variety
194. of sometimes conflicting philosophical positions, including positivism, pragmatism,
195. skepticism, phenomenalism, empiricism, utilitarianism, and social-constructionism. It is not
196. surprising, therefore, that very different projects
197. have shared the label of sophistic rhetorical
198. theory.
199. Gorgias is another sophist whose texts
200. have been found to contain numerous explicit
201. or implicit theoretical doctrines. In Helen,
202. Gorgias describes the workings of logos, or
203. speech. Gorgias begins the relevant section by
204. declaring that "Logos is a powerful lord that
205. with the smallest and most invisible bodyaccomplishes most god-like works. It can banish
206. fear and remove grief and instill pleasure and
207. enhance pity. I shall show how this is so." In the
208. process of describing the workings of logos,
209. Gorgias develops an analogy that proved to be
210. very influential: "The power of speech has the
211. same effect on the condition of the psyche as the
212. power of drugs to the nature of the body; for
213. just as different drugs dispel different secretions
214. from the body, and some bring an end to disease and others to life, so also in the case of
215. logos—some bring pain, others pleasure, some
216. bring fear, others instill courage in the hearers,
217. and some drug and bewitch the psyche with a
218. kind of evil persuasion." Based on these and
219. other passages, theorists have identified Gorgi-
220. anic theories of discourse that have been likened
221. to contemporary aesthetic, psychological, and
222. speech-act theories of language.
223. Another influential Gorgianic text is
224. known as On Nature or On Not-Being.
225. Though the original text is lost, Gorgias' arguments have been retold by later writers such
226. as Sextus Empiricus. According to Sextus,
227. "Gorgias says nothing 'exists'; if it exists, it is
228. unknowable; and if it is knowable, it cannot be
229. made evident to others." Though the precise
230. meaning of the Greek word translated here as
231. "exists," or esti, is open to argument, Gorgias'
232. text is a provocative assault or parody of metaphysical writers in general and monists such as
233. Parmenides and Melissus in particular. Gorgias'
234. arguments, though not always formally valid,
235. challenge the reader to rethink in what ways
236. humans come to know and communicate about
237. the world around us. As before, a variety of
238. contemporary interpretations have been offered
239. that position Gorgias as everything from a profound thinker to a radical nihilist, a skeptic to
240. a trivial jokester. Regardless of how Gorgias is
241. interpreted now, it is clear from references in
242. Isocrates and Sextus that Gorgias' arguments
243. about existence and knowledge were widely
244. discussed in classical Greece.
245. Contemporary theorists have postulated
246. all sorts of implicit or explicit theories in Gorgias' various texts, including "theories" of art,
247. style, catharsis, and knowledge. Such claims are
248. potentially misleading, inasmuch as they overestimate the maturity of theory development
249. and imply more coherence and completeness
250. than can be supported with the available evidence. Nonetheless, as in the case of Protagoras,
251. Gorgias' texts serve as inspiration for a variety
252. of contemporary thinkers interested in"sophistic rhetoric."
253. The sophists also are utilized as inspiration
254. for contemporary approaches to pedagogy.
255. Based on evidence that some of the sophists,
256. Protagoras in particular, were supporters of
257. Athenian democracy, some contemporary writers describe "sophistic pedagogy" as empowering students to participate effectively as citizens
258. of the modern polis. Some writers have described "sophistic rhetoric" as pedagogy that is
259. emancipatory, subversive, empowering, or
260. aimed at cultural critique, while others simply
261. contend that the most useful aspect of sophistic teaching was that it aided participation in the
262. democracy. It should be noted that, once again,
263. it is difficult to make generalizations about the
264. sophists' political doctrines as a group. While
265. the evidence is clear that Protagoras was an
266. explicit ally of Periclean democracy, such evidence is not available about all sophists, and
267. some sophists even opposed democracy as a
268. form of government and worked against the
269. democracy in ancient Athens.
270. In sum, it may be helpful to refer to a multitude of "sophistic rhetorics" rather than to
271. posit a singular notion of sophistic rhetoric.
272. Although there has evolved a standardized list
273. of the "older sophists" of the fifth century B.C.E.,
274. the term sophistic has been used to describe a
275. variety of people who have little in common
276. other than being Greek. Even among the sophists on the standardized list, the different practices and doctrines resist easy generalization.
277. The phrase "sophistic rhetoric" has been used
278. to describe a collection of very different rhetorical practices, from a heavy reliance on poetic
279. figures of speech to the development of two-
280. sided debate. It has been used to denote a variety of explicit theories and doctrines about discourse in general and Rhetoric in particular.
281. And, finally, it has been used to describe certain
282. forms of pedagogy that are consonant with
283. participatory democracy.
284. It may be useful to draw a distinction between two types of scholarly projects that engage the texts of the sophists. "Historical reconstruction" is work that attempts to understand
285. the contributions of past theorists and practitioners in their original historical context. A
286. scholar interested in the historical reconstruction of different sophistic rhetorics might try to
287. answer questions such as these: Who were considered "sophists" in fifth-century B.C.E. Greece?
288. In what ways does Plato distinguish between
289. fifth- and fourth-century B.C.E. sophists in his
290. dialogues? How did Protagoras theorize about
291. logos? What were the political implications of
292. the discourse of Hippias? What were the cultural ramifications of Gorgias' version of the
293. traditional Athenian funeral oration? How do
294. fifth-century B.C.E. texts that theorize about
295. logos differ from fourth-century B.C.E. texts that
296. theorize about rhêtorikê?
297. A somewhat different sort of scholarly engagement can be labeled a contemporary appropriation of sophistic texts that is aimed
298. at producing new or neosopbistic rhetorical theory, criticism, or pedagogy. Rather than describe
299. past theories and practices as they evolved
300. through the contingencies of the past, scholars
301. who attempt the contemporary appropriation of
302. classical texts do so to shed light on Rhetorical
303. concerns manifest in the present. A scholar interested in contemporary appropriation for the
304. purposes of describing neosophistic theory, criticism, or pedagogy might try to answer questions
305. ssuch as these: Who are today's "sophists"? Canwe devise a postmodern neosophistic theory of
306. Rhetoric? Which aspects of Gorgias' composition
307. style can inform contemporary composition
308. studies? Can Protagoras' vision of education
309. serve as a useful model today? Such questions
310. direct and develop the concerns of ancient authors in ways probably not anticipated by those
311. authors. Instead, contemporary needs and interests guide the process of selectively appropriating aspects of past authors' texts.
312. As mentioned previously, Gorgias' texts
313. were simultaneously poetic and logical for the
314. time in which they were written. Typically, however, contemporary theorists who champion a
315. "Gorgianic" style tend to highlight the poetic
316. and figurative side and downplay Gorgias' use
317. of systematic reasoning. Such selective appropriation is desirable because a primary goal of
318. neosophistic rhetorical studiesis to chart possible futures of rhetorical theory and practice.
319. Whereas historians may feel obligated to describe aspects of sophistic doctrines that they
320. may find problematic or even obnoxious, neosophistic scholars are free to value those aspects
321. of sophistic rhetorics that they find usefuland
322. explore their potential for future directions in
323. theory, criticism, and pedagogy.
324. Of course, historical reconstruction and
325. contemporary appropriation of sophistic rhetorics are related endeavors. Historians tend to be
326. drawn to the study of the sophists for contemporary reasons that inform their interpretations
327. (such as an interest in style), and contemporary
328. theorists often depend on the work of historians
329. to guide their understanding of sophistic texts.
330. Sometimes the line between these two types of
331. scholarship is hard to draw. Nonetheless, the
332. methods, goals, and values associated with historical reconstruction and contemporary appropriation can be delineated with sufficient clarity
333. that scholars may find it useful to distinguish
334. between historical studies of the various sophisticRhetoricsand the advancement of neosophistic
335. Rhetorical theory, criticism, and pedagogy.
