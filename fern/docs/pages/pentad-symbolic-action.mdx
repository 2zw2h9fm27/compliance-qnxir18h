Campbell, George (1719–1796)
Author of The Philosophy of Rhetoric (1776), an
important eighteenth-century British psychological-epistemological rhetoric. Since its appearance, The Philosophy of Rhetoric has generally
been praised by critics and is often ranked as the
most important treatise among the "new" rhetorics of the Scottish Enlightenment. The book,
popular in the British Isles, was also translated
into German (1791) and had a great influence in
America throughout the nineteenth century.
Although early commentators debated
whether the work was basically a "classical" or
"modern" rhetoric, recent scholarship has
placed its philosophical foundations in the Enlightenment. As a minister, Campbell through
his philosophical writings sought to combat
epistemological and religious skepticism, and
his rhetorical writings were primarily aimed at
arming ministers and theologians for defending
the Christian religion against atheists.
Although Campbell had sketched out some
ideas on rhetoric as a minister at Banchory-Ternan
(1748–1757), the body of the work consists of
discourses presented to the famous Aberdeen
Philosophical Society (1758–1773), of which he
was a founding member. (Some of these original
discourses came to light in 1982.) Campbell's discussions in this club with Thomas Reid, James
Beattie, and Alexander Gerard, among others,
helped mold his ideas on philosophy. Campbell
and his colleagues published works that established the common-sense school of philosophy in
opposition to David Hume's skeptical system.
Moreover, the group also shared a philosophical outlook, goal, and method. One goal of
their society was to create a philosophy of the
human mind. The method was to be Reid's slow,
patient method of induction. As avowed empiricists, they wanted to apply the Baconian-Newtonian method to the study of human nature.
Moral philosophy would not progress until a careful empirical study of psychology had been carried
out. In his rhetoric Campbell classifies rhetoric as
a branch of psychology. To understand rhetorical
principles, one must know psychology; to understand psychology, one must proceed inductively in
examining the faculties and powers of the mind.
Hence, Campbell's purpose in the Philosophy is
"to exhibit a tolerable sketch of the human
ture, to ascertain . . . the radical principles of that
art [that is, rhetoric]." Here, then, we see rhetoric in the service of the study of human nature or
the philosophy of the mind.
The moderns, Campbell held, had made
little or no improvement over the ancients in
discovering the rules of composition; but a true
philosophy of rhetoric would enter a "new
country"—a study of the principles of human
nature that explains why the "rules" work. In
elucidating the relationship between the principles of rhetoric and the human mind, he employs such common eighteenth-century topics
as sympathy, the association of ideas, and faculty psychology.
Aside from several chapters in Book I
(which present a doctrine of evidence), the
remainder of the treatise is basically a typical
belletristic rhetoric of the period. Belletristic
characteristics in Campbell's work are (1) his
broad definition of eloquence to include poetry
and all forms of imaginative literature; (2) his
lack of any treatment of memory or delivery; (3)
his emphasis on elocution (Books II and III treat
style or verbal criticism), a canon that is common to both speakers and writers; and (4) his
inclusion of topics normally treated in poetic
theory, such as wit, humor, ridicule, verisimilitude, taste, and sublimity.
Campbell's famous definition of eloquence
as "that art or talent by which discourse is
adapted to its end" and his classification of the
five ends of speaking addressed to the four faculties of the mind (the understanding, the imagination, the passions, or the will) was worked out
in a theological club in the 1740s. He held to this
definition in both his Philosophy of Rhetoric and
in his posthumously published Lectures onPulpit Eloquence (1807). This "expanded" definition of rhetoric (versus the classical definition of
rhetoric as persuasion) had a great effect on the
teaching of composition and public speaking in
America well into the twentieth century.
In 1840 Campbell's opera omnia appeared
in six volumes; in that edition the Philosophy
of Rhetoric makes up about 16 percent of his
publications.
Dennis R. Bormann
University of Nebraska (Lincoln)

198. Cassirer, Ernst (1874–1945)
199. German Neo-Kantian philosopher whose work
200. on the theory of "symbolic forms" helped to
201. shape the arguments of modern semiotics, the
202. study of signs. In The Philosophy of Symbolic
203. Forms (1923–1929), An Essay on Man (1944),
204. and other influential books, Cassirer elaborates
205. a theory of knowledge as cultural artifact and
206. a theory of culture as semiotic artifact, examining the diverse modes of symbolic action,
207. including language, myth, science, history, art,
208. and religion, in terms of which culture and
209. knowledge are formed. Cassirer regards symbolic action as the essence of consciousness,
210. where the production of meaning through the
211. coalescence of subject and object, perceptual
212. matter and conceptual structure, is enabled by
213. the mediation of signs. Human experience, the
214. substance of culture, is a web of symbolic practices and evolving meanings, a product of imaginative energies, a range of prerational, common-sense, practical, and theoretical
215. knowledge comprising both the "everyday life"
216. of historically situated groups of people and
217. also their intellectual, moral, and spiritual
218. understandings.
219. Cassirer is "Neo-Kantian" in accepting the
220. "idealist" premise that a priori intellectual principles and operations account for "experience."
221. But he promotes what he calls "anthropological
222. philosophy," modifying Kant's "critique of reason" (which had assumed an ahistorical andrestricted view of mind as discursive rationality) by
223. appeal to the diversity of symbolic forms that
224. emerge, interrelate, and supplant each other in the
225. continuing production of meaning within history.
226. Hence, although he writes extensively (as Kant
227. had done) about science as the most fully evolved
228. form of consciousness, he also explores alternative forms, notably language and myth, that present other dimensions of the "human spirit"
229. through their own distinctive modes of operation.
230. Cassirer's intellectual values are rooted in the
231. liberal tradition of German humanism, powerfully
232. influenced by the philosophical idealism of Marburg (where he studied under Hermann Cohen,
233. a great nineteenth-century authority on Kant), and

238. the production of culture through forms of symbolic action entails a continuing process of human
239. self-realization, a perpetual liberating of the "spirit." Cassirer explores the interpretive nature of
240. knowing and the concrete functions of symboliza-
241. tion in order to explain the power of expressivity
242. to actualize human potential and thereby realize
243. human freedom. His work, taken together, is a
244. systematic overview of that power: the capacity of
245. myth to articulate an animate, passionate world
246. in which human feelings, desires, and fears are objectified, through symbols, as responsive agencies

248. experience, the syntactically structured universe of
249. objects, actions, causalities, and predications that
250. constitute ordinary life; the capacity of science to
251. articulate an austerely rational world of abstract
252. relationships. The symbolic practices that compose these worlds are characterized by differing
253. principles of operation, but they have equal value
254. as "organs of reality," producing the variety of
255. knowledge in response to the variety of human
256. need and aspiration.

269. A fifteenth-century Italian aristocrat who
270. served under the dukes of Mantua, Milan, and

272. and served under three popes. Castiglione's

279. He died at Toledo in 1529 from a violent fever.
280. Castiglione's greatest achievement was his Il
281. Libro del Cortegiano (Book of the Courtier)
282. which, along with Machiavelli's Frince, enjoyed
283. an enduring influence over court life in all of Renaissance Europe. The book purports to be Symposium-like discussions, held at Urbino under the
284. direction of Duchess Elizabetta Gonzaga and
285. with the participation of noblemen and court ladies, concerning what qualities —talents, arts, and
286. moral character—constitute the perfect courtier.
287. Castiglione's nobles engage in an epistemic
288. rhetoric wherein the merits of noble or common
289. birth are debated as seriously as skill in the military and the fine arts. The concept of sprezzatura
290. (nonchalance) is central to this discussion, and refers to the courtier's need to make his studied accomplishments seem effortless. Much is made
291. of the fact that these four evenings of exchange are
292. designated as a "game" by the assembly. The
293. dicussions of both the perfect courtier and the perfect court lady allow the participants to make
294. oblique political and social criticisms of their peers
295. and the many foreign and ecclesiastical influences
296. upon them. The discussion of the court lady challenges the inherent misogyny of the period, just as
297. the commentary on the ideal courtier allows criticism of courtiership in general—a common topic
298. at Renaissance courts. The goal of the courtier, according to this assembly, should be to practice an
299. aesthetic contemplation of beauty—particularly
300. that of court ladies—while maintaining personal
301. distance. This contemplation of beauty evokes
302. Plato's contemplation of the Forms. Reversing
303. Machiavelli, Castiglione's courtier becomes adviser and mentor to his prince. As is often the case
304. with Italian Renaissance writers, an inverted political and social system is suggested, examined,
305. and "played." Whether this game is amusement,
306. subversive exercise, or catharsis is never clear.

308. over the past decade are either literary or historical, although the complexity and ambiguity

310. rhetoric. Its formal elements make the book a

319. assembled nobility. The Book of the Courtier
320. becomes a study of the uses of political rhetoric, a feature that extends its value beyond being a mere variation on Renaissance courtesy
