Declamation
"Practical exercises" in oratory (Greek melete;
Roman declamatio), originally incorporated
into the curricula of Greek and Roman rhetoric schools in order to prepare students for future positions as advocate-politicians; introduced as early as the fourth centuryB.C.E. and
prominent through the Roman and Hellenistic
periods.
Declamations typically began with the
teacher, or master rhetor, proposing a theme or
cause (case). This was sometimes a general
statement, or thesis, such as "It is better for a
man to marry," and sometimes a more specific hypothesis, like "John was right to marry
Christine," in which names and other details were added. Sometimes the teacher added
colores, details, in order to make a theme more
complicated, interesting, or familiar, or simply
to add fresh possibilities to a stale theme. The
teacher introduced each theme with a sermo—
a preliminary outline of the situation or case—
which included the roles of relevant characters,
the principal arguments to be employed
(including commonplaces), developments to
which it might be susceptible, and potential
pitfalls. Frequently, he would perform a "model" declamation for his students to imitate.
Students then composed and delivered their
speeches to an audience of their classmates,
sometimes in the form of mock trials, after
which the teacher would critique their performances. Often, an exercise known as prosopopoeiae was included in declamations, requiring that students declaim in the character
of the person for whom they were speaking—
usually a mythological or historical personage
at some critical point in life. Because students
were required to attack or defend
theses (sometimes both), declamation was often criticized for failing to instruct students in
the moral and intellectual responsibilities of
the orator. Respected teachers of rhetoric like
Quintilian (see, for example, Institutio II.x.l-
6), Tacitus, and Cicero, however, defended
declamation as a potentially valuable exercise,
but only to the degree that it cultivated general
moral development and remained connected to
public oratory.
Although declamation continued to be
taught in the East through the Byzantine period,
it was particularly influential in Rome, where
it acquired prestige and popularity toward the
close of the Republic, and remained important
until the fall of the empire. Here, however, declamation became a form of "school rhetoric"
that became increasingly dissociated from practical oratory. The courts of the Greek demos
and the Roman Republic had provided environments where free speech thrived, but, under
the dictatorship of the Roman Empire, freedom
of discussion in the forum and in the schools
ceased; consequently, so did the need for orators
trained in rhetoric. Indeed, the very real threat
of physical abuse, exile, and even assassination
effectively deterred those who desired to speak
against the emperor on current or controversial
issues (such as tyranny, a stock theme in classical declamations), thus forcing rhetoric and
oratory to find new contexts and, ultimately,
new forms. Under these restrictions, rhetors and
their students turned increasingly to fictitious
themes, or scholastica, wildly improbable, fantastic, and romantic situations involving conflicting laws and shocking crimes such as adultery, rape, incest, murder, and homosexuality.
Many of these themes are collected in the elder
Seneca's work, Suasoriae et Controversiae (Deliberative and Forensic Themes), which records
different treatments of the same themes and the
author's own criticism.
Because "safe" worlds about which to
declaim literally had to be constructed from
scratch out of the imaginations of those dedicated to practicing oratory, invention became a
valuable skill during this period. But invention
as investigation was largely supplanted by invention as imagination, and rhetoric was increasingly associated with poetic. Moreover,
rhetoric and oratory were becoming separate
fields of study: The former focused on law and
philosophy, the latter on style and delivery.
Historians have said that this difference marks
the shift from an "old" to a "new" rhetoric.
Within this context, declamatory skill was cultivated for amusement, as a social grace, with
no necessary connection to historical facts, public law courts, or contemporary realities. And
as long as declamations offered occasions to
discourse persuasively (and entertainingly) on
controversial and complex situations, it seemed
irrelevant that they also involved the acceptance
of logical impossibilities, perversions of history,
and nonexistent laws.
Orators responded in a variety of ways
to this new "style" of oratory: Some lapsed
into the "theatrical display or insane raving" that Quintilian feared in Institutio Oratoria (II.x.8); others continued to promote the
intellectual activity for which declamation
was originally designed. Still others rented
halls and began holding public exhibitions,
hoping to attract students with their virtuosity. This trend toward public display further popularized declamation and motivated
many teachers of oratory to open their doors
to the public several days each year, effectively turning schools into auditoriums. Declamation became an end initself,
unconnected to the law courts and without
much substance generally.
Many critics have noted that declamation
is proof that style, when cultivated in artificial
isolation (that is, without connection to
thought), goes bad. They denounce it for
its lack of interest in the development of the
"whole man," for its perversion of the educational process, and for its general cultivation of
mediocrity. But in spite of such negative criticism, declamation continued to be used in the
West through much of the medieval period. (St.
Augustine, however, ignored it inDe Doctrina
Christiana.) Its emphasis on display found a
counterpart in the eighteenth-century elocutionary movement, and its themes survive in composition handbooks from the eighteenth and
nineteenth centuries, as well as in handbooks
that teach "extemporaneous speaking."
Elizabeth Ervin
University of North Carolina at Wilmington

36. Deconstruction
37. Both a critique of the Western metaphysical tradition and a defense of the plurality of
38. meaning. The term deconstruction possesses
39. both a philosophical dimension and a rhetorical dimension. The philosophical dimension
40. derives from the writings of the French philosopher Jacques Derrida, and the rhetorical
41. dimension may be associated most directly
42. with American and French discourse theorists
43. who apply Derrida's language philosophy to
44. the study of literary texts. As a philosophical
45. concept, deconstruction constitutes a thoroughgoing critique of essentialism, or what
46. Derrida calls "the metaphysics of presence"; as
47. a rhetorical concept, deconstruction challenges
48. the notion of unitary meaning and stresses
49. instead the intertextuality that derives from the
50. inherently rhetorical nature—the rhetoricity—
51. of all texts. Although both the philosophical and the rhetorical dimensions of deconstruction share a common and often difficult
52. vocabulary, the differences between these
53. dimensions are important, for the American
54. rhetorical version of deconstruction—represented best by the Yale School—suggests a
55. reading strategy or interpretive methodology
56. that does not align precisely with Derrida's
57. conception of deconstruction.
58. In his critique of the Western metaphysical tradition, Derrida follows and perhaps
59. ends a line of inquiry represented best by
60. philosophers such as G.F.W. Hegel,Friedrich
61. Nietzsche, Edmund Husserl, and Martin Heidegger. Derrida attacks the presupposition that
62. language—and, consequently, meaning—possesses a metaphysical presence, essence, or center that provides a stable structure or foundation on which we can confidently construct
63. our judgments and beliefs about the world.
64. Reduced to its bare bones, deconstruction constitutes a demonstration that this metaphysics
65. of presence contains within itself certain contradictions, or aporias, that lead to its own
66. undoing. Derrida develops this line of thinking in two seminal books, Of Grammatology
67. and Dissemination, and in two collections of
68. essays, Writing and Difference and Margins of
69. Philosophy. In these works Derrida employs
70. three key concepts that convey the philosophical project of deconstruction: logocentrism,
71. differance, and the supplement.
72. For Derrida, the Western philosophical tradition has been dominated by the search for a
73. metaphysical order that possesses at its center
74. an enduring structure of reason, meaning, or
75. essential truth. Derrida employs the term logocentrism to refer to the presupposition that a
76. Word, or logos, exists that will stabilize all
77. thought and inquiry. According to Derrida, all
78. the myriad branches of Western philosophy
79. partake of logocentrism, the wistful metaphysical desire for the final Word. As the determination of being as presence, logocentrism always
80. embodies a ground or foundation that privileges certain key concepts while dismissing or
81. negating others. As Derrida points out, philosophers in the Western philosophical tradition
82. have privileged good before evil, the positive
83. before the negative, the pure before the impure,
84. the simple before the complex, the essential
85. before the accidental, and the imitated before
86. the imitation. These hierarchies, in turn, establish a binary relation based on negation. In the
87. relation permanence/change, for example, permanence represents a positive concept while
88. change represents a negative one, so entities that
89. manifest permanence are naturally privileged
90. over those entities that change. Logocentrism,
91. then, privileges the positive terms in such hierarchical oppositions while treating the inferior
92. second term as the positive term's negation.
93. Consequently, logocentrism may be understood
94. to be the metaphysical presupposition that a
95. stabilizing word, center, or presence exists that
96. enables us to distinguish confidently the differences between things that are positive or good
97. and those things that are negative or bad. In
98. other words, logocentrism corresponds to the
99. desire for an enduring structure or presence that
100. will put an end to difference and that will enable us to locate—within its oppositional hierarchy of positive/negative or good/bad terms—
101. all that can be known.
102. Derrida attacks the logocentric tradition
103. by turning the logic of logocentrism against
104. itself. Instead of understanding presence as a
105. metaphysical foundation, system, or logos that
106. overcomes difference, Derrida argues that
107. presence and therefore logocentrism actually
108. depends on difference, or what he calls différance. In French, the term différance sounds the
109. same as différence, and the -ance ending shows
110. up only in writing. In writing, différance takes
111. on the condition of a pun, where the word
112. means both to differ and to defer. According
113. to Derrida, "Différance is the systematic play
114. of differences, of traces of differences, of the
115. spacing by which elements relate to one another. This spacing is the production, simultaneously active and passive (the a of différance
116. indicates this indecision as regards activity and
117. passivity, that which cannot yet be governed
118. and organized by that opposition), of intervals
119. without which the 'full' terms could not signify, could not function" (Positions, 38–39).
120. This definition requires a great deal of unpacking, but on its most fundamental level, Derrida's explanation of différance makes two
121. important distinctions about language, one
122. temporal and the other spatial. On the one
123. hand, a word or sign stands for something else
124. and, obviously, comes after what it designates;
125. the sign temporally succeeds its referent or
126. Ddiffers from it in time. On the other hand, thesign also constitutes a "space" between itself
127. and its referent that defers meaning from the
128. referent to the sign.Différance, then, refers
129. both to temporal difference and to spatial deferral. Without this différance, no signification
130. or meaning would be possible, because the
131. very nature of signification depends on difference and deferral.
132. In this configuration, however, the term
133. différance incorporates a troubling paradox.
134. Because it names nothing that is positive—only
135. that which is deferred—différance signifies
136. something that cannot be named; it signifies a
137. quality that is missing within the act of signification. Therefore, différance possesses no
138. negative member, in the sense that the term
139. names only a gap or absence that possesses no
140. referent. Of course, a sign without a referent
141. is no sign at all, and we know that, on one
142. level, the word différance certainly signifies
143. this gap, absence, or condition of nonrefer-
144. ence. On another level, however, différance
145. points to an aporia that characterizes the
146. condition of every sign. At once,différance
147. signifies both the presence of something—a
148. gap, absence, or condition of nonreference—
149. and its absence, and this condition of non-
150. reference is pure negation without anything to
151. which it can stand as a positive term. Derrida
152. argues that the paradox embodied indifférance—the presence of absence—denotes the
153. situation of language itself. As a system of
154. relations constituted by difference and deferral, language possesses no foundation, essential structure, or intrinsic presence, so logo-
155. centrism's raison d'etre—the presupposition
156. that a presence exists that will stabilize meaning and provide a framework for understanding—evaporates.
157. A good example that demonstrates
158. how différance undermines logocentrism is
159. Derrida's formulation of the supplement. Like
160. the concept ofdifférance, the supplement
161. possesses two contradictory meanings. First,
162. a supplement is something inessential that
163. is added on to something else; for example,
164. Rousseau thought that writing was a supplement to speech, something inessential that
165. is added on to speech and that is secondary
166. to speech. However, and here is the second sense
167. of the term, a supplement compensates for
168. a lack in the thing that it completes. In the
169. case of speech and writing, for example,
170. Derrida points out in Of Grammatology that
171. something must be lacking in speech if it
172. requires the supplement of writing. Because
173. the supplement always designates something
174. that is incomplete, speech cannot be complete
175. if it requires writing as its supplement. In
176. fact, because speech exists as only one use
177. of language that supplements other uses, speech
178. itself may be seen as a supplement to writing and to other forms of language within a process of endless supplementarity. As a result
179. of this endless supplementarity, the logocentric
180. desire for a stable presence or foundation
181. for meaning can never be realized because
182. language exists as an endless chain of supplementarity where one aspect of language,
183. such as speech, only supplements all the others.
184. Nothing lies outside our utterances and our
185. texts except other utterances and texts that
186. stand in a supplementary relation to one another. In the realm of meaning, what we encounter is a generalized intertextuality where
187. our utterances and our texts refer only to other
188. utterances and texts.
189. From a rhetorical perspective, de-
190. construction extends Derrida's critique of the
191. metaphysics of presence into the realm of
192. textual interpretation. In its rhetorical dimension, deconstruction stresses the indeterminacy
193. of textual meaning through an analysis of the
194. oppositional hierarchies that provide the text
195. with its aura of stability and closure.
196. Although Derrida carefully disassociates
197. himself from any critical or rhetorical methodology, deconstruction in America—especially the version of deconstruction
198. promulgated by Paul de Man and some members of the Yale School—nonetheless embodies a general and codifiable procedure that is
199. frequently employed to reveal a text's plenitude of meaning.
200. This general procedure embodies two
201. rhetorical moves: First, deconstructionists
202. isolate the hierarchical binary oppositions
203. within a text that give a text its stability or
204. presence, and, second, they destabilize or
205. deconstruct these oppositions by seeking out
206. the aporias that these oppositions suppress.
207. By revealing the aporias that every text suppresses, the deconstructionist produces a new
208. reading or interpretation for the text that is
209. supported by the text itself. It is important to
210. note that the deconstructionist does not bring
211. to the interpretive act a predetermined framework of understanding into which the text is
212. inserted. Rather, for the deconstructionist, the
213. text unravels itself and supplies alternative
214. readings within its own language. In other
215. words, the text is self-deconstructive. The
216. interpretive act, then, is interminable, for one
217. reading simply yields another reading that
218. becomes itself the object of deconstruc-
219. tive analysis. To deconstruct a text therefore
220. means to dismantle the oppositions and hierarchies that give a text its aura of stability in
221. order to make known that which the text
222. conceals. Such a move allows the deconstruc-
223. tionist to reinscribe the text within a different
224. order or meaning.
225. One of the results of this interminable
226. overturning of textual meaning is the a-
227. wareness of the rhetoricity inherent in all texts.
228. As deconstruction decenters the text to uncover that which the text seeks to conceal, it
229. also reveals the rhetorical operations that give
230. a text its sense of presence and stability and,
231. in so doing, blurs the traditional boundary between the critical act and the creative act. Traditionally, critical analysis has been seen to be
232. parasitic on other texts; that is, a specific instance of critical analysis can occur only if
233. there first exists another text to analyze. Because the critical act has been regarded as
234. parasitic on and secondary to the creation of
235. texts, the creative act of fashioning new meaning has been privileged in our culture over the
236. supposedly less creative analytic act of uncovering meaning. If, however, all texts—including critical texts—are ultimately undecidable
237. and therefore susceptible to plural interpretations, as deconstruction insists, then no clear
238. boundary may be established between the critical text and the creative text. Both kinds of
239. texts engage in the meaning-making process,
240. and both ultimately resist closure and reduction to one stable interpretation. Because the
241. critic and the artist engage in similar activities,
242. the critic does not discover or uncover meaning; the critic, like the artist, creates meaning
243. within the critical text, a text—like the literary
244. text—that harbors the aporias and unstable
245. oppositions that lead to its own inevitable
246. deconstruction.
247. By revealing the fissures, uncertainties, and
248. gaps latent in every text's structure of meaning
249. and thereby exposing the rhetoricity inherent in
250. every text, deconstruction reveals the futility
251. inherent in the effort to discover an epistemo-
252. logical system that will stop the play of interpretation and stabilize meaning. But, because the
253. Western tradition of rhetoric has been dominated and even defined by the search for just
254. such an epistemological system, we have been
255. historically conditioned to yearn for a methodology or critical technique that will fix meaning and provide an end to interpretation. By
256. disclosing the futility of this logocentric search
257. for a timeless center, deconstruction reveals to
258. us our historical condition and helps us to
259. understand why we yearn for hermeneutical
260. certainty.
261. Of course, just as we cannot escape our
262. histories, neither can texts escape theirs. Texts
263. cannot escape the structures of meaning imposed upon them by their historical situations, and so every text reiterates its hierarchical historical condition. For this reason,
264. deconstruction does not correspond to a new
265. New Criticism or to a formalist interpretive
266. methodology of close reading, as some critics
267. of deconstruction contend. Derrida, PaulDe
268. Man, Geoffrey Hartman, and J. Hillis Miller,
269. among others, have taken great pains to point
270. out that deconstruction does not convert the
271. text into an autonomous artifact that yields
272. up its meaning after it undergoes a close textual analysis; rather, they point out that all
273. texts participate in a process of intertextuality
274. where meaning is always already connected
275. to other texts and to other hierarchies of historical understanding. To say, for example,
276. that every text deconstructs itself does not
277. mean that history, culture, and inter subjectivity are irrelevant to the act of interpretation.
278. In fact, deconstruction acknowledges the
279. truism that every text is embedded in historical, cultural, and intersubjective relations and
280. that no text can escape these complex structures of meaning. By laying bare this
281. rhetoricity—this continual historical, cultural, and intersubjective reshaping of meaning—deconstruction does not sanction a
282. version of formalism but, instead, refutes formalisms of all sorts.
283. In both its philosophical dimension
284. and its rhetorical dimension, deconstruction
285. challenges and subverts the structures we
286. employ to explain ourselves to ourselves.
287. In this enterprise deconstruction demonstrates the complexity of signification and the
288. inevitability of interpretation, and it continually calls into question the Western compulsion to discover an essential core of human
289. being.
