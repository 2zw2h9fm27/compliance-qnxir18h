Bitzer, Lloyd F. (b. 1931)
Professor of Communication Arts, University of
Wisconsin, Madison; B.S. 1955 Southern Illinois University; M.A. 1957 Southern Illinois
University; Ph.D. 1962 University of Iowa.
In his famous 1968 essay "The Rhetorical Situation," Lloyd F. Bitzer argues that
rhetoric is inspired and conditioned by a complex set of elements that compose the rhetorical situation. Specifically, he claims that the
elements of exigence, audience, and constraints inspire and determine the appropriateness of discourse. Bitzer posits that a rhetorical situation has structure and organization
and exists independently of the discourseapplied to it. The situation, according to Bitzer,
is real, objective, "publicly observable," and
historically actual (11). Situation generates a
need for discourse that can inspire mediating
action in an audience capable of instigating
change. In Bitzer's theory, discourse presupposes the existence of situation.
Bitzer defines exigence, audience, and
constraints. Exigence is "an imperfection
marked by urgency" (6); it is alterable orresolvable by appropriate discourse. The audience must be able to influence and effect
change within the parameters of a specific rhetorical situation and is therefore "capable of
being influenced by discourse" (8). Constraints
can influence the rhetor's discourse choices
and the audience's reactions. Constraints are
motives, belief systems, prejudices, and so
forth, that act as obstacles to resolution. Although a situation in Bitzer's view exists objectively, it must be recognized as requiring discourse, or, in most circumstances, it will
deteriorate.
In a 1973 article in Philosophy andRhetoric, Bitzer's theory was challenged by scholar
Richard E. Vatz, who argued that discourse
generates situation and that the rhetor imbues
any situation with meaning through discourse.
According to Vatz's relativistic argument, situation can never exist before discourse.
Bitzer's later essays "Rhetoric and Public Knowledge" (1978) and "Functional Communication: A Situational Approach" (1980)
develop his situational theory. "Public Knowledge" examines the nature of public representation—public as it relates to the speaker and
the audience. A public worthy of representation
is a group whose communication is based in a
shared "set of truths and values" (83). Speaker
and audience can effectively represent this public when they share these fundamental concerns.
In "Functional Communication" Bitzer expands primarily on exigence and the development of situations in relation to both physical
and mental environments. Although Bitzer allows that exigence can exist as an objective
condition, its perceived urgency is based on the
degree of interest felt by speaker and audience—
areas over which these two bodies sometimes
disagree. Bitzer determines that four phases of
situational development exist, from initial genesis of its components—exigence, audience, and
constraints—to final dissolution, at which point
a situation is no longer changeable. In these two
essays, Bitzer's situational theory approaches
maturity.
In addition, Bitzer is a scholar of eighteenth-century rhetoric, particularly of the theories of Richard Whately and George Campbell.
In "Whately's Distinction Between Inferring
and Proving" (1992), Bitzer examines in depth
Whately's discourse theory and the scholarly
research surrounding it. Bitzer's introduction to
the 1988 edition of Campbell's Philosophy of
Rhetoric provides a lengthy historical analysis
of Campbell's rhetorical theory.
Bitzer's contributions to both contemporary and eighteenth-century rhetoric are invaluable resources for modern scholars.
Leslie Dupont
University of Arizona

350. The most important work by Boethius on
351. rhetoric is a treatise on logic,De differentiis
352. topicis (c. 522), also known as Topica Boetii.
353. Here Boethius focuses on so-called topical logic,

356. treatise, Boethius analyzes and compares different classical systems of topical invention, including Aristotle's and Cicero's. In the last
357. book, he turns to the theoretical issue implicitly
358. raised by this borrowing of rhetorical topics for
359. philosophical inquiry, namely, the exact boundaries between philosophy and rhetoric. Boethius
360. holds that philosophical argument, or dialectic,
361. deals with general questions (theses) while rhetorical argument deals with specific instances
362. (hypotheses). Dialectic employs complete syllogisms that can be examined in dialogue with an
363. adversary; rhetoric employs truncated syllogisms that are presented persuasively to the
364. audience.
365. These distinctions subordinate rhetoric to
366. dialectic. Rhetoric becomes a means of applying general rules of argumentation, established
367. by dialectic, to specific cases. Rhetorical argument has no epistemological force of its own.
368. Boethius goes even further than Aristotle in
369. denying rhetoric a separate knowledge-generating status. Boethius thus works against the
370. Ciceronian tradition of classical rhetoric, an
371. approach that treats language and knowledge
372. as interrelated and thus affords rhetoric some
373. parity with philosophy.
374. The Aristotelian approach proved attractive to later medieval scholars in the universities, which were dominated by the dialectical
375. study of theology and law. As Boethius appears
376. to advocate, rhetoric takes a preliminary and
377. subordinate place in the medieval university
378. curriculum. Book IV of Topica Boetii (which
379. has not been translated into English) was often
380. used alone as a textbook on rhetoric. The most
381. important classical source of this work is the
382. Rbetorica ad Herennium. Boethius uses its terminology and shares its emphasis on invention,
383. barely mentioning style, memory, and delivery,
384. and giving the reader very little sense of how
385. rhetoric might actually be used to affect an audience. His aim is to account for rhetoric, not
386. to explain it, treating it in a series of definitions
387. that analyze its parts.

402. University of Chicago, 1947,1950 (in English).

407. Booth's work has a remarkable coherence—remarkable given its wide range of subject matters: fictional technique, irony, metaphor, modernist philosophy, critical pluralism,
408. teaching, the ethics of fiction. The coherence
409. derives from his consistent rhetorical perspective, one that takes rhetoric as both a practice
410. and a mode of analysis. As practice, it is the use
411. of signs to effect a meeting of minds and, ultimately, a community; as a mode of analysis, it
412. is the effort to understand the means by which
413. such meetings can come about. Moreover,
414. Booth believes that rhetorical study can make
415. a difference in our daily lives: The more we
416. know about the workings and effects of our discourse, the more we can use it for the common
417. good.
418. The Rhetoric of Fiction(1961) argues that
419. abstract rules about fictional technique (showing is good, telling is bad) are inadequate.
420. Booth demonstrates that different techniques
421. create different effects and serve different purposes, and that different purposes can inform
422. effective fiction. In attending to the relationships among techniques, effects, and purposes,
423. Booth joins his discussion of rhetoric in fiction
424. with a treatment of fiction as rhetoric. Along
425. the way, he develops his most widely used
426. terms and concepts: implied author—the image of the flesh-and-blood author created
427. through the text; reliable narrator—one whose
428. values and beliefs are endorsed by the implied
429. author; unreliable narrator—one whose values
430. and beliefs diverge from those of the implied
431. author.
432. A Rhetoric of Irony(1974) analyzes how
433. authors and readers do the complex dance of
434. ironic communication. Booth divides irony
435. into two main types:(1) stable, in which an
436. author covertly signals the reader to reject the
437. text's literal meaning and to reconstruct a
438. new, fixed and finite, meaning; and(2) unstable, in which the author intends everyreconstruction itself to be subject to further
439. undermining. Booth's discussion of this second type is at once disappointing and fascinating, because unstable irony works against
440. the full convergence of minds that Booth values so highly.
441. Modern Dogma and the Rhetoric ofAssent (1974) asks how speakers are able to
442. change listeners' minds, and under what conditions listeners should change their minds. Arguing against the fact-value split, Booth identifies
443. multiple sources of appeals to change belief and
444. multiple warrants for assent: logical, emotive,
445. ethical—each of which can take different forms
446. in different discourses. Moreover, Booth's investigation leads him to propose that we change
447. one of our entrenched intellectual habits.
448. Rather than doubting a new speaker until given
449. strong reasons not to, we should assent until
450. given warrants to doubt.
451. Critical Understanding (1979) applies the
452. rhetoric of assent to literary criticism. Booth
453. seeks a philosophically justifiable pluralism that
454. would reduce critical warfare by valuing multiple questions and recognizing that at least two
455. answers to the same question could be fully
456. adequate—while also regarding some questions
457. and answers as unproductive or inadequate.
458. After examinations of the pluralisms of R.S.
459. Crane, M.H. Abrams, and Kenneth Burke,
460. Booth concludes that the philosophical quest
461. has failed. But he argues that pluralism's potential to reduce warfare is so great that this failure is less important than pluralism's promise.
462. So he develops a pluralism founded on a commitment to three interlocking values: vitality,
463. justice, and, the greatest of the three, understanding.
464. In The Company We Keep (1988), Booth
465. employs the metaphor of reading as friendship
466. to investigate the ethical dimensions of literature. Just as some friends are good influences
467. and others dangerous, so too with books. Booth
468. rejects the notion that ethical judgments of
469. works of fiction can be made by distilling their
470. moral messages. Instead, he contends that any
471. book extends to its readers a diverse group of
472. invitations, and the quality of the life we live
473. while responding to those invitations determines its ethical quality. The friendship metaphor also leads Booth to give new emphasis to
474. the social character of reading through his attention to what he calls coduction: the process
475. by which our conversations about books influence our experiences and our evaluations of our
476. friends.
477. Booth's first collection of essays, Now
478. Don't Try to Reason with Me (1970), shows his
479. skills as a public speaker and especially as an
480. ironist. The second collection, The Vocation of
481. a Teacher (1988), provides a portrait of the
482. rhetorician as teacher and an implied pedagogical program based on careful attention to the
483. acts of reading, writing, speaking, and listening.
484. Especially noteworthy in Vocation are Booth's
485. "Teacher's Journal" and "The Idea of a University—as Seen by a Rhetorician." The Harper
486. and Row Rhetoric (1984) and The Harper
487. and Row Reader (1984), done with Marshall
488. Gregory, show how Booth's general commitments as a teacher give shape to his ideas about
489. teaching writing. His most recent offering of
490. friendship is an edited collection, The Art of
491. Growing Older (1992), representations andreflections on aging.

518. Bormann, Ernest G. (b. 1925)
519. Contributed to symbolic convergence theory
520. and developed fantasy theme analysis.
521. Bormann, a professor of Speech Communication at the University of Minnesota, received his
522. B.A. from the University of South Dakota and
523. his M.A. and Ph.D. from Iowa State University.
524. He has taught at a number of institutions, including the University of South Dakota, Eastern Illinois University, and Florida State University. He has written books on research methods
525. in communication studies, but he is best known
526. for his work in symbolic convergence theory
527. and fantasy theme analysis.
528. Bormann first introduced fantasy theme
529. analysis in his 1972 article "Fantasy and Rhetorical Vision." Fantasy theme analysis grew out of
530. his work with the small-group communication
531. seminar at Minnesota, a project that produced
532. a large body of data about small-group communication and dynamics. Bormann found the key
533. to understanding that data in the work of Robert Bales. Bales's book Personality and Interper-
534. sonal Behavior describes how group fantasizing
535. emerges within the context of small-group dynamics. The group "dramatizes" when certain
536. messages are sent. The tempo of conversation
537. increases. People interrupt one another or complete one another's sentences. People may blush
538. or laugh, emerging from self-conscious roles.
539. Members of the group begin swapping stories.
540. Tension is released, and the members of the
541. group feel a sense of community or common
542. culture. In his later works, Bormann calls this
543. process of dramatizing in small-group communication "symbolic convergence."
544. These dramatizing messages could take
545. any number of forms: "a pun or other wordplay, a double entendre, a figure of speech, an
546. analogy, an anecdote, allegory, fable, or narrative" (Bormann 1985:4). The most important
547. dramatizing message for Bormann is a narrative
548. of characters acting out a role in a setting apart
549. from the "here-and-now" of the group. The
550. characters in these narratives may be real or
551. imaginary, and their action may be set in the
552. past or the future. The specific content of one
553. of these dramatizing messages is called a "fantasy theme." In the process of symbolic convergence, members of a group share fantasies that
554. have common fantasy themes. Bormann calls
555. such recurring dramatic situations "fantasy
556. types." The sharing of fantasy themes and fantasy types may lead to symbolic convergence
557. and the formation of a community characterized by a group consciousness. The community
558. then can use these fantasy themes to win new
559. converts to the group, maintain the cohesive-
560. ness of the group, and keep the group focused
561. on its sense of mission or vision.
562. Even though these fantasy themes are set
563. apart from the "here-and-now" of the group,
564. they reflect certain elements of group communication. By analyzing fantasy themes, the critic
565. may be able to understand the hidden agenda
566. or repressed problems of the group. Analysis of
567. these narratives can also reveal how groups
568. with no common history can create a sense of
569. community and common culture. By identifying recurring characters, plots, and settings
570. (fantasy types), the critic can reconstruct what
571. Bormann calls the "rhetorical vision" of the
572. group and can share vicariously in this vision.
573. In his original article, Bormann suggests
574. that fantasy theme analysis can be used to understand small-group communication and the
575. rhetoric of social movements and mass media.
576. Fantasy theme analysis has also been applied to
577. the analysis of political and religious rhetoric,
578. marketing and advertising, and organizational
579. behavior. Despite the popularity of fantasy
580. theme analysis, it has received some criticism,
581. particularly from G.P. Mohrmann(1982).
582. Mohrmann argues that the practice of fantasy
583. theme analysis does not follow logically from its
584. theoretical framework and that such analysis
585. tends to be circular. See Bormann (1982) for a
586. response to Mohrmann.
