Literally a mask. From the Latin word for the
mask worn by actors in the classical theater,
persona has come to be applied in literary criticism to the "mask" an author puts on in a literary work. It is a useful device for distinguishing between the author and the "character" of
the storyteller or poet implied in the story or
poem.
The importance of this distinction is most
clear in first-person works. For example, in
Gulliver's Travels, Gulliver is a persona created by Swift for relating his satire. Indeed, the
distinction between persona and author isespecially useful in criticism of satire because it
enables critics to make a link between the implied author and what is being satirized. But
it is also helpful in other first-person works.
In Huckleberry Finn, Huck is a persona that
critics are careful to distinguish from Mark
Twain. Browning's Duke in "My Last Duchess" and Fielding's Tom Jones in the novel
bearing his name are other examples of personae.
However, the concept may be applied in
more subtle instances of differentiation between
the author and the speaker in the story or poem.
In Wordsworth's "Tintern Abbey," for instance,
the speaker in the poem seems to be closelyassociated with the poet himself. Modern criticism, however, questions this identification by
using persona to delineate between the author
in his or her everyday life and the "character"
of the speaker who lives in the lines of the poem,
a role that the poet created for the speaker. And
the same may be said of third-person narrators
of stories and poems, those that do not have a
clearly defined speaker. The concept of persona
may be used to refer to an implied speaker because all fiction in some sense is told by someone created by the author.
Persona is similar in some ways to the rhetorical concept of ethos. Both allow critics to
identify and discuss a "character" created in a
discourse, though the former is for literary purposes and the latter for persuasive ones. In fact,
it may be argued that the current use of persona
in literary criticism provides a way for literary
critics to understand the rhetorical nature of
literary works through a literary ethos.
Michael Carter
North Carolina State University

5. Persuasion
6. The process by which language or symbolic
7. actions influence the choice-making of others.
8. Though sometimes used interchangeably with
9. the term rhetoric, persuasion is most often identified today as the end of a process occurring
10. within rhetorical situations—that is, situations
11. inviting or allowing the exertion of persuasive
12. influence. Persuasion is also typically distinguished from coercion, wherein choice-making
13. is influenced by resort to physical force or extreme punitive actions. Persuasion operates in
14. the realm of practical decision-making, taking
15. for its domain the entire sweep of everydayaffairs involving contingent matters in the social
16. sphere, in which people make choices about
17. how to behave, what to believe, and what to
18. value.
19. In the Western tradition, persuasion was
20. first systematically studied and taught by the
21. sophists, in and around Athens in the fifth and
22. early fourth century B.C.E. Typically, these figures tempered philosophical inquiry with interest in civic affairs. In the two millennia since
23. these beginnings, the study, teaching, and practice of persuasion have undergone several transformations. These transformations were never
24. clean breaks or complete paradigm shifts, however, and especially in modern and contemporary times one can easily find quite different
25. conceptions of persuasion in play at the same
26. time.
27. These various perspectives toward persuasion are distinguishable by their coordinates
28. along several conceptual dimensions. First, perspectives toward persuasion may differ according to the extent to which they limit their investigations to influence in the public and mass
29. realm or include influence in the private and
30. interpersonal realm as well. Second, perspectives toward persuasion differ according to their
31. inclination to limit persuasion to attempted influence that is explicit, whose persuasive character is conventionally acknowledged, or to
32. include as well the implicitly persuasive potential of forms of discourse not conventionally
33. regarded as persuasive. Third, perspectives toward persuasion differ according to the extent
34. to which they are persuader-centered, regarding
35. persuasion as a function of a communicator's
36. intent to persuade, or audience-centered, regarding persuasion as including—or indeed as
37. limited to—the potential of messages or behaviors to influence others, whether that effect was
38. intentional or not. Fourth, perspectives on persuasion vary according to their approach to the
39. question of ethics in persuasion, some regarding both the means and the ends of persuasion
40. as ethical problems, others focusing on the ethics of persuasive means and regarding the critique of the various ends to which persuasion
41. may be addressed as outside the jurisdiction of
42. the field of persuasion itself.
43. Within the history of rhetoric, three distinct perspectives toward persuasion are obvious: These may be termed the classical, symbolist, and institutional perspectives. Each of these
44. has its characteristic historical and intellectual
45. traditions as well as its own canon of thosesocial practices that it regards as the proper subject for the study of persuasion.
46. The classical perspective on persuasion
47. could also, with only little imprecision, be called
48. Aristotelian. It was and remains largely shaped
49. by assumptions tracing back to the Rhetoric of
50. Aristotle, in which the various means of persuasion are grouped under the general rubric of
51. rhetoric as the art of selecting among persuasive
52. strategies offered by particular topics and situations, and by the predispositions of particular
53. audiences. While subsequent scholarship within
54. this tradition has emphasized the application of
55. the classical principles to such discursive forms
56. as drama and poetry, epistolary writing, sermons, advertising and public relations, it has
57. most typically retained the original, Aristotelian
58. emphasis on the persuasive power of public
59. address in civic life. This perspective also lays
60. primary emphasis on strategic—hence, intentional—choices among persuasive strategies;
61. even modern social-psychological study of attitude change, on the surface audience-centered,
62. never abandons the assumption that this understanding of audience psychology has strategic
63. implications. Thus the paradigm and rationale
64. of persuasion within the classical perspective is
65. the intentional and explicit attempt by an individual to influence matters of civic concern by
66. directly addressing an audience.
67. Aristotle identified three primary means of
68. persuasion: the use of evidence and patterns of
69. reasoning within the message (logos), the image
70. and authority of the persuader (ethos), and the
71. predispositions and frame of mind of the audience (pathos). The intellectual and practical traditions represented by the classical perspective
72. have held to these distinctions.
73. The classical emphasis on use of evidence
74. and argument to appeal to an audience's reason
75. is consistent with the Aristotelian characterization of humans as rational animals.
76. Aristotle identified two resources available to
77. the persuader under this head: examples, and
78. the enthymeme—a deductive argument based
79. on the general and probable, rather than the
80. universal and necessary, that required participation of the audience for its construction. Typically, analysis and evaluation of evidence and
81. argument is accomplished by two general criteria: material validity (the truth of an argument's
82. premises) and formal validity (the correctness of
83. the reasoning from premises to conclusion).
84. Until comparatively recently, these criteria were
85. construed in a manner quite consistent with
86. their origins in formal logic.
87. The work of contemporary theorists,
88. perhaps most significantly that of Stephen
89. Toulmin, has advanced the general idea that
90. neither reasoning nor reasonableness is limited
91. to the strictures of formal logic. Toulmin's work
92. recognizes that humans construct arguments
93. for the purpose of persuading in those cases
94. where matters are contingent, and thus scientific-style demonstration is not possible. Following Toulmin, scholars have treated the standards of reasoning as more variable by field and
95. social context than was traditionally considered
96. to be the case. In place of the traditional analysis of syllogistic reasoning in terms of major
97. premise, minor premise, and conclusion have
98. come a method of analysis featuring claim,
99. data, and warrant. The claim, the proposition
100. a persuader advances against at least the possibility of disagreement, is supported at minimum
101. by data or grounds, references to examples, testimony, statistical information, authoritative
102. statements, or the like, that an audience might
103. plausibly accept as a foundation for advancing
104. the claim. This movement from grounds to
105. claim in an argument is supported by one or
106. more warrants, principles of legal, religious,
107. ideological, scientific, practical, or other nature
108. that an audience would recognize as justifying
109. the claim in light of the grounds. Other elements
110. of this analysis demonstrate further its departure from traditional syllogistic analysis: the
111. invocation of the warrant may be justified by
112. producing backing, the claim may be qualified
113. based on the strength of the grounds-warrant-
114. claim link, and the possibility of rebuttal conditions may be acknowledged. The Toulmin
115. model is still a formal model, like the traditional
116. syllogism and not strictly either an empirical or
117. prescriptive model, yet it clearly demonstrates
118. a crucial theme in contemporary thinking about
119. reasoning and persuasion: Its standards recognize collaborative, contextual, rhetorical considerations in reasoning, as well as "purely"
120. formal and objective factors.
121. Aristotelian theory identified three components of the image and the authority of the
122. persuader as a means of persuasion. Demonstrations of the persuader's moral character
123. show that the persuader's values are congruent
124. with those of the audience. Demonstrations of
125. good will toward the audience increase the
126. audience's trust in the persuader, and demonstrations of clear reasoning and command of
127. the subject matter enhance the audience's perception of the persuader's competence. Contemporary research on ethos and credibility, which
128. boomed in the 1960s, added one further dimension, dynamism. This research also concluded
129. that there may be less distinction for audiences
130. between good will and good character than traditionally supposed. Generally, contemporary
131. research has also tended to reduce the issue of
132. a persuader's moral character to the smaller,
133. though important, issue of a persuader's perceived trustworthiness.
134. Emphasis on the moral qualities perceived
135. to be embodied by a persuader remains crucial,
136. however, in a second area in the contemporary
137. study of persuader image and authority: the
138. phenomenon of charisma. Charismatic individuals wield extraordinary influence, well beyond what might have been duly conferred
139. upon them by formal institutions. Sometimes
140. mistakenly regarded as a matter solely of
141. the personality of the charismatic leader, charisma nevertheless requires a willing audience of
142. followers and a situation ripe for widespread
143. change in the status quo in order for the
144. charismatic leader to emerge. Charismatic persuaders typically articulate a clear vision for
145. change in the status quo and rely on unconventional and highly audience-centered tactics
146. to achieve their ends. Success for charismatic
147. persuaders is frequently ephemeral, since they
148. may lose their following if they can no longer
149. deliver change, and yet if they are successful
150. they often become part of a new status quo
151. within which their once-extraordinary, charismatic persona may appear to become co-opted
152. or irrelevant.
153. The comparatively rare phenomenon of
154. charisma is based on audiences' longing for
155. heroes, prophets, and messiahs. By comparison,
156. image management—another area of contemporary development on the classical concept of
157. ethos—makes no such demands upon the
158. would-be persuader. Image management, driven
159. by the insatiable demand of a plethora of commercial media for "news," has created a distinctly modern phenomenon: the "celebrity,"
160. "personality," or "notable," a figure who is
161. famous not for achievement, power, wealth,
162. virtue, or evil but simply by virtue of being well
163. known. Media images are created strategically,
164. out of simple, vivid, and concrete images, which
165. make sense within the frame of reference of
166. their audience; they rely on the mass media for
167. their dissemination and force. Such celebrity
168. can then be invoked to promote a wide spectrum of persuasive purposes. Image management represents the furthest departure of contemporary developments upon the classical
169. concept of persuader image and character, for
170. several reasons. First, it represents a weakening
171. of the connection between the managed image
172. of the persuader and the integrity and competence of the persuader's claims. Second,
173. the strategies of image management tend to
174. be more indirect than those of traditional
175. persuader ethos. Image management can
176. attempt to bolster the persuasive power of a
177. source even without that individual's apparent
178. participation; it also creates a reserve of persuasive potential that is comparatively independent
179. of both topic and situation.
180. The third general means of persuasion
181. identified by the classical perspective is the psychological or emotional state of the audience.
182. Certain states of mind, linked with the experience of pleasure and pain, may predispose audiences to respond in ways consistent with the
183. persuader's purposes. Aristotle identified fourteen different emotions and discussed each in
184. terms of its causes, the experience of pleasure
185. or pain associated with it, the inclinations to act
186. such an emotion may create, and strategies for
187. kindling or extinguishing such emotions in an
188. audience. For example, audiences tend to feel
189. anger when they believe they have received
190. undeserved ill treatment from another. Audiences who feel anger also feel an impulsetoward revenge for the slight, and their experience
191. of anger is characterized by pain at the thought
192. of being treated badly and pleasure at imagining revenge or retribution.
193. Arising from a social scientific context for
194. the study of human behavior, many contemporary studies of audience motivation investigated
195. the effects of rewards and punishments of various types on persuasion. Among the more influential in this line of research were the Yale
196. studies of persuasion conducted during the two
197. decades following World War II and a more
198. recent body of research on fear appeals. This
199. work suggests that rewards are generally more
200. effective than punishments in accomplishing
201. persuasive effects and that moderate fearappeals are more persuasive than extreme ones.
202. Other developments in the study of audience
203. motivations have been in three general areas:
204. equilibrium theories, growth theories, and personality theories. Equilibrium theories include
205. the cognitive balance theory of Fritz Heider
206. and the cognitive dissonance theory of Leon
207. Festinger and associates. Both general perspectives share several key assumptions: First, individuals hold within themselves many different
208. cognitions—thoughts, beliefs, memories, atti-
209. tudes, and values; second, these cognitive elements can come into conflict or tension with
210. one another. According to equilibrium theories,
211. this tension is inherently uncomfortable or unpleasant, and causes the individuals (consciously or unconsciously) to reduce this unpleasant experience by changing their attitudes
212. or behavior to restore equilibrium.
213. Cognitive balance theory examines the
214. three-part relationship among a perceiver/audi-
215. ence, a second person, and a person or thing
216. toward which the perceiver and other are positively or negatively inclined. In general, balance
217. exists when the various elements and relations
218. are in harmony from the point of view of the
219. perceiver. Where they are not—where, for example, the perceiver is positively disposed both
220. toward the object in question and toward the
221. second person, while the second person is negatively inclined toward the object—the situation
222. is psychologically unstable, and the perceiver
223. experiences an impulse to change his or her attitude toward the other person or toward the
224. object in question. Cognitive balance theory
225. treats this tension reduction as largely automatic and nonreflective.
226. Cognitive dissonance theory goes beyond
227. cognitive balance theory by attempting toaccount not only for the valence of the attitude
228. toward other and object but also the intensity
229. of this liking or disliking, as well as the importance to the individual of the attitude in question. Less predictively powerful under controlled conditions than cognitive balance theory,
230. cognitive dissonance theory nevertheless reintroduces a satisfying degree of complexity to
231. description of the attitude change process in
232. persuasive audiences. Cognitive dissonance
233. theory suggests that the pressure to reduce cognitive tension by change in behavior or attitude
234. builds gradually as the number and intensity of
235. dissonant cognitions increases. This theory further assumes that the process of tension-reduction may very well have an external, self-aware
236. social component in it, rather than being a
237. purely psychological reflex.
238. Compared to equilibrium theories' emphasis on reduction of psychological tension as a
239. motivator of audiences, growth psychology
240. posits that humans are not merely driven by
241. avoidance of pain but also motivated by the
242. desire for growth through the satisfaction of
243. needs. The analysis of human needs as hierarchically ordered, with self-actualization as the
244. apex, is largely the work of humanistic psychology, for which Abraham Maslow was a chief
245. proponent. Growth psychology argues that individuals are motivated not merely by avoidance of pain but by the satisfaction of meeting
246. needs and moving toward self-actualization; it
247. suggests that audiences will be most strongly
248. motivated by persuasive appeals that address
249. their lowest-order unmet need. It differs from
250. equilibrium theories further by its ability to
251. explain how individuals can be motivated to
252. undertake long-term activities, which may do
253. little to reduce stress or tension in the short run
254. but which may over time tend to produce
255. growth.
256. The classical perspective in its contemporary position retains much of the legacy of its
257. roots in antiquity. It is heavily connected in the
258. distinction between reason and passion. Even
259. today its proponents' insistence that the two are
260. not antithetical terms remains trapped in large
261. measure by the terms of the debate established
262. two and a half millennia ago, when Plato attacked rhetoric, the art of persuasion, as being
263. nothing more than pandering to and gratifying
264. audiences, rather than challenging them with
265. the truth. In the eighteenth century, this tension
266. centered on the so-called conviction-persuasion
267. duality. This division separated persuasion
268. aimed at behavioral change from argument
269. aimed at cognitive change and attempted to
270. separate rational appeals aimed at conviction
271. from emotional appeals aimed at persuasion.
272. This duality has been maintained frequently in
273. the twentieth century, especially as a means of
274. maintaining subdiscipline boundaries. It is also
275. supported by the pervasive modernist cultural
276. belief in "objectivity," the presumption that
277. persuasion, which appeals to "the facts" and to
278. "reason" rather than to the emotions, is both
279. pragmatically more effective and ethically superior. Nevertheless, this distinction has often dissipated within serious theoretical treatments of
280. the subject.
281. The Platonic attack on the art of persuasion as an art of flattery that disregarded truth
282. set the stage as well for continuing ethical concern about a process of influence that operates
283. in the realm of the contingent rather than from
284. an epistemological position that included an
285. ideal of truth. Because the classical perspective
286. has generally regarded the principles of persuasion as morally neutral, and insisted rather that
287. the ends toward which these techniques are directed are the proper object of ethical critique,
288. persuasion has always been vulnerable to such
289. ethical charges. In response to this, different
290. contemporary approaches to persuasion imply
291. or specify differing levels and perspectives for
292. responsible action on the part of both sources
293. and audience of persuasion.
294. The symbolist perspective, although generally more modern in temperament than the classical perspective, nevertheless draws upon intellectual roots that are in some cases nearly as old
295. as those of the classical perspective. The symbolist perspective centers on the notion that all
296. persuasion is really to a significant extentself-
297. persuasion, involving the active participation of
298. an audience. Thus symbolist theories explain
299. how audience members decode or interpret a
300. variety of symbolic actions such that influence
301. is accomplished. While the classical perspective
302. emphasizes the intentional and explicit attempt
303. by an individual to influence matters of civic
304. concern by directly addressing an audience, the
305. symbolist perspective departs from—or at least
306. greatly expands—these parameters, taking into
307. account unintentional effects as well as intentional, private or interpersonal interaction as
308. well as public address, implicit forms of persuasion as well as explicit. Because all symbols represent interests and motives, from the symbolic
309. perspective all symbols, and all acts of interpretation, are considered inherently persuasive.
310. At the heart of the symbolist perspective is
311. the capacity of symbols to represent the interests, motives, and values of those who perceive
312. them. This process of recognizing our own
313. motives represented in the symbolic acts of another has been termed "identification" by Kenneth Burke, whose writings have contributed
314. significantly to the contemporary developments
315. of this perspective. Symbols and symbol-systems are organized as codes, learned conventions shared by cultures and subcultures regarding the interpretation of symbolic message
316. elements. These conventions, again, are generally receiver-centered; while not ignoring the
317. intentional, artistic, and strategic purposes to
318. which persuaders may put symbols, theyassume that meaning and interpretation of symbols reside finally within the perceiver of those
319. symbols. Codes shape the influence of symbols
320. and symbolic acts in a variety of media: written
321. and spoken language and dialects, nonverbal
322. behavior as well as architecture and the manipulation of physical objects, film and television production, dance and musical performance, rituals and ceremonies, and so on. They
323. also comprise the conventions of interpretation
324. which define a wide variety of genres and
325. subgenres within each of these media: Within
326. language, they distinguish the creation of meaning in narrative from that in exposition; within
327. narrative, they distinguish comedy, tragedy, and
328. quest—and so on.
329. The symbolist perspective identifies three
330. broad areas of inquiry and practice on persuasion: language, nonverbal and nondiscursive
331. symbolism, and structure and form. Language
332. is the most obvious and most elaborated code
333. in our experience. In addition to its instrumental function of signification and predication,
334. which was most central to the classical perspective on persuasion, language also serves the
335. constitutive function of creating symbolic reality. Even the most elementary function of language, naming, is itself laden with potentially
336. persuasive consequences, since the act of naming is a cultural expression of the importance of
337. the thing named as well as a demonstration of
338. our power over the thing named.
339. The traditional analysis of figures of speech
340. and figures of thought also exemplifies this
341. function. Figures of speech include those uses of
342. language for ornament or figuration wherein
343. the words used retain their literal meaning.
344. Rhyming, metrical devices, parallelism, and
345. repetition are examples of figures of speech.
346. Figures of thought, on the other hand, involve
347. the use of language wherein the literal meaning
348. of the words used is no longer able to account
349. for the symbolic meaning of the expression as
350. a whole. Two of the more powerful figures of
351. thought are irony and metaphor. In both cases
352. the figure of thought allows a persuader to
353. evoke powerful identifications that are strongly
354. resistant to translation into literal language.
355. Nonverbal symbolism is generally built
356. from elements of body movement, visual images, and acoustic images, alone or in some
357. combination. Nonverbal and nondiscursive
358. symbolism enjoyed only small attention within the classical perspective, not infrequently
359. lumped under the bland heading of "delivery."
360. In part this is because of the prevailing Western cultural tendency to view all creation
361. of meaning through the lens of language and
362. linguisticality, as we speak of "body language,"
363. "reading a person like a book," and so on.
364. However, nonverbal symbolism tends to be
365. nondiscursive in its form: holistic in its presentation, rather than linear; specific and concrete
366. in its reference, rather than general andabstract; tending toward multiple and sometimes
367. contradictory meanings, rather than univocal
368. and singular. Hence nondiscursive symbolism
369. frequently operates in persuasion in ways that
370. a more language-centered approach will
371. be unable to account for. Nonverbal and
372. nondiscursive symbolism has a content level, at
373. which it reports, conveys, or displays information. It also has a relational level, at which it
374. displays or comments upon the relationship
375. among the parties communicating.
376. Symbols, discursive or nondiscursive, do
377. not ordinarily exist free floating in a vacuum;
378. they occur linked with other symbols in a structure, which gives shape or pattern to symbolic
379. content or subject matter. These structures
380. themselves are empirical in nature, and a list of
381. general structure types would include rhythm,
382. hierarchy, association, and boundaries. Yet
383. structure alone is insufficient to explain this
384. aspect of the persuasive power of symbols. Symbols influence and motivate us not merely
385. through their referents, content or subject matter. They are also things that we experience,
386. objects of our senses that are (or at least appear
387. to be) located in space and time. This aspect of
388. symbols, their form, is also important to understanding persuasion from the symbolist perspective.
389. At a psychological level, form refers to the
390. arousal of anticipation followed by the consummation of that anticipation. Once we detect
391. form, we form expectations; a form is completed when it somehow resolves that senseof
392. expectation. This resolution may be temporary
393. or permanent, expected or unexpected. There
394. are several basic forms: Syllogistic progression
395. is any form wherein what comes first somehow
396. requires or demands precisely what comes after. "Syllogistic" does not simply mean "argument;" a well-formed argument is merely one
397. type of syllogistic progression. Qualitative progression can involve variations on a theme, extensions of an idea, or development of some
398. other quality. Repetitive form is the consistent
399. reappearance of a thing—but always in slightly
400. different guise. The "principle" involved in the
401. repetition may be simple and obvious, like
402. rhyming, or subtle and extended, like the repetition of certain kinds of characters or events
403. or imagery. In addition to these more generic
404. forms are the many conventional forms thatan
405. audience may understand beforehand from specific cultural experiences. There are many examples of these conventions: church sermons,
406. sonnets, tragic opera, rock concerts, prime-time
407. situation comedies, advertisements, and campaign speeches are just a few instances. Forms
408. can be nested within one another: minor or incidental forms tend to occur in larger or more
409. complex works, or actions, and can be discussed partly independently of the overall work.
410. Forms can be interrelated andmay often overlap in a particular work or event. Forms can
411. also conflict with one another, as when one
412. form leads us to expect one thing, while an intermingling form requires some other resolution
413. for itself.
414. From a purely aesthetic point of view, we
415. judge the artistic merit of a work byhow well
416. the outcome resolves the developing form, or
417. how perfectly the form is presented. From a
418. persuasive point of view, we judge the extent to
419. which the formal aspects of a work place the
420. audience in a particular frame of mind, or creates in them a particular motivation.
421. The structure of persuasive messages is a
422. topic of considerable discussion and inquiry.
423. Both expository and narrative patterns of organization contribute to persuasive effects. While
424. research from the social sciences suggests that
425. didactic organizational patterns emphasizing
426. the clarity of a persuasive argument are effective, there is controversy about the order of
427. presentation of multiple arguments or persuasive appeals. Study of primacy and recency effects argue for the benefits ofplacing the strongest arguments either first or last in a more
428. complex persuasive message structure. Contemporary developments regarding structure tend
429. to emphasize the persuasive effects of narrative
430. structures like those found in novels or films.
431. Narrative structure appears to be most persuasive when the story presented is both coherent
432. (narrative probability) and true to life (narrative
433. fidelity). Mythic structures are assumed to be
434. particularly persuasive as their sequence of dramatic action appears often to transcend both
435. time and culture.
436. The status of ethics is more ambiguous
437. with regard to persuasion from the symbolist
438. perspective than inthe case of the classical perspective. Since the emphasis is on audience,
439. rather than persuader, ethical standards for tactics and goals have been discussed less explicitly in this perspective. General consensus seems
440. to exist that persuasion involving symbolsor
441. symbolic actions that are themselves inherently
442. degrading or demeaning is unethical, yet even
443. this consensus is not without its unresolved
444. problems. Chief among these is the problem of
445. justifying the censure of a persuader for tactics
446. that an audience finds offensive while simultaneously locating authority for the interpretation
447. of symbols in the audience itself, rather than in
448. the persuader.
449. Unlike the other two perspectives, the institutional perspective is distinctly contemporary in its scope and theorizing; its intellectual
450. and practical roots extend back scarcely further
451. than the period shortly before World War I. Its
452. context is the modern society, moderated by
453. mass media, which have become both extraordinarily pervasive and interpenetrated with
454. other institutions, all of them large, enduring
455. social collectives empowered by custom or law
456. to perform important social functions. Studies
457. of persuasion from this perspective coincide
458. with the rise of various persuasion industries
459. such as advertising and public relations. The
460. institutional perspective is far less coherent
461. across its entire spectrum than either the classical or symbolist perspective. Its coherence
462. comes from the twentieth-century recognition
463. that once persuasion moves to an institutional
464. context—that is, when institutions serve as audience, source, or context of persuasion—many
465. insights and assumptions of the classical and
466. symbolist perspectives apply only in a qualified
467. form, if at all.
468. The institutional perspective has as yet
469. no comprehensive theory of persuasion. Rather, four broad areas of practice and inquiry
470. are generally recognized: campaigns, social
471. movements, propaganda, and ideology. Some
472. of these areas, particularly the first three,
473. were at their outset noticeably classical in
474. their orientation, but the ability of the classical perspective to keep from being outpaced
475. by changes in persuasive practices at the institutional level has become uncertain indeed.
476. This can be explained by three contemporary
477. phenomena: Both contemporary theory and
478. practice have significantly blurred the distinction between public and private; the corporate, bureaucratic character of much
479. discourse has rendered problematic the traditional link of persuasion to the moral and
480. ethical choices of a single, identifiable individual; and the increasing hybridization of entertainment in a commercialized mass culture
481. has made the distinction between explicit and
482. implicit persuasion increasingly problematic.
483. Since the classical perspective is defined by
484. fairly sharp distinctions in each of these dimensions, its ability to serve as a basis for
485. theorizing or practice at the institutional level
486. is at least to some extent called into question.
487. Each of the four broad types of persuasion
488. at the institutional level has its characteristic
489. theoretical and practical concerns. Persuasive
490. campaigns are explicit persuasion for private
491. motives or gain within an institutional context.
492. Campaigns are generally characterized by centralized planning, legitimate leadership, and
493. differing tactical objectives at different stages of
494. the campaign. Product and commercial campaigns tend to exploit both mass and interpersonal communication media, relying on exposure to mass media to create general awareness
495. of a product or idea, and more direct contact
496. with opinion leaders to influence choicesregarding the product or idea. Political campaigns
497. typically have structures that are largely shaped
498. as responses to external situations: primaries,
499. ballot deadlines, general elections, and so on.
500. All forms of campaigns typically rely on a
501. highly segmented view of their audience and
502. focus attention on those segments where the
503. greatest support can be garnered with the most
504. efficient use of resources.
505. Social movements are explicit and implicit
506. persuasion for public gain that attempts to
507. change an institution, its structure, or its practices. By being "outside the system," the range
508. of tactics available to social movements isdifferent from that available to legitimated campaigns. Certain tactics available to "legitimate"
509. or institutionally sanctioned persuasion will not
510. be available to a social movement, while other,
511. perhaps more creative or unconventional tactics
512. become available in their place. Establishing
513. one's legitimate right to leadership can be problematic in a social movement, as can be the need
514. to keep movement members motivated. Social
515. movements typically face the problem of militant and moderate factions within its ranks;
516. such divisions have implications for motivation
517. and discipline of the membership.
518. Propaganda is explicit, institutionally
519. sponsored persuasion, typically with specific
520. and limited goals, aimed at a mass audience (or
521. "the masses"). Propaganda generally relies on
522. close coordination between media and the institutional source of the persuasion. Its most
523. commonly found strategy is the constant repetition of a fairly simple—and simplified—set of
524. themes or messages. Propaganda is characterized by absolute concern with effects, relies on
525. ideology for its justification, and typically
526. works explicitly or implicitly to repress dissent.
527. Sociologist Jacques Ellul has noted that propaganda may advance either political or sociological ends, serve either to agitate a mass audience
528. into action or to pacify it into inaction, originate either with the authority of institutional
529. elites or with the pressure to conformity among
530. the masses, and either emphasize or reject the
531. traditional forms of rationality in its messages.
532. Ideology is implicit persuasion, usually institutionally sponsored, aimed at preserving the
533. institution and its authority. Its building blocks
534. are cultural values, shared myths, and sources
535. and structures of societal power. The goal of
536. ideology is the formation and preservation of a
537. world view and culture, larger in scope and
538. more enduring in scale than anything contemplated by propaganda, social movements, or
539. campaigns. When a clear pattern of values and
540. myths permeates diverse messages and media
541. from diverse, though interrelated, institutions,
542. members of the culture tend to take for granted
543. this pattern and the world view it embodies, not
544. questioning its validity or its relationship to
545. reality outside its own terms. The power of an
546. institutionally backed world view to gain ascendancy within its culture creates or perpetuates
547. a hegemony, whereby the ascendancy of that
548. world view over other possible rivals is preserved. Ideological persuasion is characterized
549. by multiple messages over time and is strongly
550. associated with not only formal institutional
551. channels of communication but also the forms
552. and content of popular culture.
553. The question of ethics from the institutional perspective is as complex as the problems
554. of theory and practice. Where we consider explicit persuasion that originates "inside" institutions, there are generally formulated ethical
555. codes (as well as legal ones) that may apply.
556. When our concern turns to persuasion that
557. originates from without, as in social movements, or when the persuasion is implicit, like
558. ideology, such judgments become more problematic. This is even more true since in both of
559. the latter cases the source of ethically questionable messages may well be unclear, owing to the
560. bureaucratic nature of institutions and the decentralized nature of movements.
561. Institution-level persuasion is ethically
562. problematic in yet another way. Both the classical and symbolist perspectives grant a certain
563. reasonable and moral autonomy to their audiences, albeit for different reasons. But most institution-level persuasion depends on reaching
564. a large audience. The scale, and frequently the
565. cost, of such persuasion contributes to a general
566. redefinition of the audience according to the
567. principle of conformity, by which moral autonomy is downplayed in favor of homogeneity and obedience—epitomized in the industrialized West as the consumer.
