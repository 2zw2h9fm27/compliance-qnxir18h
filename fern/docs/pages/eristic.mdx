<div className="prose prose-lg max-w-none leading-relaxed bg-emerald-50 border-emerald-200 border-l-4 pl-6 py-2 pr-4 rounded-r-lg">

The branch ofrhetoric that deals specifically
with controversy or disputation. Benjamin Jowett, the nineteenth-century translator of Plato,
succinctly defined it as "the art of fighting with
words." The term isderived from the Greek
word for strife, eris; Homer, for example, says
that Athene stirred up eris between Agamemnon and Menelaos (Odyssey 3.136).For
the pre-Socratic philosopher Heraclitus, eris
seems to denote rivalry as a principle of nature
whereby interaction between opposites is the
origin of all change.
It is likely that the adjectival form eristike
was first used to denote a special kind of rhetoric early in the fourth century B.C.E. Schiappa
argues that Plato coined the termto mean
"seeking victory in argument." The practice so
named certainly predates Plato however, and
most likely developed among the older sophists
of the fifth century B.C.E.
In Lives of the Great Philosophers, Diogenes Laertius used the term eristic in connection
with two of the older sophists, Euclidesof
Megara and Protagoras of Abdera. Euclides "inspired the Megarians with a frenzied love of controversy," which may explain why some sources
refer to eristic as "the Megarian school." Diogenes Laertius credits Protagoras with writing a
book titled The Art of Eristic (Technê Eristikon).
He also says that Protagoras was "the firstto
institute contests in debating . . . and the first to
show how to attack and refute any proposition"
(9.52–53; Hicks's translation). An emphasis on
either competition or refutation might well have
been described as eristic by dialecticians of succeeding generations.
Such methods, though quite appropriate
for fifth-century applications in oratorical displays and contests and in legal speeches, were
not well adapted to establishing a form of discourse for philosophical speculation. Hence,
Plato, Isocrates, and Aristotle were all critical
of eristic practices. In Sophist, Plato compared
a contentious speaker to a prizefighter whose
goal is victory rather than knowledge or truth:
Eristic lacks legitimacy because it countenances ambiguity. Isocrates offered detailed
criticism of other speech teachers in Against
the Sophists and Antidosis. He attacked his
contemporaries for their ignorance and defects
of character in general, but in particular for
their claims to be able to teach standard techniques guaranteeing success.
Aristotle wrote of eristic as both a branch
of rhetoric and a type of reasoning. In Rhetoric
he included eristic among activities that give
pleasure—at least to those who have the experience and ability to secure victory (1371a). In
a list of ten fallacies, he criticized a specific use
of apparent syllogism in eristic (1402a). In
Topics eristic is identified as one of four types
of reasoning, and distinguished from the three
others (demonstration, dialectic, and false reasoning): "Reasoning is eristic if it is based
on opinions which appear to be generallyaccepted but are not really so" (100b). In Sophistical Refutations eristic is called an unfair kind
of fighting (171b).
What these later critics called eristic seems
to have emerged in fifth-century Athens as
a pragmatic response to political and juridical
conditions. It was a loose category of techniques and formulas rather than an organized
school of thought. The term, however, has always carried a somewhat pejorative sense. The
term rhetoric is itself often deployed in much
the same way in present-day popular usage.

19. Ethics
20. Involves issues and standards concerning degrees
21. of right and wrong, of virtue and vice, and of
22. obligation in human conduct. In Western cultures, for example, such standards as honesty,
23. truthfulness, promise-keeping, justice, fairness,
24. and humaneness typically are used to judge the
25. ethicality of human behavior and practices. Some
26. philosophers draw distinctions between ethics
27. and morals as concepts. Other philosophers use
28. the terms ethics and morals more or less interchangeably—as will be the case here.
29. Ethical issues arise in human conduct
30. whenever that behavior could have significant
31. impact on other persons, when it involves conscious choice of means and ends, and when the
32. behavior can be judged by standards of right
33. and wrong. Potential ethical issues thus are
34. inherent in rhetoric because it can be judged on
35. a right-wrong dimension, because it involves
36. potential significant influence on other humans,
37. and because the rhetor (persuader, advocate, arguer) consciously chooses specific ends
38. sought and communicative means to achieve
39. those ends.
40. Ethical concerns have been central
41. to rhetorical theory and practice at least since
42. Plato condemned rhetoric as sophistry
43. in a negative sense and characterized rhetoric
44. as no more intellectually respectable than mere
45. cookery. In contrast, Aristotle in his Rhetoric
46. described rhetoric as an "offshoot" of ethics.
47. Contemporary rhetorical theorists Richard M.
48. Weaver in The Ethics of Rhetoric (6, 24) and
49. Kenneth Burke in The Rhetoric of Religion (41,
50. 187) argue that all human use of language necessarily involves matters of ethical responsibility.
51. Ethical Responsibility
52. Rhetors' ethical responsibilities may stem from
53. a status or position they have earned or been
54. granted, from established ethical principles, from
55. commitments (promises, pledges, agreements)
56. they have made, or from subsequent consequences (effects, impacts) of their rhetoric on
57. others. Responsibility includes the elements of
58. fulfilling duties and obligations, of beingaccountable to other individuals and groups, of
59. being evaluated by agreed-upon standards, and
60. of being accountable to our own conscience.
61. The ethics of rhetoric also should encompass both individual and social ethics. What are
62. the ethical virtues of character and the central
63. ethical standards that should guide an individual's choices? What are the ethical standards
64. and responsibilities that should guide the rhetoric of organizations and institutions? For an
65. ethically suspect rhetorical practice, where
66. should individual and collective responsibility
67. be placed? The study of rhetorical ethics should
68. suggest standards both for individual daily and
69. context-bound choices and also for institutional/systemic policies and practices.
70. Whether persuaders seem intentionally and
71. knowingly to use particular content or techniques is a factor most persons take into account
72. in judging degree of ethicality. If a dubious rhetorical choice seems to stem more from accident,
73. from an unintentional slip of the tongue, or even
74. from ignorance, often people are less harsh in
75. their ethical assessment. In contrast, it can be
76. argued that rhetors have an ethical obligation to
77. verify the soundness of their evidence, reasoning,
78. and terms prior to presentation to others; careless preparation should not be an adequate excuse to lessen the harshness of ethical judgment.
79. In assessing the ethics of rhetoric, a persistent
80. question is, Does the end justify the means? Does
81. the necessity for achieving a goal widely acknowledged as worthwhile always justify the use of ethically questionable techniques? To say that the end
82. does not always justify the means is not to say that
83. ends never justify means. The rhetor's goal simply
84. should be considered as one of a number of potentially relevant ethical criteria from among which
85. the most relevant and appropriate standards are
86. selected. Under some circumstances, such as a
87. threat to physical survival, the goal of personal or
88. national security might temporarily take precedence over other relevant ethical criteria such as
89. promise-keeping or truthfulness. A number of
90. scholars have argued that rhetorical means employed can have effects on audience thought and
91. decision-making habits apartfrom and in addition
92. to the specific end that the rhetor seeks. No matter the purpose they serve, the arguments, appeals,
93. structure, and language the rhetor chooses do
94. partly shape the audience's values, thinking habits, language patterns, and level of trust.
95. Rhetorical Ethics and the Audience
96. In most public and private rhetoric, a fundamental implied contract or unspoken assumption is
97. that words can be trusted and people will be
98. truthful. Unless there are reasons to be skeptical,
99. audiences expect rhetors to mean what they say.
100. Even if rhetors do not know the absolutely certain "ultimate truth" about something, audiences expect rhetors to say what they believe to
101. be true and not to say what they believe to be
102. false. Various types of rhetorical settings, rhetorical roles, and specific situations also may have
103. unspoken expectations that help define the ethical relationship between persuader and audience.
104. For example, one's expectations concerning honesty, accuracy, and relevance of information
105. probably differ when involved with an American
106. Cancer Society representative persuading one to
107. contribute funds or with the stereotypical used-
108. car dealer. If the Cancer Society representative intentionally uses false statistics, the ethics of that
109. choice could be condemned because the audience
110. does not expect a representative of a reputable
111. humanitarian society to use questionable techniques; in such a situation, the audience would
112. be especially vulnerable.
113. What are the ethics of audience adaptation?
114. To what degree is it ethical for advocates to alter their ideas and proposals in order to adapt to
115. the needs, capacities, desires, and expectations of
116. an audience? To secure acceptance, some rhetors
117. adapt to their audience to the extreme of so
118. changing their idea that the idea is no longer
119. really theirs. These rhetors merely say what the
120. audience wants them to say regardless of their
121. own convictions. On the other hand, some degree of adaptation in language choice, supporting materials, organization, and presentation to
122. reflect and respect the specific nature of the audience is a crucial part of successful and responsible rhetoric. Rhetors must decide on an appropriate ethical position between their idea in its
123. pure form and that idea modified to achieve
124. maximum impact with an audience.
125. What are the ethical responsibilities of audience members, of readers and listeners as
126. cocreators of meaning with rhetors? The image
127. held of the rhetorical process may influence the
128. answer to this question. Audience members
129. would seem to bear little responsibility if they
130. are viewed as inert, passive, defenseless receptacles, as mindless blotters uncritically accept-
131. ing arguments and ideas. In contrast, rhetoric
132. today is typically viewed as a transaction in
133. which both rhetor and audience bear degrees of
134. mutual responsibility to participate actively in
135. the process. This image of audience members as
136. active participants can suggest various responsibilities. Johannesen (135–37) explores two
137. major audience ethical responsibilities: reasoned skepticism and appropriate feedback.
138. Six "perspectives" for ethical assessment of
139. rhetoric follow. Each perspective represents a
140. major viewpoint or conceptual lens that scholars explicitly, and others often implicitly, use to
141. judge the ethics of rhetoric. These perspectives
142. are not mutually exclusive, nor are they exhaustive of possible stances.
143. Political Perspectives
144. A political system (system of government)usually contains within its ideology an implicit
145. and explicit set of values and proceduresaccepted as crucial to the health and growth of
146. that governmental system. Once these essential
147. political values are identified for a political system, ethical standards for evaluating rhetorical
148. means and ends within that particular system
149. can be derived from those values. The assumption is that rhetoric should foster realization of
150. these values and that communication techniques and tactics that retard, subvert, or circumvent these fundamental political values
151. should be condemned as unethical.
152. Naturally, each different system of government (such as Italian Fascism, Soviet Communism, or German Nazism) might embody differing values leading to differing ethical judgments.
153. Within the context of American representative
154. democracy, for instance, various analysts pinpoint values and procedures they view as fundamental to optimum functioning of that political
155. system. Rooted in such values and procedures,
156. Haiman espouses a "degree of rationality" ethical standard, and Nilsen develops a "significant
157. choice" rhetorical ethic. Wallace advocates the
158. "four moralities": Habit of Search, Habit ofJustice, Habit of Preferring Public to Private Motivations, and Habit of Respect for Dissent.
159. Human Nature Perspectives
160. Human nature perspectives, as considered here,
161. focus on the essence of human nature. Unique
162. characteristics of human nature that set humans
163. apart from other living things are identified.
164. The assumption is that uniquely human attributes should be enhanced, thereby promoting fulfillment of maximum human potential.
165. Audiences could judge the degree to which a
166. rhetor's appeals and techniques either foster or
167. undermine the development of fundamental
168. human characteristics. They would condemn as
169. unethical a technique that dehumanizes, that
170. makes a person less than human. Both rhetors
171. and audiences could use human nature perspectives to assess the ethics of rhetoric regardless
172. of situation, culture, religion, or governmental
173. form; the assumption would be that a human
174. is essentially human no matter the context.
175. While they recognize Aristotle's belief that
176. the capacity for rationality is one defining characteristic of humans, Rowland and Womack interpret Aristotle's ethic of rhetoric to be summarized
177. as follows: the sound, relevant, integrated use
178. of both reason and emotion in the service of
179. practical wisdom and the general public good.
180. Wieman and Walter develop an ethic for rhetoric
181. that focuses on the two uniquely human attributes
182. of symbol-using capacity and the need for human
183. beings of other humans. Johnstone roots his rhetorical ethical duties of resoluteness, openness,
184. gentleness, and compassion in the distinctly human capacity to persuade and be persuaded. Scott
185. bases his suggestions for an "epistemic" ethic of
186. rhetoric on the unique human capacity to generate or create knowledge through the rhetorical
187. process itself. And Eubanks advocates an ethic for
188. rhetoric founded on the central human capacity
189. to create and sustain values and to apply them in
190. rendering value judgments.
191. Dialogical Perspectives
192. Dialogical perspectives emerge from scholarship
193. on the nature of communication as dialogue
194. rather than monologue. The works of philosopher Martin Buber and humanistic psychologist
195. Carl Rogers have proven seminal in the development of such perspectives. Dialogical perspectives contend that attitudes toward each other
196. among participants in communication are an
197. index of the ethical level of that communication.
198. Dialogical attitudes are held to be more fully
199. human, humane, and facilitative of self-fulfillment than are monological attitudes.
200. Communication as dialogue is characterized
201. by such attitudes as authenticity, inclusion, confirmation, presentness, mutual equality, honesty,
202. concern for the welfare of others, trust, open-
203. mindedness, respect, humility, lack of pretense,
204. nonmanipulative intent, sincerity, encouragement of free expression, and acceptance of others as persons with intrinsic worth regardless of
205. differences over belief or behavior. Communication as monologue, in contrast, is marked by
206. deception, superiority, exploitation, dogmatism,
207. insincerity, pretense, personal self-display, self-
208. aggrandizement, judgmentalism that stifles expression, coercion, possessiveness, condescension, self-defensiveness, and the viewing of others
209. as objects to be manipulated. Communication as
210. dialogue flowers most naturally in private, two-
211. person, face-to-face oral communication settings
212. that extend, even intermittently, over lengthy
213. periods of time. Nevertheless, Johannesen (57–
214. 77) contends that dialogical perspectives apply
215. in varying degrees to public oral and written discourse, and he explores an ethic for rhetoric
216. rooted in dialogue.
217. Situational Perspectives
218. Situational perspectives focus regularly andprimarily on elements of the specific rhetorical situation at hand. Most perspectives discussed here
219. (and others) make some allowances, on occasion,
220. for the modified application of ethical criteria
221. in special circumstances. An extreme situational
222. perspective, however, routinely makes ethical
223. judgments only in light of each different context.
224. Among the concrete situational factors relevant
225. to making a purely situational ethical evaluation
226. are (1) the role or function of the rhetor for the
227. audience; (2) audience standards for reasonableness and appropriateness; (3) degree of audience
228. awareness and approval of the rhetor's techniques; (4) degree of urgency for implementation
229. of the rhetor's proposal; (5) audience goals and
230. values; and (6) specific audience standards for
231. ethical discourse. From a primarily situational
232. perspective, Rogge argues that unlabeled hyperbole is ethical in a political campaign, that imperiled national security could justify use of otherwise unethical rhetorical techniques, and that
233. in some situations an acknowledged leader has
234. the responsibility to rally support by using emotional appeals that circumvent human rational
235. choice. Also to be explored from situational perspectives could be the nature of ethical standards
236. that should apply to the rhetoric of protest, agitation, and social movements (Johannesen
237. 82–88).
238. Religious Perspectives
239. Religious perspectives stem from the moral and
240. spiritual values, guidelines, and rules embodied
241. in the ideology and sacred literature of various religions. Criteria derived from a religion
242. could be used to assess the ethics of rhetoric. For
243. example, the Bible warns against lying, slander, and bearing false witness. Taoist religion
244. stresses empathy and insight rather than reason
245. and logic as roads to truth; citing facts and demonstrating logical conclusions are minimized in
246. favor of feeling and intuition. Griffin (27–41) develops an ethic for Christian evangelism that describes, through the analogy of love, the true lover,
247. nonlover, flirt, seducer, rapist, smother lover, and
248. legalistic lover. Jensen compares the monotheistic
249. Judaic, Christian, and Islamic traditions with the
250. Asiatic religions of Confucianism, Taoism, Hinduism, and Buddhism in a search for potential
251. standards for ethical rhetoric.
252. Utilitarian Perspectives
253. Utilitarian perspectives emphasize criteria of usefulness, pleasure, and happiness to assess rhetorical ethics. Some utilitarians expand the concept
254. of happiness to include such intrinsically good
255. consequences as health, friendship, and knowledge. The utilitarian standard for evaluating rhetorical means and ends could be phrased as a
256. question: Does the technique or goal promote the
257. greatest good for the greatest number of people
258. in the long run? Brockriede synthesizes a standard for ethical rhetoric from the influential utilitarian view of English philosopher Jeremy Ben-
259. tham. Brembeck and Howell (225–47) develop
260. a "social utility" ethic for persuasion that stresses
261. usefulness to the people affected, survival potential for groups involved, and benefits and harms
262. central to a culture's value system.
263. Some Emerging Perspectivess
264. Rhetoricians and philosophers continue to explore the constituents of additional ethical positions that profitably may inform the ethics of
265. rhetoric. In The Abuse of Casuistry, Albert Jon-
266. sen and Stephen Toulmin argue for the rehabilitation and modern adaptation of casuistry (or
267. paradigm case ethics)—a method for ethical reasoning and judgment developed from ancient
268. Greek times through the sixteenth century and
269. a method that could encompass general rules and
270. particular circumstances. Smith explores the use
271. of the casuistic method for the rhetoric of physicians, and Boeyink views casuistry as useful
272. for journalistic ethics. Another issue probed by
273. scholars is the possibility of ethical standards for
274. cross-cultural or intercultural communication.
275. Can there be an overarching, transcendent ethic
276. for rhetoric between people of different cultures?
277. Is the Golden Rule at all adequate for cross-cultural discourse if the person from the other cul-
278. ture does not wish to be treated as I would like
279. to be treated? Johannesen (216–20) evaluates
280. several sets of suggested ethical standards for
281. cross-cultural rhetoric. In Ethnic Ethics, Anthony Cortese attempts to restructure moral
282. theory away from absolute, certain, universal
283. ethical norms to contingent and culture-bound
284. ethics.
285. An emphasis on duties, obligations, rules,
286. principles, and the resolution of complex ethical
287. dilemmas has dominated the contemporary philosophy of ethics. This dominant emphasis has
288. been true whether as variations on Immanuel
289. Kant's categorical imperative, John Rawls's depersonalized veil of ignorance to determine justice, on statements of intrinsic ultimate good, or
290. on Jeremy Bentham's or John Stuart Mill's utilitarian views. The past several decades, however,
291. have witnessed a growing interest among ethi-
292. cists in a largely ignored tradition that goes back
293. at least as far as Plato's and Aristotle's philosophies of ethics. This largely bypassed tradition
294. typically is called virtue ethics or character ethics. Most ethicists of virtue or character see that
295. perspective as a crucial complement to the current dominant ethical theories. Ethicists describe
296. virtues variously as deep-rooted dispositions,
297. habits, skills, or traits of character that incline
298. persons to perceive, feel, and act in ethically right
299. and sensitive ways. Also they describe virtues as
300. learned, acquired, cultivated, reinforced, capable
301. of modification, capable of conflicting, and ideally coalesced into a harmonious cluster. Johannesen (1991) explores at length the implications
302. of virtue ethics for the role of "character" in contemporary American political rhetoric. Herrick
303. examines the nature of rhetorical virtues as
304. rooted in rhetoric as a human practice.
305. Feminist scholars offer critiques of male-
306. dominated ethical traditions with special focus
307. on ways in which traditional ethics have functioned to subordinate or trivialize women's
308. ethical experience; rather, the moral experiences of women and men are worthy of equal
309. respect (Jaggar). Among other things, feminist
310. ethicists question the privileging of rationality
311. over emotion, of universalizability and detachment over particularity and engagement, of the
312. public sphere of discourse over the private
313. sphere, and of individualism over relationships. Feminist scholars argue the case against
314. sexist language, and some argue for the necessity to slant the truth in order to survive in a
315. male-dominated world. Steiner explores the
316. implications of feminist perspectives for an
317. adequate ethics of communication. Gilligan,
318. Noddings, and Manning are three feminist scholars who examine an ethic of care
319. rooted in relationships, interdependence of self
320. and others, compassion and nurturance, and
321. concrete responsibilities as an equal complement to the presently dominant ethic of justice
322. rooted in individual autonomy, impartial rules,
323. rights, and a logic of equality and fairness. In
324. evolution are both a specifically feminist ethic
325. of rhetoric (as part of feminist conceptions of
326. the rhetorical process) and an ethic of rhetoric inclusive of feminist perspectives.
327. Postmodern theorists and social critics
328. problematize modernist assumptions of truth,
329. reality, and knowledge as certain, immutable,
330. transparent, universal, solely discovered by
331. pure rationality, and uncontaminated by power and historical conditions. Postmodern perspectives see theories as only partial views of
332. their objects and see knowledge, reality, and
333. truth as contingent, constructed in communication, and conditioned by historical circumstances. Most postmodernists question the
334. notion of an autonomous self able to decide
335. apart from the social, economic, institutional,
336. and linguistic context in which that self is immersed and constructed. Postmodern theorists
337. unmask and question the fundamental rules,
338. norms, and procedures imbedded in cultures
339. and institutions that are unquestioned, taken for granted, and simply taken as the way
340. things are. While postmodern theorists differ
341. on a number of concerns, virtually all demonstrate concern for exposing the (often unnoticed) rules, roles, and regulations for discourse and language as major determinants of
342. self, institutions, and cultures. Based on the
343. works of Jean-Francois Lyotard and of Michel
344. Foucault, Porter sketches a locally and communally based postmodern ethics of composition and rhetoric that centers on how the values of the individual, community, discipline/
345. field, and tradition/culture intersect in the
346. composing process. Cooper builds upon the
347. works of Foucault, Jiirgen Habermas, and
348. Nancy Fraser to present a postmodern ethic
349. for political advocacy that focuses on the need
350. to re-create the citizen, on procedures that
351. open up citizen access to means of communication, and on consequences of political discourse that preserve citizens as agents who
352. genuinely can participate in government.
