The ancient Greek word ητoρικ translated as
"rhetoric" in English. Rhêma is a word that can
be traced back to Homeric days and refers toa
"saying" or "that which issaid." In the Iliad,
Achilles is referred to as a mythôn te rhêtêr
(9.443), a phrase that is usually translated as "a
speaker of words" but in context is better understood as a "teller of tales." The word rhêtêr
appears only once in all of the surviving texts
prior to the middle of the fifth century B.C.E.
During the fifth century, the masculine form
rhêtôr emerged as a quasi-technical legal term
used to refer to those who spoke often in the
assembly or in the law courts. Though rhêtôr
literally means "speaker," the word conveyeda
sense similar to the modern-day word politician, asall political discourse of the time was
presented orally.
The -ikê ending in Greek connotes skill or
art. Thus, rhêtorikê literally means "art or skill
of the rhêtôr. " Rhêtorikê can be used as an adjective such that one can call someone or something "rhêtôrly" or "rhetorical," or it can be
used as a substantive, as in the word rhetoric.
The Greek word rhêtorikê cannot be found in
any text that can be dated confidently priorto
Plato's dialogue Gorgias, usually dated 385
B.C.E. Though it iscertainly possible that the
word was in use prior to this date, several scholars have conjectured that Plato may have coined
the word inorder to describe the teachingsof
his competitors and to distinguish it from his
own teachings. Such a conjecture has been
supported by the absence of the word rhêtorikê
in many texts of the same era (including the surviving texts ofIsocrates), and by Plato's documented habit of coining many new words with
-ikê endings to describe various arts. In particular, a series of verbal arts were first described by
Plato with such terms as dialectic, eristic, and
antilogic, making it a respectable possibility
that rhetoric is alsoa Platonic coinage.
Edward Schiappa
University of Minnesota

342. Rogerian Rhetoric
343. Refers to theories and practices derived from
344. psychologist Carl R. Rogers's ideas about communication and teaching. The introduction of
345. Rogers to the field of rhetoric and composition
346. is attributed to Young, Becker, and Pike (1970)

349. for teaching argument. Later Hairston (1982)

352. by Zappen (1980), has influenced strategies for negotiation, mediation, and problem-

354. Initial questions about the usefulness of adapting Rogers's ideas to rhêtôrical theory and teaching writing asked whether
355. Rogerian principles constituted a rhetoric
356. distinct from Aristotelian argumentation
357. and whether they could be transferred to writing from the face-to-face, oral communication of individual and group counseling or negotiation. Rogers objected to the formulaic
358. use of his principles as techniques or steps
359. abstracted from specific therapeutic situations. Trying not to be reductive, however, some
360. teachers extended Rogers's "person-centered"
361. principles into invention and oral discourse
362. for "active listening," peer response groups,
363. and collaborative writing.
364. Pedagogically, Rogerian principles apply to
365. all aspects of the composing process, from individual or group activities before drafting to
366. writing the final product. For argumentation
367. and problem-solving, where they have been
368. taught most often, Rogerian strategies differ
369. from the approaches of forensic and adversarial
370. argument. Rogers stressed the interconnections
371. of thought and emotion, not only in specific acts
372. of communication but also in explanations of
373. the transactions.
374. In an influential 1951 paper, Rogers stated,

376. feelings so well that you could summarize them" (331–32). "Active listening" was

379. Rogerian empathy is bound up in ethos
380. and ethics. Although Rogers recognized that
381. empathy could be used for hostile as well
382. as growth-enhancing ends, he objected to
383. the use of his principles manipulatively for the
384. adversarial purpose of winning an argument.

388. take the risk of changing rationally and emotionally on an issue is, for Rogers, to make an
389. effort to cooperate and negotiate for mutually
390. satisfactory solutions to problems.
391. Rogers's rationalistic assumptions for
392. conflict resolution reflect the influences of
393. John Dewey and William H. Kilpatrick's ideas

396. held that differences could be resolved rationally and harmonious relationships established. He extended his principles from
397. the therapist-client situation to that of larger

401. all discourse situations. Feminist and social critics, in particular, object to Rogers's psychologizing rhetoric and humanistic psychotherapy. Yet
402. Rogers's contributions to the theory and practice
403. of mutual communication and student-centered,
404. experiential learning form a rhetoric that facilitates individual and social development.

430. Roman Rhetoric
431. An elaborate program of study and training,
432. designed to produce an individual who could,
433. as the occasion demanded, respond with appropriate and effective language. The system itself
434. endured almost two thousand years. From the
435. second century B.C.E. well into the eighteenth
436. century, Roman rhetoric was taught, learned,
437. and practiced. The concept of Roman rheto-
438. ric is something of a misnomer. Actually,
439. the Greeks had developed rhetorical theory and
440. rhetorical pedagogy to a remarkable state prior
441. to the takeover of Greece by Rome in the second century. Nonetheless, the Romans in the
442. first and second century did much to modify
443. existing Greek rhetorical theory so that it would
444. be better suited and better adapted to their own
445. civic and political purposes.
446. For the Romans, prior to the second century B.C.E., the tutorial method of instruction
447. was the predominant educational practice.
448. A Roman young man would apprentice himself to an older, more established individual and
449. would learn from this older person what was required in a given profession. For example, if one
450. wished to become a legal advocate, one would
451. apprentice oneself—or one's family wouldapprentice him—to an already successful individual. Much like contemporary guild systems
452. wherein one progresses through a lengthyapprenticeship to master a craft or trade, the early
453. Roman tutorial format had both advantages
454. and weaknesses. The relatively small number of
455. aristocratic, patrician families made professional apprenticeship possible. Without doubt
456. one could learn politics or military science or
457. legal pleading from the precepts and daily practice of an experienced mentor. As an educational arrangement, however, tradition is privileged over innovation, custom over creativity.
458. As the Republic began to expand and as Rome's
459. middle class also expanded, the situation became susceptible to change. The Greek method
460. of rhetorical schooling met the cultural need.
461. When Greece was conquered by the Romans, many of the teachers of rhetoric were
462. taken to Rome as slaves. Their knowledge and
463. experience in teaching the art of persuasion, an
464. art with pragmatic and utilitarian consequences, was perceived as valuable for a young
465. Roman destined for a civic career. As some of
466. these educated slaves gained their freedom, they
467. established schools. Teaching rhetoric to the
468. young Romans caused them to be expelled
469. in 161 B.C.E., in part because their teachings
470. seemed not practical and ill-suited to the Roman belief in the customs of their ancestors and
471. the old ways of learning. Nonetheless, the inherent practicality of rhetoric, particularly in the
472. law courts and assemblies, soon proved to be an
473. indispensable skill, and once recognized as such,
474. rhetoric became an accepted requirement for
475. Roman aristocrats.
476. We know, for example, that L. Plotius Gal-
477. lus was teaching in Latin a system of rhetoric
478. when he was censored in the first century.
479. By this first century, however, the way rhetoric
480. was taught had become codified into five interrelated arts. Two extant treatises, Cicero's
481. De inventione (89 B.C.E.) and the Rhetorica ad
482. Herennium (86 B.C.E.), clearly attest to the con-
483. cretization and codification of Roman rhetoric
484. as early as the first century B.C.E. This program
485. of study and training was broken down into a
486. three-part system. Precepts, rhetorical rules,
487. were taught and had as their goal theoretical
488. understanding which would undergird performance and practical application. Second, practice of various forms and modes of discourse
489. formed a significant part of the Roman rhêtôrical schema; finally, imitation was used to reinforce and expand what was learned in the precept and practice configurations. One can easily
490. see the impact of Isocrates and his teaching of
491. nature, art, and practice in the fourth and third
492. centuries B.C.E. in this three-part methodology.
493. Each of these three will be considered in turn.
494. The Precepts of Roman Rhetoric
495. Our sources for the codified rules of Roman
496. rhetoric are the Rhetorica ad Herennium,
497. Cicero's De inventione, Partitione oratoria,
498. Topica, Brutus, Orator, and Quintilian's
499. Institutio oratoria. From these primary sources,
500. one learns that the theoretical material of Roman rhetoric was divided and subdivided, and
501. classified and subclassified. The three genres—
502. judicial, deliberative, and demonstrative—
503. formed a structural pattern under which the
504. precepts could be addressed. Knowing that the
505. Romans were litigious and that one could attain
506. fame in the Roman culture by distinguishing
507. oneself in the law courts, it is not surprising to
508. discover that most of the attention of the rhetoricians was focused on the judicial or forensic
509. genre.
