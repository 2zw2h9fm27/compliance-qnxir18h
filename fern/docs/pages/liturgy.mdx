## Definition & Etymology

<div className="prose prose-lg max-w-none leading-relaxed bg-emerald-50 border-emerald-200 border-l-4 pl-6 py-2 pr-4 rounded-r-lg">

In the modern age, denotes a wide range of corporate religious ritual; used in reference to the
written formulae for rituals as well as for the enactment of the religious ceremonies themselves;
applies to the rituals of the Hopi Indians and
Buddhists as well as to those of Christians. For
most cultures and religions, liturgy functions in
common ways: it commemorates sacred events
and personages; it marks sacred time; it thanks,
praises, and supplicates deity; it mystically joins
humans with God or the Gods or Creation; it
binds believers together into communities of understanding, faith, moral conviction, and behavior; and it places humanity within a meaningful cosmic context.
Liturgy refers to the ceremony, song, chanting, reading, preaching, praying, acting, dancing,
gesture, use of visual symbols, and every other aspect of communal religious worship. It refers to ceremonies for initiation, healing, marriage, birth, and
death, as well as to ceremonies that order the structure of leadership within the religious community.
Control of the liturgy has long meant profound
influence in the deepest dimensions of humanrelations—spiritual, psychological, and political.
The word liturgy has acquired its broad use
only in the past century or so, and it is still not universally used in this way. For example, in Eastern
Christendom, the word refers exclusively to the
celebration of the Eucharist (commonly called
"Mass" in the Roman tradition), the central ritual
of all Christian churches. The Eucharist is the
ceremony wherein believers eat bread and drink
wine or water to commemorate the atoning sacrifice of Jesus Christ in either a symbolic or, as some
traditions believe, literal partaking of His flesh and
blood. It is the ritual that they believe mystically
unites them with the spiritual Body of Christ.
The word liturgy is derived from Latin
liturgia, an adoption of the Greek λειτovργα
( ργov, "work or act" +λαóζ, "people, public"—meaning a work for the people. In ancient
Greek usage, it referred to works done for the
state, usually by wealthy citizens. Early Christians adopted the word to describe the "people's
work" of their communal worship of Christ in
the Eucharist. The windings of usage of liturgy
since the early days of Christendom are too
lengthy to trace here; modern liturgists use the
word in its broad sense both when looking back
on religious ritual and when describing modern
formulae and practice. This includes reference
not only to the Eucharist but also to a broad
range of worship services and ceremonies.
A common method of classification is to
refer to all the liturgical formulae and practices
of a tradition as the "rites" of that tradition—for
example, the "Byzantine Rite," "Syrian Rite,"
"Roman Rite," and so on. Each "rite" contains
multiple subgroups, and within each group categories of ritual are recognized—for example, in
addition to the Eucharist, there are other ceremonies that may be performed by priests, some that
may be performed only by bishops, and some
that may be performed by laypersons. The principal liturgical books corresponding to these
categories in the Roman Rite have historically
been called the "missal," the "pontifical," and
the "divine office" (containing prayers, songs,
and so forth, to be performed at set hours).
Liturgists generally divide the study of liturgy
into three broad categories: biblical and historical,
theological, and practical or pastoral (White
1990:2). Some liturgists distinguish the verbal dimension of liturgy from that part which is purely
ritualized action, music, or visual symbolism. This
is the category of liturgy most directly related to
rhetorical studies, yet there is a rhetorical dimension to the entire range of liturgical devices—consider rhetorician Kenneth Burke's definition of human beings as creatures that respond to symbols.
Christian liturgies have undergone vast and
varied development over the two millennia since
Christ. Imagine the view from the earliest known
codification of Christian ceremony (the Didache,
c. 100) through the thousands of rule books,
lectionaries, prayer books, hymnals, breviaries,
missals, and other liturgical books and liturgical rulings and guidelines produced by all the branches of Christianity through the ages. Reflect on
developments in church calendars as the number
of saints, miracles, and holy days swelled through
the years. Review the developments in religious art,
architecture, vestments, and the cornucopia ofliturgical objects. Consider the writings and sermons
of Luther, Calvin, Zwingli, Knox, and all the famous reformers and preachers as they held forth on
the spirit and forms of true worship. Consider the
combinations and recombinations of liturgical lore
as traditions have borrowed from each other, reacted against each other, adapted liturgical doctrines and practices to their cultures, their political
and intellectual and spiritual frameworks, their
agendas. The history of Christian worship reveals
an immense variety of forms.
From the point of view of those who believe that there is only one correct way to worship, this variety can be deeply disturbing. But
another point of view rejoices in the diversity of
Christian worship, or at least argues to allow it:
The fact is, that God has established in no
church, any particular form, or manner of
worship, for promoting the interests of religion. The scriptures are entirely silent on
these subjects, under the gospel dispensation,
and the church is left to exercise her own discretion in relation to all such matters. . . .The
only thing insisted upon under the gospel dispensation, in regard to measures, is that there
not suppose that by "order" we are to understand any particular set mode, in which
any church may have been accustomed to
perform their service. (Finney 273, 276)
Liturgical evolution within Christianity is integral with the development of Christianity itself,
and so has had its periods and places of tight
control by church authorities and of freedom.
The modern age has seen tremendous freedom
in liturgical practice, freedom attained largely
through the efforts of "liturgical movements"
aimed at adapting traditional rites to the needs
of modern communities and cultures. Among the
hallmarks of recent liturgical reform has been the
Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy by Vatican
Council II (1963), which brought the work of
communal worship closer to the Roman Catholic people by allowing greater lay participation,
vernacular language, more emphasis on Liturgy
of the Word (preaching/instruction), improved
liturgical education, simplification of some rites,
and other features designed to make worship
more meaningful to laypeople. Many Protestants, for centuries endeavoring to distinguish
themselves from Catholic traditions by making
preaching the centerpiece of their meetings, have
enriched their services by reaching back to draw
upon common liturgical sources with Catholics.
At the same time that diversity in religious
practice is being more widely celebrated, ecumenical trends are celebrating the common
ground shared by Christians. Even so, that common ground does not extend all the way to the
Lord's Table in every tradition; many Christians
still bar each other from Communion. The center place of Christian liturgy, the Lord's Supper,
the place where Jesus invited his disciples to "be
one" in him, ironically stands as an age-old
barrier to Christian unity.
Russel Hirst
University of Tennessee

24. Locke, John (1632–1704)
25. English philosopher whose theories of language, epistemology, logic, morals, politics, and
26. education profoundly changed rhetorical theory
27. and practice in the eighteenth century and beyond. Particularly relevant to rhetoric are his
28. Essay Concerning Human Understanding,
29. Some Thoughts Concerning Education, and
30. The Conduct of the Understanding, in which he
31. demonstrates that humans are free and able to
32. use their reason to think and judge reality for
33. themselves. The more theoretical Essay shows
34. how human understanding works, emphasizing
35. language and how its "abuse" can defeat the
36. understanding and check the growth of knowledge. The works on education and logic extend
37. similar theories into practice. The popularity of
38. Locke's texts helped bring about the turn from
39. traditional rhetoric toward new theories of
40. knowledge, aesthetics, and psychology in eighteenth-century rhetoric, especially in Scotland.
41. Locke's family belonged to the smaller landowning gentry of the Puritan trading class. As a
42. reward for his father's battles on the side of Parliament in the Civil War, Locke was able to receive
43. a true gentleman's education and rise in social
44. standing. Early in his career he was an Oxford
45. don and a physician. In 1667 he became physician, secretary, and counselor to Lord Anthony
46. Ashley Cooper (later first Earl of Shaftesbury),
47. liberal politician who influenced Locke's mature,
48. more progressive turn in philosophy. Shaftesbury
49. also introduced Locke to the economic and political activities that would span his career. Locke's
50. early collaboration with the physician Thomas
51. Sydenham strongly influenced him to systematically study medicine. Other important influences
52. were Boyle, Descartes, and Gassendi. Early in his
53. career, Locke spent four years in France. Under
54. suspicion for his association with the radical politics of Shaftesbury, Locke fled to Holland, where
55. he remained in exile from 1683 to 1689, a period
56. of intense philosophical production. He returned
57. to England in 1689, escorting the Princess of
58. Orange, later Queen Mary. In 1691 Locke took
59. up residence at Oates with Sir Francis and Lady
60. Masham. He died at Oates on October 28,1704.
61. The Essay on Human Understanding was
62. begun shortly after Locke officially became a
63. Royal Society member in 1668. It was not published until 1689, however, after nearly twenty
64. years of constant revision. The next year, Two
65. Treatises of Government, written perhaps a decade earlier, was published anonymously. The
66. latter work set forth Locke's critique of Robert
67. Filmer's conservative Patriarchia, along with his
68. revolutionary theories of human freedom, labor,
69. and property. Some Thoughts Concerning Education, a compilation of advice on child-rearing for
70. a distant relative, first appeared in 1693. His
71. logic, The Conduct of the Understanding, was not
72. published until two years after his death in 1704.
73. In Ayer's terms, Locke's groundbreaking Essay sets out a philosophy of "anti-dogmatic realism" coming prior to the subsequent idealism or
74. conceptualism that tends to cloud our own historical vision. The Essay takes as its approach the
75. "historical, plain method" of Boyle and Newton,
76. its scope ranging from a first book denying innate
77. ideas through his argument in Book 2 that all
78. knowledge comes from experience, to a discussion
79. in Book 4 of knowledge as a set of mental operations performed upon ideas. Throughout, but concentrated in Book 3, "Of Words," we find Locke's
80. thoughts on language. Locke theorizes that words
81. are signs for ideas, and that all humans can ever
82. know are their own ideas of things, not the real
83. "essences" of the things themselves. Ayers calls
84. Locke's ideational theory "concept-empiricism,"
85. in which all our ideas derive from experience, as
86. opposed to "knowledge-empiricism," in which
87. propositional knowledge is ultimately based on
88. sensory knowledge (1:14).
89. Locke's Essay called words "signs of internal
90. conceptions," "marks for the Ideas within [Man's]
91. own Mind, whereby they might be made known
92. to other, and the Thoughts of Men's Minds be
93. conveyed from one to another" (3.i.l.2, 402), a
94. theory linguists have labeled telementation. These
95. mentalistic conceptions, or ideas, Locke defines
96. early on in his Essay as "whatever is the object of
97. the understanding when a man thinks" (l.i.8.47).
98. This linguistic theory also has been called semantic
99. idealism, because statements have no meaning
100. unless they correspond to ideas present in the
101. speaker's mind. The privacy and individuality his
102. language theory entails leads to communicational
103. skepticism because no two individuals will have
104. precisely the same ideas in mind at the same time
105. (although the ideas may have a qualitative sameness). This lack of faith in clear communication
106. poses a problem for scientific discourse and
107. knowledge that Locke attempts to solve in the
108. Essay by laying down rules for language use.
109. Locke's epistemology in the Essay revised
110. Aristotelian and late scholastic thinking. Locke
111. describes the process of coming to know as
112. a two-level operation: First the senses perceive,
113. then the mind reflects on these perceptions. Sense-
114. perception itself is involuntary and passive, but re-
115. flection is an active process under human control.
116. Along with this perception-reflection duality,
117. Locke offers another pair important to knowledge—that of the primary or secondary qualities
118. of things. Primary or "original" qualities areessential to physical things, while secondary qualities are colors, sounds, smells, and tastes produced
119. in us by the primary qualities. He classifies ideas
120. into the simple or complex—singular ideas or
121. ideas compounded of simples, a traditional framework. Simple ideas cannot be learned from language through a process of definition but must be
122. obtained directly from experience in a passive
123. manner. Complex ideas are voluntary compound-
124. ings or combinations of simple ideas. They come
125. more under human control and can generally be
126. transmitted by language, if with difficulty. To
127. understand and communicate clearly, complex
128. ideas must be analytically divided into their component simples and words applied to the simple
129. ideas that make up the complex idea.
130. The discussion of complex ideas takes up
131. most of Book 3, "On Words," with Locke further
132. breaking down complex ideas into "modes,"
133. "substances," and "relations." Modes are further
134. divided into simple and mixed, mixed modes representing our abstract ideas such as "beauty,"
135. "gratitude," "glory," and "theft." Locke describes
136. these abstractions as nothing but bundles of ideas
137. tied together by a word used as a "knot" (3.iii.l7–
138. 19,417–19). Substances such as "gold," "man,"
139. or "horse" (actually mixtures of simple ideas)
140. cannot be adequately known because we can only
141. know the ideas they produce in us. But mixed
142. modes and relations are always adequate to the
143. human-created reality of the mental patterns they
144. represent. Thus through mixed modes in particular, a protoconstructivism enters Locke's Essay.
145. Locke's theory of language, in contrast to earlier Adamic theories, emphasizes the conventional
146. or arbitrary nature of words. He argues against the
147. "double conformity" of signs—their conformity
148. both to the things they represent and, intersubjec-
149. tively, to others' ideas of things. Not only are signs
150. and signified only arbitrarily linked, words do not
151. reveal essence and knowledge. Instead, they get in
152. the way, like clouded windows, obscuring the
153. possibility of clearly knowing anything.
154. Despite Locke's frequent emphasis on words,
155. he is not a nominalist because of the priority of
156. mental ideas. However, Locke sees knowledge and
157. words as closely connected, or "scarcely separable," even if they are not the same. Since language
158. is imperfect, the closeness of language to knowledge creates difficulties that he attempts to resolve
159. by setting rules for language use. In Book 3
160. (x.490–508), he cites seven imperfections of
161. words and discusses remedies for them. These
162. abuses include first, using words with no clear
163. ideas; using words inconsistently; using jargon or
164. "affected obscurity," taking words for things instead of ideas; making words stand for the real
165. essences of things; using words whose meaning is
166. unclear to others; and finally, using figurative
167. speech, which Locke calls "perfect cheat" in a discourse intended to instruct and where judgment
168. might thereby be influenced by the passions. In
169. expanding on this final abuse, Locke makes his often-cited denunciation of a figurai rhetoric, portraying rhetoric as a seductress attempting to deceitfully draw men away from truth (3.x.34, 508).
170. This Lockean attitude to figurative language
171. was immensely influential in the centuries to
172. come. Nietzsche begins his course in ancient
173. rhetoric by citing Locke's famous passage denigrating rhetoric as proof of the disrepute into
174. which the art of rhetoric had fallen in his day. We
175. should note that in his Essay Locke separates
176. "civil" (or rhetorical) and "philosophic" or scientific uses of language, believing that language
177. was adequately communicative for most nontechnical uses. At the same time, his Essay attempts to create a new scientific model of discourse as a model for what amounts to all
178. nontrivial communication. Those writing after
179. Locke often accepted his epistemology andaccompanying rules for scientific language as extending to all language use. This trend to make
180. scientific discourses the exemplar for all discourses of truth was part of a discursive shift bearing profound implications for future rhetorics.
181. The Essay also included a theory of attraction and repulsion of ideas similar to the Port
182. Royal logic that Locke read while in France.
183. Knowledge, or Truth, to Locke is the perception
184. of the agreement or disagreement of ideas, "as
185. Things signified by them do agree or disagree one
186. with another" (4.V.2.574). Setting up a hierarchy
187. of certainty, Locke writes that knowledge can
188. come directly and immediately through intuition;
189. or, mediately, by reason or demonstration involving chains of inference; or directly by the senses.
190. The bringing of ideas together to compare or
191. judge their agreement or disagreement is a rational, conscious, and controlled mental operation.
192. Involuntary association of ideas is suspect and its
193. connections rejected by Locke as eccentric, even
194. dangerously linked with madness. Likewise,
195. quickness in making connections between ideas
196. is disparaged as "Wit," placed into opposition
197. with the more valued "judgment," as in Hobbes.
198. Theories of associationism shaping eighteenth-
199. century rhetoric are often attributed to Locke;
200. nonetheless, Locke's own theory of association
201. is part of an attempt to control involuntaryassociation and enforce rationality.
202. Howell represents Locke's views on logic as
203. the pivotal moment in a shift from an old to a
204. new rhetoric. Locke rejects topical invention in
205. both logic and rhetoric and prefers induction and
206. chains of inference to the syllogism. In his chapter on reason in Book 4, Locke vigorously rejects
207. the syllogism along with scholastic logical methods, calling them more aimed at winning disputes than arriving at truth. Locke's Conduct of
208. the Understanding, published in 1706 and designed to be an appendix to the Essay, contains
209. rules and guides to constitute a new, more effective "method" for gaining knowledge. Howell
210. writes that the work "made twenty appearances
211. in print" by 1805, often being used as a logic text
212. by British universities (278).
213. Locke, who disparaged the relevance and
214. efficacy of contemporary Latin logic and rhetoric,
215. wrote only scattered comments on rhetoric, and
216. interpretations of his influence on rhetoric vary.
217. The Essay, following texts by Ramus, Descartes,
218. and Bacon, implies a rhetoric stressing empiricist
219. values, realism, strict rationality, and the correct
220. use of words coupled with a plain style. These values run consistently through his other texts that
221. allude to rhetoric and writing. Some Thoughts
222. Concerning Education began as a series of letters
223. from Holland to a distant relative, Edward
224. Clarke, addressing the problem of educating an
225. average boy who would be a typical member of
226. the English landed gentry. Thus the Education
227. writings are about a boy's preparation for "a
228. Gentleman's calling" and view speaking and writing as practical activities and gentlemanly social
229. graces. Locke's advice on writing in this educational treatise emphasizes practice, repetition, and
230. error correction over inventional, aesthetic, or
231. imaginative activities. Perhaps his strongest impact on rhetorical training derived from his advocacy of replacing Latin language education with
232. a "naturalistic" vernacular speaking and writing.
233. To Locke, the natural way of learning anything, but in particular a language, is by imitation
234. and habit. Thus he objects to teaching children
235. languages by "rules," emphasizing instead repetition and practice to develop good habits (Education sec. 167–69,219–28). An instructor of rhetoric while a don at Oxford, Locke discourages the
236. practice of theme-writing, especially in Latin; he
237. would dispense with written themes in favor of
238. oral questioning of students to train them in English extemporaneous speaking. However, he concedes that if themes must be written, theme-writing should be in English (sec. 171–72, 228–29).
239. His dislike of rules links with his disdain for rhetoric because he equates the "arts" of rhetoric with
240. "rules." Locke's treatise disparages the classically
241. based curriculum in rhetoric and writing and relies on two aspects: the use of models combined
242. with practice and repetition. He offers the Protestant theologian Chillingworth as a model for
243. reasoning and Cicero as a model of "the true Idea
244. of Eloquence" (Education sec. 188–89, 240).
245. In "Some Thoughts Concerning Reading and
246. Study for a Gentleman" (published in 1720), Locke
247. offers advice on the art of speaking. He describes
248. two components of the art, "Perspicuity" and
249. "Right Reasoning." The first consists of using the
250. proper words for ideas in the speaker's mind. Locke
251. advises that both perspicuity and right reasoning
252. can most readily be attained through reading models, "since we are more apt to learn by example
253. than by direction" (qtd. in Howell 500). Thus
254. Locke consistently recommends setting aside traditional arts and methods for a "natural" approach
255. based on practice and habit.
