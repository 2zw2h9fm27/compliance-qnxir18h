The belief that words have magical power. As
early as 415 B.C.E., the sophist Gorgias wrote
in his Encomium of Helen that speech can
"drug and bewitch the soul with a kind of evil
persuasion." Magic and rhetoric both traffic in
illusion; their common powers worry idealist
philosophers from antiquity forward, and
become a concern of both Enlightenment scientists and modern social critics. Magic rhetoric is fearful on two counts: (1) The magical
imagination is protean, and liable to disrupt
prevailing versions of truth; (2) "charismatic"
speech can mesmerize listeners into mindless
behavior.
Plato calls the sophistic rhetoricians dangerous magicians who—like Proteus—can give
truth different shapes. In the fifth century c.E.,
Augustine warns Christian orators to avoid the
"magic arts." In 1500 Gianfancesco Pico della
Mirandola summarizes medieval conceptions
of fantasy as a central activity of the composing imagination, and warns against demonic
phantasms. In 1667 Thomas Sprat conceives
London's Royal Society as the enemy of rhetoric and magic together, allying figurative language and witchery. The Enlightenment castigation of the magical properties of language is
met by the Romantic movement'sreassertion
of an expansive imagination, represented by
Coleridge's claim in the Biographia Literaria:
"I will cause the world of intelligences with the
whole system of their representations to rise up
before you" (13). Apart from ThomasDe
Quincey's rather eccentric connection of this
generative imagination to a lost art of rhetoric, the Romantic association of magic and language is displaced to poetics, and persists in
the neo-Romantic mysticism of William Butler Yeats. New concerns about the magical
powers of oratory, advertising, and the "culture industry" in general emerge in the 1940s
and 1950s from Frankfurt School philosophers
such as Theodor Adorno and Herbert Marcuse, who argue that the language of modern
authoritarianism is an automatizing magic.
That view is extended by Paulo Freire, who
calls "magic consciousness" the opposite of
"critical consciousness" in the philosophy of
education that he introduces in the 1960s and
1970s, and by radical feminists such as Mary
Daly in her 1987 Wickedary.
Surveying the history of magic, sociologist
Daniel Lawrence O'Keefe has noted thatsocial consensus determines the powers of both
rhetoric and magic: Magic, like rhetoric, "works because people agree that it
works" (96). O'Keefe's definition of magic as
collective social action corresponds with Kenneth Burke's designations of magic throughout his works as symbolic action. For Burke,
naming is a magical act because it "decrees"
the nature of an object or situation. In The
Philosophy of Literary Form, he calls "correct" magic the practice of multiplying decrees
through the action of broad and irresolute
social inquiry. In A Grammar of Motives and
A Rhetoric of Motives, Burke characterizes
"false" magic as an arhetorical, dehumanizing
act: The use of language to compel motion in
objects rather than to engender cooperation in
people. With Burke in mind, William Covino
contrasts "generative magic rhetoric" and "arresting magic rhetoric": The former is "constitutive inquiry, or the coercive expansion of the
possibilities for action," and the latter is"enforced doctrine, or the coercive reduction of
the possibilities for action."
William A. Covino
University of Illinois, Chicago

14. Language and the reproduction of society. In
15. everyday usage, rhetoric refers to a set of codes
16. or conventions operating when we produce or
17. interpret texts, an analytic practice used to
18. model, define, or critique this production, and
19. a pedagogy derived from such analysis. For
20. Marxism, analysis must come first and would,
21. like all other cultural practices, be subsumed
22. under Marxist theory in general. This follows
23. from the way Marxism locates itself as a theory. In contrast to other rhetorical practices,
24. Marxism does not understand itself as one of
25. many alternative ways of seeing. Indeed,
26. Marxism considers a rhetorical theory much
27. less a formal definition, such as the current one
28. as a material and ideological production circulating in fundamentally oppressive capitalist
29. relations. It would cast the notion of innocent
30. choices between rhetorics as an illusion of individual power that masks relations between
31. economic classes and constructs good political
32. subjects unaware of their conditions. So within
33. classical Marxism, there is no rhetoric. Rather,
34. Marxism offers a theory (its classical practitioners would say a "science") of human social
35. relations that logically precedes all other cultural systems. It offers a narrative of the historical struggle between classes and a set of
36. concepts that inform its analysis of culture.
37. However, because of the importance of communication as part of the material production
38. of culture, theorists interested in rhetoric have
39. adapted the historical narrative of Marxism
40. and many of its concepts.
41. Perhaps the central questions for a Marxist theory of rhetoric are how language and
42. rhetoric are part of the production of social,
43. especially class relations, and whether rhetoric
44. can help us understand and intervene in the
45. conditions of cultural production. For orthodox
46. Marxism, the answer to the first question is
47. dictated by the architectural metaphor that
48. Marx used to explain social structure. In this
49. theory, the economic base composed of the conditions and relations of production determines
50. all social relations and even the form of our
51. consciousness. Culture, including rhetoric, is
52. part of the superstructure determined by the
53. material conditions under which we live. Language and rhetoric thus help maintain the relations of production that constitute the economic
54. base. This conception of social structure means
55. that the answer to the second question—can we
56. know and intervene in our social condition?—
57. is an emphatic no. Since language and rhetoric
58. reside in this superstructure, to imagine that any
59. rhetorical system could transcend the controlling force of materiality is a romantic dream.
60. On such a model, rhetoric becomes part of the
61. production of our "false consciousness," the
62. ideology that submerses us in oppressive class
63. relations.
64. More recently, however, revisions of this
65. orthodox position by Antonio Gramsci, Louis
66. Althusser, and Raymond Williams have questioned the determining power of the base/superstructure relationship and rejected the orthodox
67. definition of ideology as false consciousness. In
68. doing so, they have vindicated language and
69. rhetoric as critical forces, allowing theorists to
70. reappropriate classical concepts and produce a
71. rhetoric that analyzes the way languagereproduces or actively disrupts existing social
72. relations.
73. Antonio Gramsci is perhaps best known
74. for his concept of "hegemony" and the effect
75. that this concept had on Marxism's thinking
76. about determination and the base/superstructure model. Gramsci separated society into
77. state and civil society, the state composed of
78. official, legal institutions of power, and civil
79. society made up of the whole range of institutions and daily practices that mediate between
80. the state and the economy. Gramsci argued
81. that hegemony was carried out by a range of
82. active and changing strategies within civilsociety, through which the dominant social class
83. secured the consent of the oppressed. Hegemony worked by diffusing the ideological position of the dominant class throughout the
84. fabric of social practices so that the special
85. interests of the powerful become identified as
86. the interests of society at large, become the
87. "common sense" to which we all automatically subscribe—"what's good for GM is good
88. for America." There are two consequences
89. of Gramsci's insight that created a powerful
90. opening for rhetoric. For Gramsci, hegemony
91. superseded "ideology" and meant that cultural
92. activity was not merely restricted to the economically determined superstructure. Now
93. hegemonic activity helped form economicactivity. A second advance created by Gramsci
94. was that hegemonic activity is a process of
95. continual struggle; if there is a powerful dominating hegemony, there are also "counter-hegemonies," competing forms of common sense
96. that disrupt the domination of a particular
97. class interest. This suggests that rhetoric can
98. analyze cultural discourses and identify ways
99. in which they construct a common sense that
100. serves the interests of a limited class and that
101. can identify possibilities for counterdiscourses.
102. Although his work is very different from
103. Gramsci's, Louis Althusser's revisions of the
104. concept of ideology and his idea of the "Ideological State Apparatuses" and their effect on
105. subjects expanded the opening Gramsci had
106. made for rhetoric. Althusser argued that
107. ideology was the largely unconscious way in
108. which we imagine our relations to the real, and
109. that it is in this realm of representation that
110. power struggles are fought. He also proposed
111. that rather than being controlled by one
112. uniform Ideology, society is composed of a
113. number of ideologies, each constructed in the
114. practices of a number of ideological apparatuses such as schooling, religion, and entertainment. These apparatuses, in turn, interpellate
115. us as subjects. That is, they call us into social
116. being in a position and identity already prepared for us by the dominant apparatuses.
117. Borrowing an idea from Foucault, we can say
118. that every statement creates a position—an
119. identity, authority, social place—for the subject
120. who utters it, and that Althusser's ideological
121. apparatuses regulate the kinds of statements
122. we can make, and thus the subject positions
123. available to us. With this theoretical understanding, rhetoric becomes a tool with
124. which to analyze the way apparatuses use language to represent the real, excluding ideas
125. that are detrimental and privileging those ways
126. of thinking and being that correspond to the
127. interests of the apparatus. Rhetoric also provides a way to explain how an institutional discourse reproduces repressive social relations
128. through the kinds of statements and, thus, the
129. subject positions it makes available.
130. One problem with Althusser's theory,
131. however, is that it describes subjects as if they
132. were completely determined by the structure of
133. dominant apparatuses and largely ignorant of
134. their condition. Reacting against these two
135. aspects of Althusser's work, theorists such as
136. Anthony Giddens, Chantai Mouffe, and Ernesto Laclau have recently argued that subjects
137. should be understood as cultural "agents"
138. capable of recognizing their place in ideological apparatuses. These apparatuses, in turn,
139. are themselves dynamic rather than static; they
140. exist only in the memory and ongoing practice
141. of social agents. This view of apparatuses as
142. changing and contingent leads to a conception
143. of society as an open and contested space rather than a closed, finite structure. Thus, Chantai
144. Mouffe and Ernesto Laclau argue that social
145. relations are constituted and organized by
146. shifting articulatory practices and that our
147. social character and relations are precarious.
148. For rhetoricians this means that as Mouffe and
149. Laclau argue, the "central problem is to
150. identify the discursive conditions for the emergence of collective action, directed toward
151. struggling against inequalities and challenging
152. relations of subordination" (153). The work of
153. a Marxist rhetoric then becomes the analysis
154. of these discursive conditions and constructing
155. a pedagogical practice as part of this "collective action."
156. One important site for this collectiveaction is schooling, and Marxism has shaped an
157. influential pedagogical theory. Theorists have
158. argued that schooling has replaced religion as
159. the most powerful apparatus, which canreproduce social relations in at least three important ways. First, schools often offer differential educational opportunities to produce
160. workers trained for jobs associated with dif-
161. ferent class positions. Less obviously, schooling is also a socialization process, teaching
162. forms of discipline and respect, and through
163. them the discipline and respect for social
164. forms. This "hidden curriculum" teaches students how to behave in hierarchical relations
165. of power. Finally, through a process that
166. Pierre Bourdieu and Jean Claude Passeron
167. call "symbolic violence," schooling legitimizes some forms of knowledge and not others, presenting as fact and common sense
168. knowledges that are historically constructed
169. and tied to material interests. Because the
170. teacher possesses the necessary "cultural capital," education participates in the hegemonic
171. process Gramsci described; it teaches students
172. to consent to essentially oppressive relations.
173. The first task of a Marxist pedagogy is to analyze how the discursive conditions of schooling—particularly the teaching of writing—
174. participate in this social reproduction and to
175. identify ways in which schooling can resist
176. this hegemonic tendency.
177. Because society is not a closed structure
178. and social agents can come to recognize their
179. conditions, Marxist rhetoric also sees education as a powerful site for ideological resistance and change. This pedagogical tradition,
180. variously called liberatory, radical, or critical
181. pedagogy, attempts to modify both the form
182. and content of education in order to help
183. students and teachers recognize the discursive
184. conditions and thus the social relations in
185. which they work. The most familiar source for
186. this pedagogical theory is the work of Paulo
187. Freire and that of associated theorists such as
188. Henry Giroux and Ira Schor. Freire's fundamental assumption is that education must help
189. us recognize the ways in which language constructs our subjectivity and our relations to the
190. social world. The purpose of education for
191. Freire is to bring students and teachers to a
192. point of cultural self-consciousness, what he
193. calls conscientizacao. This pedagogy entails a
194. classroom constructed as a dialogue between
195. students and teachers, and an openness to
196. knowledges and written forms that challenge
197. conventions and offer students and teachers
198. new, less oppressive subject positions from
199. which to speak and act.
