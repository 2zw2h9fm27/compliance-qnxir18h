178. Greek Rhetoric
179. The heart and soul of the Greek educational system in the classical period. The word rhetoric is
180. used in a variety of senses. Principal among these
181. are a stricter and a looser sense, the latter referring to the phenomenon of persuasive discourse
182. generally considered. In this entry the terms oratory and peitho (Greek for persuasion) will be
183. used to refer to the use of persuasive discourse;
184. rhetoric will be reserved, in its stricter sense, to
185. refer to the study of persuasive discourse. But in
186. whatever sense one uses the term, all must agree
187. that the Greeks figure crucially in its history: They
188. invented the term rhetoric itself. They raised the
189. practice of oratory to a position of central importance in their sociopolitical interactions; and they
190. were the first culture in the West to attempt to
191. codify the study of communication as such and
192. to systematize its precepts. And they bequeathed
193. that legacy to the Romans as well. For all these
194. reasons, Greek rhetoric is of fundamental importance to the study of rhetoric (in both senses).
195. The Archaic Period
196. In the strictest sense, it is untoward to speak of

200. (Note: All dates in this entry are B.C.E. unless otherwise noted.) But this does not mean that rhetoric itself did not exist or that its effects were not
201. noted. Even the ancients debated the nature and

203. in the Iliad and Odyssey (Kennedy 1957a)—
204. the oldest extant texts of Western literature

222. The Early Classical Period
223. Greek rhetoric, properly speaking, flourishes in
224. the high classical period, particularly under Plato
225. and Aristotle. But these philosophers were particularly indebted to their immediate predecessors, those thinkers generally known as the "pre-

227. thought of as a subcategory of the pre-Socratics.

311. tic thinkers, who wanted an uncomplicated,

313. of the negative picture many have drawn of them

318. manipulation of language. It is only in the past
319. century and a half that their reputation has begun to be rehabilitated, but the word sophistry

321. The High Classical Period
322. Certainly one of the pivotal figures in the history of rhetoric is Plato (427–347), the student
323. of Socrates and teacher of Aristotle. Because
324. what we have of his philosophy is couched not
325. in treatise but in dialogue form, it is a matter of
326. exceeding delicacy and difficulty to attempt to
327. formulate a simple "Platonic" doctrine of anything, and rhetoric is no exception. One cannot
328. know, first of all, how much Plato's portraits of
329. Socrates may be relied upon for historical accuracy, nor how much Plato may have agreed or
330. disagreed with Socrates on any given topic, nor
331. how (or how much) Plato's own opinions
332. may have changed over the years. These factors
333. should induce extreme caution in any scholarly
334. approach to Plato. Common opinion and perhaps the simplest interpretation is that Socrates
335. and Plato represent a conservative reaction to
336. the sophistic movement—a highly moralistic
337. approach to truth and ethics in all walks of life,
338. including the realm of language.
339. On this account the sophists are the villains
340. of the piece, Socrates the hero. Yet the distinctions are not easy to draw: In the dialogues,
341. Socratic dialectic often seems to involve a sort
342. of logical entrapment. He seems disingenuous
343. about his own (or anyone's) ability to achieve
344. certain knowledge. The process of elenchus in
345. the dialogues often leads merely to aporia, a
346. state in which previously unexamined and unsatisfactory positions have been abandoned but
347. are not replaced by positive alternatives. Furthermore, like the sophists, he took students,
348. with whom he was extremely popular, though
349. he seems not to have accepted payment for his
350. teaching; and he pays minute attention to the
351. choice and meaning of words. Finally, he is
352. portrayed in the Platonic dialogues in repeated,
353. and deep, conversation with various sophists.
354. Certainly, if our only evidence were the Clouds
355. of Aristophanes, it would be assumed without
356. question that Socrates was a sophist.
357. The Platonic Socrates, however, does seem
358. to disagree with some of the sophists he converses with on a number of important counts.
359. Perhaps the single most important text in this
360. regard is the Gorgias, in which he contends that
361. rhetoric is not in fact a technê, an art or discipline with its own discernible rules and methods, because it cannot give an account of its
362. own arkbai (first principles). Rather, he argues,
363. it is a "knack" picked up by practice. As a
364. techne its goal would be truth; as it is, it is aimed
365. only at flattery. This notion is picked up in the
366. Phaedrus, in which (it seems) Socrates allows
367. that there could be such a thing as a philosophical rhetoric, a true technê. In the latter dialogue,
368. such a rhetoric is closely aligned with the practice of dialectic. Another Platonic dialogue that
369. offers an acute study of the nature of peithô is
370. the Symposium, in which a number of people
371. at a drinking party offer formal orations in
372. honor of erôs (passionate love, personified as
373. the god Eros). The series of speeches offers a
374. broad disparity of viewpoints on erôs, but also
375. a variety of approaches to the nature of discourse. Some of them display features that are
376. recognizably sophistic—for example, an interest in antithesis, fine distinctions in verbal definition, and attention to stylistic matters in the
377. manner of Gorgias. The speech of Socrates, of
378. course, serves as a sort of climax to the series,
379. but in it he claims to have learned everything he
380. knows about erôs from a woman of Mantinea,
381. Diotima. Thus his "speech" is actually theaccount of his dialectical interchange with
382. Diotima.
383. Many other dialogues focus on rhetorical
384. matters in one way or another. Prominent
385. among these are the Apology of Socrates, which
386. dramatizes his defense before the Athenian
387. court that eventually sentenced him to death;
388. the Cratylus, which discusses the nature of language; the Menexenus, in which Socrates recites
389. a funeral-oration; and the Protagoras, in which
390. Socrates explicitly contrasts the use of dialectical question and answer with monologic oratorical delivery.
391. It must be emphasized again (1) that a careful reading of Plato will keep in mind the fact
392. that Socrates often propounds positions sheerly
393. for the sake of argument and that he will later
394. recant or modify them, so that it is not always
395. easy to tell when he is being ironic; and (2) that,
396. because of his narrative format, it is not entirely
397. clear when Plato is in agreement with his character Socrates, or whether the dialogues may in
398. fact be a critique of the Socratic position. Moreover, one cogent reading of Plato might be that
399. he is deconstructing all positions and all certainties (the "Socratic" as well as the "sophistic").
400. Aside from Plato and Aristotle, the most
401. important single figure in the history of rhetorical instruction during the high classical period
402. was Isocrates (436–338). Mentioned, perhaps
403. ironically, in Plato's Phaedrus as a promising
404. young thinker, Isocrates set up a school in Athens that was the greatest rival to Plato's Academy and Aristotle's Lyceum. Central to its curriculum was rhetorical instruction (Isocrates'
405. term was philosophia). Something of his educational philosophy can be learned from his
406. Against the Sophists, Helen, and Antidosis.
407. Among Isocrates' other remaining works—
408. speeches that were usually revised and published as pamphlets—are the Areopagiticus,
409. Busiris, Panathenaicus, Panegyricus, and
410. Philip. What has apparently not survived, unfortunately, is Isocrates' rhetorical handbook;
411. one ancient tradition has it that he destroyed it
412. himself. It is possible that it was an "esoteric"
413. work—that is, intended for use only within the
414. walls of his own school (Kennedy 1963:71–72).
415. Apparently, it included some notion of the
416. genres of oratory, a fairly conventional approach to the parts of the oration, and some
417. discussion of stylistic matters. It is also possible,
418. as in the case of Thrasymachus, that this "handbook" consisted of a collection of Isocrates'
419. speeches.
420. There is a tradition that Aristotle, on his arrival in Athens, spent some time with Socrates.
421. As this is chronologically impossible, it has been
422. suggested that "Socrates" here is a scribal error
423. for "Isocrates," and that in fact Aristotle studied for a time with Isocrates before going over
424. to Plato (Chroust 1973:1.94–101). This provocative suggestion would, if true, explain Aristotle's early interest in rhetoric; moreover, it
425. would give due recognition to the far-reaching
426. impact Isocrates has had on the rhetorical tradition in the Western world.
427. If one name were singled out as the most
428. important—for better or worse—in the history
429. of Greek rhetoric, it would have to be that of
430. Aristotle (384–322). His approach to rhetoric,
431. while it owes much to the Gorgias and Phae-
432. drus of Plato, was unlike anything that preceded
433. it; and it has influenced (if only by way of contrast) virtually every rhetorical system that has
434. followed it in Western culture. In fact, the history of Greek rhetoric might well be written in
435. terms of two strains or systems—the sophistical, which concentrated on teaching the moria
436. logou, or parts of the oration, and the Aristotelian (Solmsen 1941), these two traditions finding an eventual synthesis in the rhetorical treatises of Cicero and Quintilian (see the extended
437. treatments of these in Kennedy 1972).
438. The opening words of the Rhetoric are a
439. direct challenge to the contention in the Gorgias
440. that rhetoric is antistrophal to cooking: rather, says Aristotle, it is antistrophal to dialectic, Plato's pet practice. By this opening salvo,
441. Aristotle signifies that he is going to situate rhetoric as a genuine techne, and to offer a philosophical account of its principles. Rather than take a
442. moria logou approach, he breaks rhetoric down
443. according to the species of discourse and according to the parts of rhetoric. The species of discourse are three, each with its own telos, or goal,
444. and oriented toward its own time: sumbouleuti-
445. kon, "deliberative" (or "political") oratory, concentrating on the expedient, and aimed toward
446. future action; dikanikon, "judicial"(or "forensic") oratory, concentrating on the just, and
447. focusing on the determination of past fact; and
448. epideiktikon, "epideictic" (or "ceremonial") oratory, concentrating on praise and blame, and situated primarily in the present. The parts of rhetoric in Aristotle's schema are four, and these
449. account for the overall structure of the treatise:
450. heuresis (Latin inuentio, thefinding of argument), taxis (Latin disposition the arrangement of
451. the parts of the speech), lexis (Latin elocutio,
452. questions of style) and hupokrisis (Latin actio,
453. the delivery of the oration). Rather than concentrate on taxis and lexis, as in the sophistic tradition, Aristotle spends the bulk of his treatise—the first two books—on heuresis, which he
454. locates in three—(and only three) modesof
455. proof: ethos, the (perceived) good character of
456. the speaker; pathos, the emotion(s) aroused in
457. the audience by the discourse; and logos, the sua-
458. sive force of logical argument, as expressed by
459. deduction in the enthymeme or rhetorical syllogism, and by induction in the example. Each of
460. these is proper, or native, to a particular part of
461. the rhetorical situation: ethos to the rhetor; pathos to the audience; and logos to the words of
462. the speech itself. (The latter should be studied in
463. relation to the formal syllogism, which Aristotle
464. was the first, it seems, to discover and to write
465. about, in the Prior and Posterior Analytics; the
466. Topics and Sophistical Refutations discussthe
467. use of logic in dialectical situations.)
468. Issues of heuresis, including various topics
469. for enthymeme, various kinds of ethos, and the
470. arousal of various emotions, are treated seriatim
471. in Book 2 of the Rhetoric. Book 3 (which may
472. have been written at quite a different time than
473. the rest of the Rhetoric) treats the topics of lexis
474. and taxis. The section on lexis includes perceptive discussions of good and bad style; of figured
475. language, such as metaphor and simile; of grammatical correctness; of propriety in style; of prose
476. rhythms; and of periodicity, among other topics.
477. The section on taxis (in which Aristotle is on territory especially associated with the sophists) attempts to offer the most philosophical assessment
478. of the topic: The core of a speech is defined as
479. prothesis (proposition) and pistis (proof); to these
480. may be added proem and epilogue, but the last
481. two parts are not as essential as the first two are.
482. A treatise that dates from this period (some
483. time before 341) isthe Rhetorica ad Alexandrum
484. (so called because of a prefatory epistle dedicating the work to Alexander the Great). Formerly
485. attributed to Aristotle, it was probably written by
486. Anaximenes of Lampsacus. Its particular importance is as a prime example of sophistic rhetoric.
487. It is thought that the Rhetorica ad Alexandrum
488. may have been influenced by the technê of Isoc-
489. rates (q.v. above). There are, however, superficial
490. resemblances to the Aristotelian system: Chapters 1–5 correspond in their subject matterapproximately to Book 1 of Aristotle's Rhetoric;
491. chapters 6–22, to Rhetoric 2; and the rest of the
492. treatise to Rhetoric 3. (Chapters 23–28 are on
493. style, chapters 29 –37 on arrangement, and chapter 38 covers several miscellaneous topics.)
494. The Postclassical Period
495. As the tutor of Alexander, son of Philip of Mace-
496. don, Aristotle might well be said to have had a direct impact on the shaping of Hellenistic culture.
497. He seems to have fostered Alexander's love of
498. things Greek, and wherever Alexander's empire
499. expanded, the Greek language went too as a lingua franca. That of course brought with it the
500. ability to read Greek literature, as well as anentree into Greek rhetorical training. With the rise
501. of Rome, their eventual creation of a republic, and
502. their interest in Greek culture, the avid Roman
503. interest in rhetoric was a natural development.
504. There was no single Greek theorist after Aristotle who matched his stature in the field of rhetoric, but several Greek thinkers of the postclassical
505. period deserve mention. First among these chronologically would be Aristotle's pupil Theo-
506. phrastus (c. 370–c. 287), much of whose work is
507. now lost, but whose interesting Characters survives, along with some botanical and other scientific treatises and some work on metaphysics. It
508. appears that Theophrastus' rhetorical treatises ran
509. the gamut from invention, arrangement, and style
510. to delivery. He wrote on enthymemes and epicheir-
511. emes, on topoi, and on the use of humor. Of particular interest is his now-lost On Style,which can
512. be reconstructed in a sketchy way from the testimony of Cicero (Kennedy 1957b). While, as has
513. recently been demonstrated, there was already
514. some conscious taxonomy of the kinds of style in
515. the fifth century (O'Sullivan), the notion of an
516. array of virtues of style had received its earliest
517. clear expression in the Rhetoric of Aristotle; it was
518. this hint that Theophrastus picked up in his On
519. Style. His categories seem to have been correctness
520. (hellenismos—that is, the good use of Greek), clarity (to saphes), propriety (to prepon), andornament (perhaps kataskeue). It does seem, too, that
521. he offered (but did not entirely originate) a threefold taxonomy of the kinds of style—plain, intermediate (or "mixed"), and grand.
522. It is difficult to date securely the author,
523. known as Demetrius, of a treatise On Style. He
524. was formerly identified with Demetrius of Phal-
525. eron, an Athenian statesman and orator born
526. c. 350, and a contemporary (and student) of
527. Theophrastus'. It is now thought, however, that
528. this treatise must date from the third, second, or
529. even first century. On Style is of particular interest in terms of its contribution to ancient literary
530. criticism. After a discussion of the rhetorical period and its components, the work is arranged
531. around an eightfold taxonomy of literary styles:
532. There are four good kinds, plain (iskhnos), elevated (megaloprepês), elegant (glaphuros), and
533. forceful (deinos). Each of these, in turn, has its
534. corresponding vice: arid (xêros), frigid
535. (psukhros), affected (kakozêlos), and unpleasant
536. (akharis). For Demetrius, one might say, the vices
537. are virtues manqués. In its attention to the virtues of style, the treatise shows some common
538. interest (and possibly filiation) with the work of
539. Theophrastus; on the other hand, it goes beyond
540. what we know of the Theophrastean system in
541. its elaboration of more than one kind of good
542. style. In this respect it prefigures the work of
543. Hermogenes (q.v. below).
544. One of the "transitional figures" of the
545. postclassical period—a Greek-speaking man
546. living in Rome during the first century and writing for a primarily Roman audience—was Dio-
547. nysius of Halicarnassus. In addition to his historical work Roman Antiquities, he wrote
548. a number of rhetorical works, including On
549. Mimesis, which survives only in fragmentary
550. form; On the Ancient Orators, which treats of
551. Lysias, Isocrates, Isaeus, and Demosthenes, four
552. of the ten canonical Attic orators; various other
553. essays (some in epistolary form) on Thucydides,
554. Demosthenes, Deinarchus, and Plato; and On
555. the Arrangement of Words, which treats of stylistic matters such as composition and euphony.
556. Dionysius was a proponent of "Atticism" in
557. oratory, as opposed to what he saw as the excesses of "Asianism." Along with Demetrius and
558. Hermogenes, he is one of the great arbiters of
559. rhetorical style in postclassical Greek rhetoric.
560. Another important treatise from this period, whose author is also uncertain, is On the
561. Sublime, formerly attributed (among others)
562. to Dionysius of Halicarnassus and to Cassius
563. Longinus. The latter was a Neo-Platonist
564. philosopher of the third century C.E., and the
565. teacher of Porphyry. On the Sublime, however,
566. is now dated (on internal evidence) to the first
567. century C.E. There are significant lacunae in our
568. extant manuscripts of the treatise; what remains
569. to us is perhaps two-thirds of the whole. Unlike
570. the work of Demetrius or Hermogenes, which
571. offers synthetic overviews of various aspects
572. of rhetorical and literary style, On the Sublime
573. concentrates on one important virtue of style,
574. hupsos, or sublimity. It is traced to five
575. sources, the five having a common ground of
576. dunamis en tôi legein, power with language.
577. Two are primarily the product of nature, three
578. of nurture. In the former category are to peri tas
579. noeseis hadrepébolon, sturdiness of ideas (later
580. in the treatise defined as to megalophues,
581. inborn greatness), and to sphodron kai enthou-
582. siastikon pathos, the arousal of vehement emotion. In the latter category are included he poia
583. ton skhêmatôn plasis, the proper construction of figures; hê gennaia phrasis, nobility of
584. phrase; and, finally, hê en axiômati kai diarsei
585. sunthesis, a general effect of dignity and elevation. The fifth heading is said to encompass the
586. other four.
587. On the Sublime is an idiosyncratic and
588. very personal work, but deeply perceptive in
589. its literary judgments. Its postclassical status is
590. evident in the elevation of such authors as
591. Homer and Demosthenes to the pinnacle of literary greatness. Quite apart from the critical
592. acumen and sensitivity of the author, we are
593. indebted to this treatise for its preservation of
594. a number of fragments of earlier authors (such
595. as Sappho) that have not otherwise survived
596. the ravages of time.
597. Perhaps the principal rival to the Aristotelian system, and certainly, during the time before the rediscovery of Aristotle's treatises in
598. the first century, the most influential element
599. of Greek rhetoric, was stasis theory (Latinstatus or constitutio). Its first formulation is traditionally ascribed to Hermagoras of Temnos
600. in the second century. His own writings have
601. not survived, but some impression of them can
602. be gained from their apparent impact on
603. the Rhetorica ad Herennium and on Cicero's
604. De inventione. (Much of what can be known
605. about Hermagoras is reported in Kennedy
606. 1963:303–21.) Hermagoras' system of stasis is
607. said to have been arranged under five headings—probably including those of stokhasmos
608. (fact), horos (definition), poiotês (quality), and
609. metalêpsis (transference).
610. A Greek work on stasis theory that
611. has survived, although from a period later
612. than Cicero, is On Staseis of Hermogenes
613. of Tarsus, one of the more important figures of the Second Sophistic. Born around
614. 161 c.E., he was said to be an oratorical child
615. prodigy, so gifted that the emperor Marcus
616. Aurelius came to hear him speak. His system of stasis is much more complex than
617. the Hermagorean system seems to have been
618. (see the treatment in Kennedy 1983:79–86); it
619. depends upon an elaborate process of diairesis,
620. or division, in order to determine the question
621. at issue.
622. Aside from On Staseis, Hermogenes' literary remains include On Invention, On Ideas
623. of Style (translated in Wooten 1987) and
624. perhaps (though this may be spurious) Progym-
625. nasmata (translated in Baldwin 1928:23–38).
626. Books 1 and 2 of On Invention treat the proem
627. and the narration. Books 3 and 4 concentrate
628. on the proof, with treatments of stasis and of
629. the use of enthymeme and epicheireme; moreover, there is a treatment of style. On Ideas
630. offers a sevenfold taxonomy of virtues of style:
631. saphêneia, clarity; megethos, grandeur; hallos, beauty; gorgotês, rapidity; ethos, character; alêtheia, sincerity; and deinotês. Some
632. of these are further subdivided; deinotês, literally "awesomeness," is typically translated
633. as "force(fulness)" and (like Pseudo-Longinus's
634. fifth source of sublimity) refers to the effective use of the other six ideas. For Hermogenes
635. the incarnation of deinotês (and thus of all
636. the ideas) is the orator Demosthenes, an exact contemporary of Aristotle. A notabledifference from the Aristotelian system is the
637. inclusion here of ethos as a matter, not of invention, but of style.
638. The Progymnasmata offers thirteen graduated rhetorical exercises of various types,
639. including the use of muthos, fable; diêgêma,
640. narrative; khreia, anecdote; gnome, proverb;
641. anaskeuê and kataskeuê, refutation and confirmation; koinoi topoi, commonplaces;
642. egkômion, encomium; sugkrisis, comparison; êthopoiïa, character-portrayal; ekphrasis,
643. description; thesis, logical examination;
644. and nomou eisphora, the citation of a law
645. in argumentation. A similar system is found
646. in the Progymnasmata of Theon and of Aph-
647. thonius; the Hermogenic text was translated
648. into Latin by Priscian in the sixth century
649. c.E. Like the controuersiae and suasoriae
650. of Latin declamation, the progymnasmata were used as rhetorical exercises in the
651. schools.
