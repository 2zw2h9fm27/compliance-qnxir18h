46. Augustine's major rhetorical work,De

55. While passages on rhetoric and philosophy
56. of language are scattered through a number of
57. Augustine's works, among them, De ordine, De
58. magistro, Confessions, De catechizandis rudi-
59. hus, De Trinitate, and De dialectica, his most
60. comprehensive treatment of rhetorical principles and methods is offered inDe doctrina
61. Christiana. The treatise can be divided both
62. conceptually and biographically into two sections. The first, comprising Books One, Two,
63. and most of Three, was written in 396 or 397;
64. the second, comprising the rest of Book Three
65. and Book Four, was written in 426 or 427, near
66. the end of Augustine's life. Books One through
67. Three discuss matters concerning the discovery
68. of God's Word in Scripture. Book Four outlines
69. how an understanding of Scripture should then
70. be used to instruct, please, and persuade the
71. faithful. For the Christian whose office is to
72. profess the faith, the Bible is the first place one
73. goes to gather material for rhetorical occasions,
74. that is, for invention. This makes invention for
75. homiletic occasions radically different from
76. invention for rhetorical occasions (law court,
77. legislature, ceremonial event) in the classical,
78. civic-oriented tradition. Not only does the Bible
79. provide a set of topoi (topics) for discoursedifferent from those listed in the pagan handbooks, it also transforms the principal task of
80. invention from selecting topics based on rhetorical situations to interpreting the truth of
81. sacred texts—that is, from analytics to her-
82. meneutics. Interpretation is necessary because
83. the words of Scripture are often obscure in
84. meaning. Before the material of Scripture can be
85. used for rhetorical purposes, its meaning must
86. first be determined.
87. Augustine begins his discussion of interpretation in Book One by drawing a distinction
88. between things (res) and signs(signa). Things
89. exist prior to signs; signs exist (in human use)
90. only for "bringing forth and transferring to another mind the action of the mind in the person who makes the sign"(De doctrina 35).
91. The signs humans use may be mere fallen shadows of the divine Word, but their highest use
92. is in seeking their original source, the primary
93. substance from which all things proceed, God.
94. Thus, Book One ofDe doctrina expounds
95. some of the cardinal tenets of Catholic Christianity on the assumption that methods of
96. scriptural exegesis and homiletical expression
97. can be effective only if knowledge of God and
98. His Word is firmly established. In discovering
99. knowledge of God, an interpreter of Scripture
100. should be guided by the truth that "the end of
101. the Law and of all the sacred Scriptures is the
102. love of a Being which is to be enjoyed. . ."(De
103. doctrina 30). Scripture may often be obscure.
104. The exact nature of things to which scriptural
105. signs refer may frequently be uncertain. So
106. where there is uncertainty about the meaning
107. of a scriptural passage, the exegete should
108. advance an interpretation that builds up charity (love of God, neighbor, and oneself for
109. God's sake).
110. Having established the basis for sound
111. doctrine through knowledge of First Things
112. and the principle of charity in Book One,
113. Augustine takes up in Books Two and Three
114. specific problems in interpreting the textual
115. source of doctrine, the Bible. Book Two discusses how to go about interpreting scriptural
116. signs that might be misconstrued through ignorance of things. Augustine elaborates on
117. some of the information and tools needed for
118. overcoming ignorance of things, among them
119. knowledge of languages (for accurate translations); general knowledge of worldly things
120. like history, natural history, and the mechanical arts; accurate texts of Scripture; and so
121. forth. Book Three concerns how an exegete
122. should interpret ambiguous signs in Scripture,
123. presenting a number of strategies for overcoming ambiguity and obscurity. Despite thedifficulties that figurai language presents to an
124. interpreter, Augustine defends its presence in
125. Scripture. He asserts that all tropes found in
126. the rhetorical handbooks are also found in
127. Scripture, and thus he defends the Bible against
128. secular charges of rhetorical vulgarity, an opinion that he himself had held as a young man.
129. In Book Four Augustine takes up more
130. traditional rhetorical issues when considering
131. how a Christian should go about professing
132. the truths discovered in Scripture. He says that
133. he will not provide a handbook of rhetorical
134. rules because they can be found elsewhere.
135. Moreover, he claims that eloquence is better
136. learned through imitating eloquent speakers
137. and diligent practice than through memorizing
138. lists of rules. What he does provide is a discussion of some principles that should guide
139. Christians who profess the faith through the
140. rhetorical means of sermons, letters, and treatises. He first asserts the necessary relationship
141. between wisdom (by which he means knowledge of sacred things) and eloquence, saying
142. that while one can know and convey truth
143. without possessing eloquence, one will, if eloquent, be more effective not just at teaching
144. the faithful what they should do but also at
145. actually moving them to do what should be
146. done.
147. He proceeds to a discussion of style and
148. adapts the Ciceronian precepts for Christian
149. purposes. Cicero had linked the use of stylistic
150. registers to the relative weight of a subject being
151. considered. Small matters, like monetary disputes, should be addressed in a plain style.
152. Great matters, like issues of life and death or
153. affairs of state, should be treated in a grand
154. style. Intermediate matters should be treated in
155. a moderate style. Augustine notes that for
156. Christians all matters are equally significant
157. because they concern man's eternal welfare, so
158. one's use of different stylistic registers should be
159. linked to one's rhetorical purpose. A pastor
160. should use a plain style for instructing the faithful, a moderate style for delighting an audience
161. and making it more receptive or sympathetic to
162. sacred teachings, and a grand style for moving
163. the faithful to action. Although Augustine says
164. that a preacher's chief homiletical purpose is
165. instruction, he acknowledges that few people
166. will act based on instruction alone; most must
167. be moved to act through the psychological and
168. rhetorical means employed in the grand style.
169. At the end of Book Four, he takes up the topic
170. of ethos and, in a departure from Aristotelian
171. doctrine, links credibility more to the rhetor's
172. actual character and behavior than to a discursive persona.
173. Augustine's discussion of discovery and
174. expression in the De doctrina must be seen in
175. light of his philosophy of language and its place
176. in a larger Christian theology. His theology
177. perpetuates the assumption of many classical
178. philosophers that realities (ideas in Plato, substances in Aristotle, God in Augustine) exist
179. prior to and independent of human knowledge
180. of them. But within that philosophical tradition,
181. the capability of human language to represent
182. reality accurately is variously conceived. In
183. Aristotle, for instance, theories of language use
184. (demonstration, exploration, argumentation,
185. deception) are tied to the degree of reality being referred to. Language can accurately represent substances, and through the science of
186. demonstration, humans can discourse with certainty on them. Human affairs, existing more in
187. the realm of accident than of substance, cannot
188. be represented with certainty, and discourse
189. upon them must be handled through the art of
190. rhetoric.
191. Augustine, on the other hand, says that
192. however hard humans might try to bridge it,
193. an eternal gulf exists between the reality of
194. God and our knowledge of and means of communicating about Him. Augustine's explorations of divine ontology inDe Trinitate are
195. wholly circumscribed by his realization that
196. language cannot completely or transparently
197. represent the transcendent reality of God's
198. nature. This position is akin to Plato's views on
199. the imperfect human representation of transcendent Ideas. What distinguishes Augustine
200. and his Christian philosophy of language from
201. Plato and other secular philosophies is the
202. theological significance he attributes to tropes.
203. As St. Paul says (1 Cor. 13.12), humans can
204. know God only per speculum in aenigmate,
205. traditionally translated "through a glass, darkly." As a former professor of rhetoric, Augustine knows that an enigma is a trope, a species
206. of "obscure allegory." Since it is only indirectly, through likeness and analogy, that humans can represent knowledge of divinity,
207. tropes function as the best linguistic means of
208. conveying this knowledge. Furthermore, obscure tropes serve a psychological as well as
209. epistemological function for Augustine: "No
210. one doubts that things are perceived more
211. readily through similitudes and that what is
212. sought with difficulty is discovered with more
213. pleasure" (De doctrina 38). The work necessary to understand the obscurity offigurai
214. language (signa) raises a hunger for pursuing
215. truth (res) and fights the arrogance of pride.
216. And, as verbal ornaments, tropes arouse a
217. listener's or reader's pleasure and make one
218. more receptive to sacred teachings. In this way
219. tropes are an essential psychological means of
220. turning humans toward God.
221. The De doctrina Christiana by itself or in
222. combination with Augustine's other writings
223. does not offer a theory of rhetoric or a manual
224. of rhetorical precepts as extensive as those written by, say, Aristotle or Cicero. But his writings
225. were essential for ensuring that the teachings of
226. those and other figures in the classical tradition
227. of rhetoric were passed on to later generations
228. as the Roman empire and its educational institutions crumbled in the fifth century. Augustine
229. defends the use of secular knowledge and arts
230. as "gold and silver, which [pagans] did not
231. institute themselves but dug up from certain
232. mines of divine Providence" (De doctrina 75).
233. Without the authority of this defense, it is open
234. to question whether rhetoric and other secular
235. liberal arts would have survived the attacks of
236. Church fathers like Jerome, who saw their proponents as fallen pagans and their precepts as
237. perversions of sacred truth.
238. The specific rhetorical principles Augustine
239. advocated became a basis for many of the
240. medieval handbooks on preaching, the ars
241. praedicandi, as well as for a later homiletical
242. tradition called Sacred Rhetoric. That tradition
243. emphasized the importance of moving the will
244. to action and of the role emotion and a grand,
245. eloquent style play in achieving that end. During the Renaissance and early modern periods
246. in particular, the tradition served as a primary
247. alternative to Protestant homiletical theories
248. emphasizing instruction and plain style. In addition, there is an as-yet-unexplored connection
249. between that tradition and the rise of Romanticism during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries as an alternative to an increasingly powerful scientific empiricism. Finally, Augustine's
250. elevation of figurai obscurities to a central epistemological and psychological role served as
251. grounds for poets such as Dante, and later, the
252. Romantics and their twentieth-century heirs,
253. modernist writers and the New Critics, to privilege the status and function of literary language
254. over other forms of language use.
