## Definition & Etymology

<div className="flex items-center mb-8">
  <GitBranch className="w-6 h-6 text-emerald-600 mr-3" />
</div>

<div className="prose prose-lg max-w-none leading-relaxed bg-emerald-50 border-emerald-200 border-l-4 pl-6 py-2 pr-4 rounded-r-lg">

Term coined by Daniel Fogarty in 1959 to designate the formalist rhetoric commonly used in
American writing instruction during most of the
twentieth century. Current-traditional rhetoric is
characterized by its emphasis on the formal features of the finished product of composing. The
current-traditional essay employs a rigorous
movement from general to specific. It displays a
thesis sentence or paragraph, three or more paragraphs of supporting examples or data, and a
paragraph each of introduction and conclusion.
The movement from general to specific isrepeated within paragraphs, which feature topic
sentences—generalized statements that are developed or specified in successive sentences. Highly
prescriptive applications of current-traditional
theme logic, such as Barrett Wendell's in English
Composition (1891), prescribe that a general-tospecific relation obtain between subjects and
predicates of individual sentences as well.
Current-traditional essays also adhere to the
principles of unity and coherence. The first principle mandates that a theme refer to only one
subject of discussion. The second mandates that
each sentence in a theme demonstrate an obvious, preferably graphic, relation to those that
precede and follow it. Current-traditional rhetoric is further characterized by its reliance on the
research paper as a major composing assignment; its collapse of invention into a three-step
process of selecting, narrowing, and outlining a
subject; its division of available genres into four
modes of discourse—exposition, description,
narration, and argument; and its emphasis on the
use of correct grammar, usage, and mechanics.
Current-traditional rhetoric descends from a
British rhetorical tradition located in Lockean
empiricism but tinctured with a dose of Scottish
common-sense realism. Its progenitors were
George Campbell's Philosophy of Rhetoric (1776)
and Hugh Blair's Lectures on Rhetoric andBelles
Lettres (1783). Several American rhetoric teachers wrote reductivized redactions of these texts
during the nineteenth century. The most influential of these was Alexander Bain's EnglishComposition (1866), which introduced the notions of
the topic sentence and the methods of paragraph
development into current-traditional thought. By
the turn of the twentieth century, the paradigm
essay was firmly in place within mainstream composition textbooks, if not within writing instruction itself. Current-traditional rhetoric dominated
textbooks published throughout the first threequarters of the twentieth century, and its signature
can still be seen in the many contemporary composition programs that divide mandatory firstyear writing courses between semesters of instruction in exposition and argument or exposition and
a research paper.
The hegemony of current-traditional rhetoric
is widely thought to have been successfully challenged during the 1980s by process-oriented pedagogy. This pedagogy, however, in which teachers
assist students while they are composing, is quite
compatible with current-traditional rhetoric, as it
accepts the empiricist epistemology that undergirds its older counterpart and as the products of
process instruction often look very much like fiveparagraph themes. The current-traditional attitude toward subjects as empirical entities that exist
entirely apart from people who write about them
and its representative theory of language have
recently been challenged by composition theorists
interested in postmodern theories of authorship
and language. This challenge to the preeminence
of current-traditional rhetoric, however, has yet to
be widely felt in the day-to-day teaching or administration of courses or programs in introductory
composition.
Sharon Crowley
Pennsylvania State University

120. Davidson, Donald (b. 1918)
121. An innovative and influential analytic philosopher. Donald Davidson indirectly impacts
122. rhetoric because of his antifoundational views,
123. which break with traditional Cartesian dualism and challenge conceptual relativism. His
124. philosophy of language asserts that no conceptual schemes exist to facilitate communication;
125. nothing, not even language or any other systems of categories, mediates between the
126. human mind and the world. For many rhetoricians Davidson's antifoundationalism offers
127. an alternative to the competing foundational
128. theories that locate knowledge in texts, cognitive processes, or discourse communities.
129. Primarily through his considerable influence
130. on the work of Richard Rorty, Davidson's
131. influence has extended to rhetorical theory,
132. epecially in the writings of Reed Way Das-
133. enbrock and Thomas Kent.
134. Educated at Harvard, Davidson was originally an English major; he earned his undergraduate degree in the classics and philosophy.
135. He returned to Harvard for his graduate work
136. in philosophy, studying with Willard Van Or-
137. man Quine. Davidson has taught philosophy
138. at Stanford, the University of Chicago, and
139. Princeton. He teaches currently at the University of California at Berkeley.
140. Davidson calls his view of communicative
141. interaction externalism. Because he believes
142. that no conceptual scheme, such as a system of
143. social conventions, mediates between humans
144. and the objective world, language for Davidson becomes intersubjective and governed
145. neither by internal mental categories nor by
146. discourse communities. His conception of
147. communicative interaction, called radical interpretation, departs from the traditional belief that language is a system of rules and conventions. Instead of conceptual schemes,
148. Davidson bases his language theory ontriangulation, the passing theory, the prior theory,
149. and the principle of charity.
150. Triangulation describes the way thoughts
151. and beliefs develop through the interaction of
152. individual language users, other language
153. users, and objects in the world. As individuals
154. interact with each other and with objects in the
155. world, a baseline of communication and
156. understanding develops. In turn, this interactive and interpretive aspect of communication depends on a passing theory. Because
157. no conceptual schemes exist, humans have no
158. means to guarantee in advance of a communicative situation that communication will
159. succeed. Communication takes place, with
160. varying degrees of success, because both the
161. speaker and interpreter employ passing theories. These largely unconscious adjustments
162. occur continually as people communicate. A
163. speaker continually adjusts to what he or she
164. imagines the interpreter is making of what is
165. being said, and the interpreter makes a
166. reasoned and generally effective guess at what
167. is being said. Prior theories, or the background
168. knowledge possessed by each communicator,
169. also facilitate communication by improving
170. the chances of effective passing theories. While
171. prior theories may make communication
172. easier, they offer no predictive guarantees for
173. success. Basic to all communication is what
174. Davidson calls the principle of charity, a
175. phrase he admits is a misnomer because it does
176. not depend on any conscious charitable acts.
177. He means that people are able to communicate
178. because they share a common world and
179. because each person assumes that, up to a
180. point, other language users are rational beings.
181. The principle of charity states that because
182. people naturally want to communicate they
183. unconsciously make a best effort to understand each other.
184. Although Davidson's conception of communicative interaction focuses on spoken
185. discourse and not on writing, his philosophy
186. of language nonetheless provides composition
187. specialists with valuable insights about the
188. nature of knowledge, belief, and truth. In
189. particular, social theorists believe that Davidson provides essential arguments against
190. radical skepticism, the doubt that we can
191. know anything with certainty. While maintaining the social dimension of language, Davidson makes the critical connection between
192. the objective world and the way language is
193. acquired and used through interaction with
194. others.

212. Day, Henry Noble (1808–1898)
213. Congregational clergyman, rhetorical educator,
214. and textbook author who presented a powerful
215. but failed challenge to the development of
216. American composition-rhetoric between 1850
217. and 1885. Born in New Preston, Connecticut,

222. lived with his uncle, college president Jeremiah

224. conservative classical college curricula in the
225. nineteenth century.
226. Between 1828 and 1836, Day taught at a

236. was all postgraduate, because the theological
237. department there did not serve undergraduates.
238. It is to that fact that the complex and somewhat
239. forbidding nature of Day's early rhetorical writings may be due.
240. The theological department of Western
241. Reserve was gradually dismantled during the
242. 1850s for financial reasons, but Day remained
243. in Hudson until 1858, teaching and engaging in
244. business ventures. In 1858 he was asked toaccept the presidency of Ohio Female College at
245. College Hill near Cincinnati, which position he
246. held until 1864 when the college failed. Dayreturned to New Haven, where he lived for the
247. rest of his life, devoting his time to turning out
248. textbooks on a variety of subjects. He died on
249. January 12, 1890.
250. Day was a classic example of the kind of
251. polymath that nineteenth-century American
252. education sometimes produced. His classical
253. training at Yale was unspecialized, and he
254. chose to read in, master, and then write about
255. many diverse subjects both during and after
256. his teaching career. Between 1844 and 1889,
257. Day wrote textbooks on elocution, rhetoric, composition, grammar, logic, literature,
258. bookkeeping, aesthetics, ethics, psychology,
259. and education. Within this vast compass,
260. however, we can trace a development of Day's
261. thought, which falls generally into two
262. phases: his work on rhetoric, literature, and
263. language, which proceeded from his teaching
264. experience at Yale, Western Reserve, and
265. Ohio Female College, and his more abstract
266. later interests in ethics, aesthetics, and the
267. different forms of "mental science" that we
268. would now classify as philosophy and psychology.
269. The rhetorical tradition into which Day
270. was born was American Blairian belletrism,
271. and it was Chauncey Goodrich's elocution-
272. tinged version of this strong tradition that he
273. first learned at Yale. Then, in 1828—the year
274. of Day's commencement—the powerful argumentative challenge to Blairian rhetoric
275. presented by Whately in his Elements of Rhetoric appeared. Day must have known both of
276. these traditions of rhetoric well, in theory at
277. least, but when he attempted to teach them
278. between 1840 and 1850, he conceived a
279. "disgust" (to use one of his favorite words)
280. for both of them as a result. The result of his
281. dissatisfaction was the creation of Day's own
282. rhetorical theories, many of which are sui
283. generis and were in serious conflict with the
284. received wisdom of his age.
285. Day's most important statements on rhetoric are all found in his first textbook on the
286. subject, Elements of the Art of Rhetoric published in 1850 and in print until 1876. In the
287. preface to that book, Day says that Blairian
288. stylistic rhetoric has the result, "as might naturally be expected where manner is the chief
289. object of regard, that exercises in composition
290. have been exceedingly repulsive and profitless
291. drudgeries" (iii). But Whately's Aristotelian
292. rhetoric, which agreed with this antistylistic
293. view, constrained rhetoric from full use, said
294. Day: "Covering the field of pure discourse as
295. addressed to another mind, [rhetoric] isredeemed from the shackles and embarrassments
296. of that view which confines it to mere argumentative composition, or the art of producing Belief" (v). Day believed that rhetorical
297. instruction must concentrate on invention as
298. opposed to style, and he saw rhetoric as completely multimodal, not merely argumentative.
299. Day's rhetoric, as exemplified in Elements, Rhetorical Praxis (1861), The Art of
300. Discourse (1867), Grammatical Synthesis:
301. The Art of English Composition (1867), and
302. The Young Composer: A Guide to English
303. Grammar and Composition (1870), was both
304. theoretical and practical; his books were
305. meant to be treatises and textbooks at the
306. same time. As an author of textbooks, he had
307. some success; his texts were in print for over
308. forty years. Finally, however, Day's rhetorical
309. ideas represent a dead end, a road that American composition rhetoric did not take. Day
310. favored the ideas of Aristotle, Cicero, and
311. German philosophical writers over those of
312. Blair, Whately, and the early writers on American composition. The moderns, he stated,
313. "have too much regarded rhetoric as a merely
314. critical art. They have directed their attention
315. mainly to pruning, repressing, and guiding;
316. and have almost wholly neglected to apply
317. any stimulus to the faculty of discourse itself"
318. (10–11).
319. His emphasis on invention and content,
320. his original and complicated taxonomy of discourse types, and especially his claim that
321. rhetoric must be "a developing and invigorating art" rather than a "merely critical art"
322. made Day into a thinker who simply did not
323. fit with the increasingly pragmatic and mechanical needs of American writing instruction, especially after the Civil War. In spite
324. of (or perhaps because of) the immense originality of Day's work, his rhetoric was never
325. as popular as that of his two prewarrivals, George Quackenbos and Richard Green
326. Parker. Never a lively stylist, Day found that
327. his stiff and treatiselike writings on discourse
328. were even less popular after the Civil War, as
329. college education shifted to meet new social
330. needs.
331. With the development of a sophisticated
332. postwar composition-rhetoric in the works
333. of Alexander Bain, John Hart, Adams S.
334. Hill, and John Genung, Day's rhetoric rapidly
335. dropped in popularity. After he left his teaching positions, he spent most of his later years
336. working on books in nonrhetorical "mental
337. science" disciplines, few of which were
338. successful, and by 1932 the National Cyclopaedia of American Biography could say
339. that his books "were of value when published
340. but have long outlived their usefulness." Rhetorical scholars have more recently found
341. much to esteem in Day's emphases on invention and vitality in writing, however, and it is
342. likely that future scholarship will see him
343. as one of the more important theorists toappear in the field of rhetoric in the nineteenth
344. century.
